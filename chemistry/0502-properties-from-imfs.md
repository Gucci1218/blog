## The Core Concept

### Intuition First

Back to the pool, because that bug has a lot to teach us.

In [Intermolecular Forces](/chemistry/0501-intermolecular-forces) we learned that molecules in a liquid are constantly tugging on their neighbors — London dispersion forces on everybody, dipole-dipole attractions on polar molecules, and the strongest of the common three, hydrogen bonding, when you've got $\ce{H}$ bonded to $\ce{N}$, $\ce{O}$, or $\ce{F}$. Water is the hydrogen-bonding superstar: every $\ce{H2O}$ molecule can grip up to four neighbors in a tight little web.

Here's the one idea that unlocks the whole topic: **a liquid's properties are just its molecules' grip strength made visible.** Everything you can see, feel, or measure about a liquid is the macroscopic shadow of that microscopic tug-of-war. Let me walk the pool deck one scene at a time.

**The water strider that doesn't sink (surface tension).** Think about a molecule deep in the pool. It's surrounded on all sides by neighbors, all pulling on it equally — a molecular group hug, balanced in every direction. Now think about a molecule sitting right at the surface. It has neighbors beside it and below it, but *nothing above it*. All those unbalanced tugs pull it sideways and downward, so the surface molecules get squeezed together into a taut, slightly stretched "skin." That skin is **surface tension**, and it's strong enough to hold up a water strider's feet — or a steel paperclip you lay down gently. Stronger IMFs → tighter skin → higher surface tension.

**The shivers when you climb out (evaporative cooling).** In any liquid, molecules are jiggling around at a whole *range* of speeds — some slow, some fast, an average we call temperature. Only the fastest, most energetic molecules have enough oomph to break free of their neighbors' grip and escape into the air as vapor. But here's the catch: when the fastest movers leave, the ones **left behind are the slower, lower-energy ones** — so the average energy of what remains drops. Lower average energy means lower temperature. The liquid literally cools itself by exporting its hottest molecules. That's why the thin film of water on your skin, evaporating in the breeze, leaves you freezing on a hot day.

**The lemonade climbing the straw (capillary action).** Two attractions are competing inside that straw. **Cohesion** is water molecules attracting *each other*; **adhesion** is water molecules attracting the *straw wall*. Because water clings to the paper straw more strongly than it clings to itself, it creeps up the walls — and drags the rest of the column up with it via cohesion. That's **capillary action**, and it's the same reason water forms a curved **meniscus** in a graduated cylinder.

**The slow honey (viscosity).** Honey pours like a lazy ribbon while water splashes. **Viscosity** is a liquid's resistance to flowing — internal friction. The more strongly molecules grip each other (and the more tangled and long they are), the harder it is for them to slide past one another, and the thicker the liquid pours.

Four scenes, one cause. For the rest of this article, whenever a property changes, your first move is always the same: *what happened to the IMF strength?*

### The Master Table: One Cause, Five Effects

Here's the entire topic on one card. Notice that every property points the same direction — except vapor pressure, which is the lone rebel that goes the *opposite* way.

| Property | What it measures | Stronger IMFs → | Because... |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Boiling point** | Temp. to turn liquid → gas | **Higher** | More energy needed to rip molecules apart |
| **Melting point** | Temp. to turn solid → liquid | **Higher** | More energy needed to loosen the lattice |
| **Surface tension** | Tautness of the surface "skin" | **Higher** | Surface molecules pulled inward harder |
| **Viscosity** | Resistance to flow | **Higher** | Molecules grip and can't slide past each other |
| **Vapor pressure** | Escaping tendency into the air | **LOWER** | Harder to escape → fewer molecules in vapor |

$$\boxed{\text{Stronger IMFs} \;\Rightarrow\; \uparrow \text{BP},\; \uparrow \text{MP},\; \uparrow \text{surface tension},\; \uparrow \text{viscosity},\; \downarrow \text{vapor pressure}}$$

Burn this into memory: **four go up, vapor pressure goes down.** If you can recite this box, you can answer the majority of Topic 3.2 questions on sight.

### Boiling and Melting Points: The Direct Readout

To boil a liquid you must give its molecules enough energy to completely break free of one another and fly off as gas. To melt a solid you must give the particles enough energy to break out of their fixed lattice positions. In both cases, **stronger IMFs demand more energy, which means a higher temperature.** Boiling and melting points are the most direct thermometers of IMF strength we have.

This is exactly the ranking skill from 0501, just phrased as a temperature. Compare these four small molecules of similar size:

| Molecule | Strongest IMF present | Boiling point |
|---|---|---|
| $\ce{CH4}$ (methane) | London dispersion only | −161 °C |
| $\ce{H2S}$ | Dipole-dipole | −60 °C |
| $\ce{NH3}$ | Hydrogen bonding | −33 °C |
| $\ce{H2O}$ | Hydrogen bonding (extra strong) | +100 °C |

Read top to bottom: dispersion-only is easiest to boil (lowest BP), hydrogen bonding is hardest (highest BP). Water's absurdly high boiling point for such a tiny molecule is the single most famous consequence of hydrogen bonding — without it, Earth's oceans would be vapor.

One more axis, still pure 0501: among nonpolar molecules with only London dispersion, **bigger (more electrons, larger molar mass) means stronger dispersion means higher BP.** That's why the halogens climb $\ce{F2}$ (gas) → $\ce{Cl2}$ (gas) → $\ce{Br2}$ (liquid) → $\ce{I2}$ (solid) at room temperature — same family, more electrons, stronger dispersion, higher boiling point each step down.

### Vapor Pressure: The Rebel Property

Picture a capped bottle half full of liquid. The fastest surface molecules keep escaping into the space above (**evaporating**), and some vapor molecules keep crashing back in (**condensing**). Pretty soon those two rates balance — evaporation rate = condensation rate — and the amount of vapor sitting above the liquid holds steady. The pressure that vapor pushes with is the **vapor pressure**.

Vapor pressure is a measure of **escaping tendency**: how badly the molecules "want" to leave the liquid. And that's set entirely by how hard their neighbors are gripping them:

$$\text{Weak IMFs} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{easy escape} \;\Rightarrow\; \textbf{high vapor pressure} \qquad \text{Strong IMFs} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{hard escape} \;\Rightarrow\; \textbf{low vapor pressure}$$

This is the one property that runs **backwards** from all the others, and it trips up half of all students. Strong IMFs give you high boiling point but *low* vapor pressure. They're two sides of the same coin: a liquid whose molecules can barely escape (low vapor pressure) is naturally the same liquid that needs a lot of heat to boil (high boiling point).

A liquid with high vapor pressure — one that evaporates readily at room temperature — is called **volatile**. Acetone (the main ingredient in nail-polish remover) has weak IMFs, so it's highly volatile: spill a little on your hand and it's gone in seconds, taking heat with it (that cold flash is evaporative cooling again). Water has strong hydrogen bonding, so it's far less volatile — a spilled puddle of water lingers for an hour. Gasoline, rubbing alcohol, and acetone all announce their high vapor pressure the same way: you can smell them from across the room, because so many molecules are escaping into the air.

**Temperature raises vapor pressure.** Heat the liquid and you speed up all the molecules, so a *larger fraction* of them clear the energy bar needed to escape. Vapor pressure climbs steeply — not in a straight line, but a curve that sweeps sharply upward as temperature rises. If you plotted vapor pressure (y-axis) against temperature (x-axis) for water, you'd get a low, nearly flat line near room temperature that bends upward more and more steeply, blowing up to $760\ \text{torr}$ right at $100\ \text{°C}$. Plot acetone on the same graph and its curve sits **above and to the left** of water's everywhere — more volatile means higher vapor pressure at every temperature.

### Boiling, Redefined — and Why Altitude Matters

Here's the payoff definition. **A liquid boils when its vapor pressure rises to equal the surrounding (atmospheric) pressure.** At that moment molecules can form vapor bubbles *inside* the liquid, not just evaporate from the surface, and the whole thing roils.

$$\boxed{\text{Boiling occurs when: vapor pressure} = \text{atmospheric pressure}}$$

At sea level, atmospheric pressure is $760\ \text{torr}$ ($1\ \text{atm}$), and water's vapor pressure hits $760\ \text{torr}$ at exactly $100\ \text{°C}$ — the number you memorized in grade school is really a statement about matching pressures.

Now go up a mountain. In Denver (the "Mile-High City") atmospheric pressure is only about $630\ \text{torr}$, and at the top of a tall peak it's lower still. Water's vapor pressure now only has to climb to $630\ \text{torr}$ to match the thinner air — and it reaches *that* value at about $95\ \text{°C}$. So **water boils at a lower temperature at altitude**, which is exactly why the back of a cake-mix box has "high-altitude directions" and why boiling an egg takes longer up there (the water is cooler even though it's boiling). Flip it around: a pressure cooker *raises* the pressure above the liquid, so the vapor pressure has to climb even higher (hotter) to match — the water boils at ~120 °C and food cooks faster.

### Surface Tension and Capillary Action

**Surface tension** is the energy stored in that stretched surface skin, and it scales directly with IMF strength. Water's surface tension is famously high because of hydrogen bonding — high enough to float a paperclip, support a water strider, and pull spilled water into round beads. Stronger IMFs → higher surface tension. (One aside: **soap and detergents lower surface tension** by wedging between water molecules and interrupting their hydrogen bonding — that's how soapy water can wet and clean surfaces that plain water just beads up on.)

**Capillary action** is the tug-of-war between **cohesion** (liquid-to-itself attraction) and **adhesion** (liquid-to-container attraction):

- **Adhesion wins** (water in glass or a paper straw): the liquid climbs the walls and curves *up* at the edges, forming a **concave meniscus** (a little valley). This is the straw-climbing lemonade.
- **Cohesion wins** (mercury in glass): the liquid pulls away from the walls and bulges *up* in the middle, forming a **convex meniscus** (a little hill).

This connects straight back to lab skill and sig figs: when you read a graduated cylinder or buret, you read water at the **bottom of the concave meniscus**, at eye level, and estimate one digit past the smallest marking (see [Significant Figures](/chemistry/0102-significant-figures)). Every volume measurement you take in lab is you reading a capillary-action curve.

### Viscosity

**Viscosity** is a liquid's resistance to flow — its internal friction as layers of molecules try to slide past one another. Three things make a liquid more viscous (thicker):

1. **Stronger IMFs** — molecules that grip each other hard resist sliding. Glycerol and honey are loaded with $\ce{-OH}$ groups that hydrogen-bond in every direction, so they ooze; water, with less extensive bonding, splashes.
2. **Longer, more tangled molecules** — long chains physically snag and knot around each other like spaghetti, independent of IMF type. Motor oil and syrup are viscous largely for this reason.
3. **Lower temperature** — this one is the knob you can turn. Heat gives molecules energy to slip past their neighbors' grip, so **warming a liquid lowers its viscosity.** Warm honey pours far faster than cold honey straight from the fridge; this is why you microwave syrup.

So viscosity marches with the rest: stronger IMFs → higher viscosity, right alongside higher boiling point and surface tension.

The instant-knockout-that-lasts-an-hour is fiction; the fact that a volatile liquid readily leaves a rag and enters the air is exactly the vapor-pressure chemistry from this article.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Boiling Point from IMFs

**Problem:** Rank $\ce{CH4}$, $\ce{HCl}$, and $\ce{H2O}$ from lowest to highest boiling point, and justify.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Identify the strongest IMF in each.** $\ce{CH4}$: nonpolar → London dispersion only. $\ce{HCl}$: polar → dipole-dipole. $\ce{H2O}$: $\ce{H}$ bonded to $\ce{O}$ → hydrogen bonding.

**Step 2 — Rank IMF strength.** Dispersion (weakest) < dipole-dipole < hydrogen bonding (strongest).

**Step 3 — Translate to boiling point.** Stronger IMFs need more energy to separate → higher boiling point, in the same order.

**Answer: $\ce{CH4} < \ce{HCl} < \ce{H2O}$. Methane has only weak dispersion forces (lowest BP), $\ce{HCl}$ adds dipole-dipole, and water's hydrogen bonding requires the most energy to break, giving the highest boiling point.**

### Example 2: Dispersion and Size

**Problem:** All four halogens ($\ce{F2}$, $\ce{Cl2}$, $\ce{Br2}$, $\ce{I2}$) are nonpolar. Why do their boiling points rise steadily down the group?

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Identify the only IMF.** All nonpolar → London dispersion is the only force present.

**Step 2 — What controls dispersion strength?** The number of electrons / molecular size. More electrons → a larger, more polarizable electron cloud → stronger instantaneous dipoles → stronger dispersion.

**Step 3 — Order by size.** $\ce{F2} < \ce{Cl2} < \ce{Br2} < \ce{I2}$ in electron count, so dispersion strength rises in that order.

**Answer: Boiling point rises $\ce{F2} < \ce{Cl2} < \ce{Br2} < \ce{I2}$ because each has more electrons than the last, strengthening London dispersion — the only IMF available to nonpolar molecules.**

### Example 3: Reading a Vapor-Pressure Table

**Problem:** At 20 °C, three liquids have these vapor pressures. Rank them by IMF strength and by volatility.

| Liquid | Vapor pressure at 20 °C |
|---|---|
| Diethyl ether | 442 torr |
| Ethanol | 44 torr |
| Water | 18 torr |

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Recall the inverse rule.** High vapor pressure ⇔ weak IMFs ⇔ high volatility.

**Step 2 — Rank volatility (just follow the numbers).** Ether (442) > ethanol (44) > water (18). Ether is the most volatile, water the least.

**Step 3 — Rank IMF strength (the reverse).** Because vapor pressure is inverse to IMF strength: water (strongest) > ethanol > ether (weakest). This fits: water and ethanol both hydrogen-bond, but water bonds more extensively; ether has no $\ce{O-H}$, so no hydrogen bonding at all — only weaker dipole/dispersion forces.

**Answer: Volatility: ether > ethanol > water. IMF strength: water > ethanol > ether. Higher vapor pressure always means weaker intermolecular forces.**

### Example 4: Which Evaporates Faster?

**Problem:** You spill a drop of acetone ($\ce{(CH3)2CO}$, polar, no $\ce{O-H}$) and a drop of water ($\ce{H2O}$) on the counter. Which disappears first, and why?

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Compare IMFs.** Water hydrogen-bonds (strong). Acetone is polar but has no $\ce{H}$ on $\ce{O/N/F}$, so it can't hydrogen-bond with itself — only dipole-dipole and dispersion (weaker).

**Step 2 — Weaker IMFs → higher vapor pressure → more volatile.** Acetone's molecules escape the liquid far more easily.

**Answer: The acetone evaporates first. Its weaker intermolecular forces give it a higher vapor pressure (greater volatility), so more of its molecules escape into the air per second than water's.**

### Example 5: Evaporative Cooling (the FRQ favorite)

**Problem:** Explain, in molecular terms, why you feel cold when water evaporates off your skin.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Molecules have a range of speeds/energies.** In the water film, molecules move at many different speeds; temperature is the *average* of that energy.

**Step 2 — Only the fastest escape.** Evaporation requires enough kinetic energy to break free of neighboring molecules' attractions. Only the highest-energy molecules clear that bar and leave as vapor.

**Step 3 — The average of what remains drops.** With the fastest (highest-energy) molecules gone, the molecules **left behind** have a lower average kinetic energy. Lower average kinetic energy *is* lower temperature.

**Step 4 — Your skin supplies the heat.** The now-cooler water film pulls heat from your skin to keep evaporating, and you feel that heat loss as cold.

**Answer: Evaporation removes the highest-energy molecules from the liquid, lowering the average kinetic energy — and therefore the temperature — of the water left on your skin. That cooler film draws heat from your skin, so you feel cold.**

### Example 6: The Altitude-Boiling Problem

**Problem:** Water's vapor pressure is 760 torr at 100 °C and about 630 torr at 95 °C. Atmospheric pressure at the top of a mountain is 630 torr. At what temperature will water boil there, and why is it lower than 100 °C?

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Definition of boiling.** A liquid boils when its vapor pressure equals the surrounding atmospheric pressure.

**Step 2 — Match the numbers.** On the mountain, atmospheric pressure is 630 torr. Water's vapor pressure reaches 630 torr at 95 °C.

**Step 3 — Interpret.** Because the air pushes down with less pressure, the vapor pressure needs to climb only to 630 torr (not 760) to match it — and it hits that value at a lower temperature.

**Answer: Water boils at about 95 °C on the mountain. Lower atmospheric pressure means the vapor pressure only needs to reach a smaller value to equal it, which happens at a lower temperature.**

### Example 7: The Pressure Cooker

**Problem:** A pressure cooker seals in steam and raises the internal pressure to about 2 atm (1520 torr). Explain why food cooks faster inside it.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Boiling condition.** Water boils when its vapor pressure equals the surrounding pressure — now 1520 torr instead of 760.

**Step 2 — Higher pressure → higher boiling temperature.** The vapor pressure must climb much higher (hotter) to reach 1520 torr, so water boils at roughly 120 °C instead of 100 °C.

**Step 3 — Hotter water, faster cooking.** The liquid water is now ~20 °C hotter while still liquid, and higher temperature speeds up cooking.

**Answer: Raising the pressure raises the boiling point (~120 °C), so the water is hotter than normal boiling water — and hotter water cooks food faster.**

### Example 8: Surface Tension and the Paperclip

**Problem:** A steel paperclip is about eight times denser than water, yet you can float one on a still glass of water. Explain, and predict what happens when you add a drop of dish soap.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Why it floats.** It's not buoyancy (steel is denser than water). Surface molecules, missing neighbors above, are pulled inward and sideways into a taut skin held together by hydrogen bonding — this **surface tension** supports the paperclip's weight as long as the skin isn't punctured.

**Step 2 — What soap does.** Soap molecules insert themselves between water molecules and disrupt their hydrogen bonding, sharply lowering the surface tension.

**Step 3 — Result.** With the skin weakened, it can no longer support the clip, and it sinks.

**Answer: The paperclip rests on water's high surface tension (from hydrogen bonding), not buoyancy. Adding soap lowers the surface tension by disrupting the hydrogen bonds, so the skin weakens and the paperclip sinks.**

### Example 9: Cohesion vs. Adhesion (the Meniscus)

**Problem:** Water forms a concave (U-shaped) meniscus in a glass tube, while mercury forms a convex (dome-shaped) one. Explain both, and state where you read the water level.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Water.** Water's adhesion to glass (it hydrogen-bonds to $\ce{-OH}$ groups on the glass surface) is stronger than its cohesion to itself, so it climbs the walls → concave, U-shaped meniscus.

**Step 2 — Mercury.** Mercury's cohesion (metallic bonding between $\ce{Hg}$ atoms) far exceeds its weak adhesion to glass, so it pulls into itself and away from the walls → convex, domed meniscus.

**Step 3 — Reading the level.** For water, read the volume at the **bottom of the concave curve**, at eye level, estimating one digit past the finest graduation.

**Answer: Water is concave because adhesion to glass beats cohesion; mercury is convex because cohesion beats adhesion. Read water at the bottom of its meniscus.**

### Example 10: Viscosity Prediction

**Problem:** Glycerol, $\ce{C3H5(OH)3}$, is a syrupy liquid used in soap and lotion; it has three $\ce{-OH}$ groups per molecule. Predict whether it is more or less viscous than water, and explain.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Count hydrogen-bonding sites.** Water has two $\ce{O-H}$ bonds and two lone pairs; glycerol has **three** $\ce{-OH}$ groups per molecule, so it can form an extensive, dense network of hydrogen bonds in every direction.

**Step 2 — Add the shape factor.** Glycerol molecules are also larger and can tangle, further resisting flow.

**Step 3 — Conclude.** More hydrogen bonding plus a bigger, tangling molecule → much greater resistance to molecules sliding past one another → higher viscosity.

**Answer: Glycerol is far more viscous than water because its three $\ce{-OH}$ groups create an extensive hydrogen-bonding network (and its larger molecules tangle), both of which resist flow.**

### Example 11: Temperature and Honey

**Problem:** Honey from the fridge barely moves, but after 20 seconds in the microwave it pours easily. Which property changed, and why?

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Name the property.** Resistance to flow = **viscosity**. Warm honey is less viscous.

**Step 2 — Molecular reason.** Heating gives the molecules more kinetic energy, enough to slip past their neighbors' intermolecular grip more easily rather than being held in place.

**Step 3 — Direction check.** Raising temperature *lowers* viscosity (thins the liquid) — the same reason you warm syrup and motor oil flows better in summer.

**Answer: Its viscosity dropped. Heating gives the molecules enough energy to overcome their intermolecular attractions and slide past each other, so the honey flows more freely.**

### Example 12: Putting It All Together

**Problem:** Two liquids, A and B, are similar-sized molecules. A is nonpolar; B hydrogen-bonds. Without any numbers, predict which has the higher boiling point, higher vapor pressure, higher surface tension, and higher viscosity.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Rank IMFs.** B (hydrogen bonding) has stronger IMFs than A (dispersion only).

**Step 2 — Apply the master box.** Stronger IMFs (B) → higher boiling point, higher surface tension, higher viscosity, but **lower** vapor pressure. A, with weaker IMFs, is the reverse.

**Step 3 — Summarize.**

| Property | Higher for... |
|---|---|
| Boiling point | B |
| Surface tension | B |
| Viscosity | B |
| Vapor pressure | **A** |

**Answer: B (stronger IMFs) has the higher boiling point, surface tension, and viscosity; A (weaker IMFs) has the higher vapor pressure. Four properties track with IMF strength; vapor pressure runs opposite.**

## Common Mistakes

| The Mistake | Why It Happens | The Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "Strong IMFs mean high vapor pressure" | Assuming *all* properties rise with IMF strength | Vapor pressure is the one exception — strong IMFs mean **low** vapor pressure. Strong grip → hard to escape → little vapor |
| Saying molecules "break bonds" when a liquid boils | Confusing intermolecular forces with covalent bonds | Boiling breaks **intermolecular forces** *between* molecules; the molecules themselves (their covalent bonds) stay intact. $\ce{H2O(l) -> H2O(g)}$, not $\ce{H}$ + $\ce{O}$ |
| Explaining evaporative cooling as "the water gets rid of heat" | Vague, non-molecular language | Name the mechanism: the *fastest, highest-energy* molecules escape, lowering the *average kinetic energy* of those left → lower temperature. FRQs require this reasoning |
| "Water boils at 100 °C, period" | Memorizing the number instead of the definition | Water boils when its vapor pressure = surrounding pressure. That's 100 °C only at 1 atm; lower at altitude, higher in a pressure cooker |
| Mixing up cohesion and adhesion | The words sound alike | **Co**hesion = liquid to itself (together); **Ad**hesion = liquid to another surface (adds on). Water climbs glass because adhesion wins |
| Claiming volatility depends on molecular weight alone | Overgeneralizing "heavier = less volatile" | Volatility tracks **IMF strength**, not just mass. Ethanol is heavier than diethyl ether yet *less* volatile because it hydrogen-bonds |
| Forgetting temperature's effect on vapor pressure/viscosity | Treating properties as fixed constants | Heating **raises** vapor pressure and **lowers** viscosity — both because molecules gain kinetic energy |
| Reading a meniscus from the top | Not thinking about which curve you're looking at | For water (concave), read the **bottom** of the meniscus at eye level, one digit past the finest mark |

## AP Exam Tips

1. **Every macroscopic property must be justified with IMF strength AND a molecular reason.** "Liquid A has a higher boiling point because it has stronger intermolecular forces — specifically hydrogen bonding, which requires more energy to overcome." Naming the *type* of IMF (dispersion / dipole-dipole / hydrogen bonding) is usually required for full credit.
2. **"Explain why liquid A has a higher vapor pressure than liquid B" is a pure IMF question.** The answer is always: A has weaker intermolecular forces, so its molecules escape the liquid more easily. Then name which forces and why (e.g., "B can hydrogen-bond but A cannot").
3. **Evaporative cooling is a recurring FRQ.** Memorize the four-beat molecular story: molecules have a *range* of energies → only the *highest-energy* ones escape → the *average kinetic energy* of those remaining *drops* → temperature falls. Vague "it releases heat" answers lose the point.
4. **Watch the direction on vapor pressure.** It is the ONE property inverse to IMF strength. On multiple choice, the trap answer usually flips it. Recite "four up, vapor pressure down."
5. **Boiling = vapor pressure meets atmospheric pressure.** Any altitude, pressure-cooker, or "why does it boil at a different temperature" question is this one sentence. Lower external pressure → lower boiling point; higher external pressure → higher boiling point.
6. **Don't say boiling breaks covalent bonds.** Phase changes overcome *intermolecular* forces only. This distinction is worth easy points and is a classic trap.
7. **Connect back to polarity and structure.** These property questions almost always start from a Lewis structure or a polarity call, so the whole chain from Unit 2 (structure → polarity → IMF type → property) is fair game in a single FRQ.

Two nonpolar liquids: pentane ($\ce{C5H12}$) and decane ($\ce{C10H22}$). Which has the higher boiling point? Which is more volatile?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Both are nonpolar, so London dispersion is the only IMF. Decane is larger with more electrons → stronger dispersion → higher boiling point.

### Problem 3

At 25 °C: methanol has a vapor pressure of 127 torr, water 24 torr, and ethylene glycol 0.1 torr. Rank the three by IMF strength.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Vapor pressure is inverse to IMF strength, so reverse the vapor-pressure order.

Vapor pressure: methanol (127) > water (24) > ethylene glycol (0.1).

IMF strength (reverse): **ethylene glycol > water > methanol.** (Ethylene glycol has two $\ce{-OH}$ groups → the most hydrogen bonding → lowest vapor pressure.)

**Strongest to weakest IMFs: ethylene glycol > water > methanol.**

</details>

### Problem 4

You put one drop of rubbing alcohol (isopropanol) and one drop of vegetable oil on your arm. The alcohol feels cold and vanishes; the oil just sits there feeling warm. Explain both observations.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Isopropanol is small and only moderately hydrogen-bonds → relatively weak IMFs → high vapor pressure → volatile. It evaporates fast, and evaporation removes the highest-energy molecules, cooling what's left (and your skin) — hence the cold flash.

Vegetable oil is made of large, heavy molecules with extensive London dispersion → strong IMFs → very low vapor pressure → essentially nonvolatile. Almost nothing evaporates, so no cooling and no disappearing.

**The alcohol is volatile (weak IMFs) so it evaporates and cools your skin; the oil is nonvolatile (strong dispersion in big molecules) so it stays put.**

</details>

### Problem 5

Write a full-credit molecular explanation of why sweating cools your body.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

1. Sweat (mostly water) on your skin contains molecules moving at a range of kinetic energies.
2. Evaporation requires enough energy to escape the liquid, so only the highest-energy molecules leave as vapor.
3. Removing the fastest molecules lowers the average kinetic energy — and thus the temperature — of the sweat left behind.
4. That cooler liquid draws heat from your skin to continue evaporating, carrying heat away from your body.

**Sweating cools you because evaporation removes the highest-energy water molecules, lowering the average kinetic energy of the remaining liquid, which then absorbs heat from your skin.**

</details>

### Problem 6

Water's vapor pressure is 760 torr at 100 °C, 630 torr at 95 °C, and 526 torr at 90 °C. In a city where atmospheric pressure is 526 torr, at what temperature does water boil? Explain.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Boiling occurs when vapor pressure = atmospheric pressure = 526 torr. Water's vapor pressure equals 526 torr at 90 °C.

The lower the external pressure, the lower the vapor pressure needed to match it, and the lower the boiling temperature.

**Water boils at 90 °C there, because its vapor pressure only needs to reach 526 torr (reached at 90 °C) to equal the reduced atmospheric pressure.**

</details>

### Problem 7

Explain why a covered pot of water boils faster than an open one, but a *sealed* pressure cooker actually raises the boiling temperature.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A loose lid mainly traps heat (less evaporative heat loss), so the water reaches boiling temperature sooner — but the boiling *temperature* is still ~100 °C because pressure is unchanged.

A sealed pressure cooker traps steam and raises the internal pressure. Now the vapor pressure must climb higher to match the greater surrounding pressure, so boiling doesn't begin until a higher temperature (~120 °C).

**A lid speeds heating but doesn't change the boiling point; a sealed cooker raises the pressure, so the vapor pressure must reach a higher value — meaning a higher boiling temperature.**

</details>

### Problem 8

Which liquid has the higher surface tension: water or hexane ($\ce{C6H14}$, nonpolar)? Explain.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Surface tension rises with IMF strength. Water hydrogen-bonds (strong); hexane has only London dispersion (weaker). Stronger inward pull on surface molecules → tighter skin.

**Water has the much higher surface tension because hydrogen bonding pulls its surface molecules inward far more strongly than hexane's dispersion forces.**

</details>

### Problem 9

Explain why adding dish soap to water lets it "wet" a greasy plate that plain water just beads up on.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Plain water has high surface tension from hydrogen bonding, so it minimizes its surface area by beading up rather than spreading. Soap molecules insert between water molecules and disrupt the hydrogen-bonding network, lowering the surface tension. With a weaker skin, the water can spread out and wet (and clean) the surface instead of beading.

**Soap lowers water's surface tension by disrupting hydrogen bonding, so the water spreads and wets the plate instead of beading.**

</details>

### Problem 10

In a glass buret, water forms a concave meniscus. Explain in terms of cohesion and adhesion, and state where you take the reading.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Water's adhesion to the glass (hydrogen bonding to surface $\ce{-OH}$ groups) is stronger than its cohesion to itself, so the water climbs the walls and dips in the center → concave meniscus.

You read the volume at the **bottom** of the concave meniscus, at eye level, estimating one digit beyond the smallest graduation (a sig-figs skill).

**Adhesion (water-to-glass) beats cohesion (water-to-water), so the water climbs the walls into a concave meniscus; read the bottom of the curve at eye level.**

</details>

### Problem 11

Rank these three by expected viscosity, lowest to highest: water, gasoline (nonpolar small molecules), glycerol (three $\ce{-OH}$ groups). Explain.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Viscosity rises with IMF strength (and with molecular tangling).

- Gasoline: nonpolar, small → weak dispersion → lowest viscosity.
- Water: hydrogen-bonds → middle.
- Glycerol: three $\ce{-OH}$ groups → extensive hydrogen-bonding network plus larger, tangling molecules → highest viscosity.

**Gasoline < water < glycerol.**

</details>

### Problem 12

A student says: "When water boils, the $\ce{O-H}$ bonds break and the water turns into hydrogen and oxygen gas." Identify the error and correct it.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Boiling is a **physical** change that overcomes the *intermolecular forces* (hydrogen bonds *between* separate $\ce{H2O}$ molecules), not the covalent $\ce{O-H}$ bonds *within* a molecule. The molecules stay intact and float apart as water vapor.

The correct process is $\ce{H2O(l) -> H2O(g)}$ — still water, just gaseous. Splitting into $\ce{H2}$ and $\ce{O2}$ would be a chemical reaction requiring far more energy (electrolysis or high heat).

**Boiling breaks intermolecular forces between molecules, not the covalent bonds inside them: $\ce{H2O(l) -> H2O(g)}$. No $\ce{H2}$ or $\ce{O2}$ is produced.**

</details>

### Problem 13

Diethyl ether and 1-butanol have nearly the same molar mass (74 g/mol), but ether boils at 35 °C while butanol boils at 118 °C. Explain the large gap.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Same size means similar London dispersion, so dispersion can't explain the gap. The difference is hydrogen bonding: 1-butanol has an $\ce{-OH}$ group (H on O) and hydrogen-bonds strongly; diethyl ether has no $\ce{O-H}$, so it can't hydrogen-bond and relies only on weaker dipole-dipole and dispersion forces.

Stronger IMFs (butanol) → much more energy to separate → much higher boiling point.

**1-Butanol boils far higher because its $\ce{-OH}$ group enables hydrogen bonding, while diethyl ether cannot hydrogen-bond despite the same molar mass.**

</details>

### Problem 14

Two liquids X and Y are placed in identical open dishes at the same temperature. After one hour, dish X is nearly empty but dish Y is almost full. (a) Which liquid has the higher vapor pressure? (b) Which has stronger IMFs? (c) Which would you predict has the higher boiling point?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

(a) X evaporated much faster → X is more volatile → **X has the higher vapor pressure.**

(b) Higher vapor pressure means weaker IMFs, so **Y has the stronger IMFs.**

(c) Stronger IMFs → higher boiling point, so **Y has the higher boiling point.**

**(a) X; (b) Y; (c) Y. Fast evaporation flags weak IMFs, high vapor pressure, and a low boiling point.**

</details>

### Problem 15

Ethanol and water are mixed and left in an open container. Ethanol (vapor pressure 44 torr at 20 °C) evaporates faster than water (18 torr). Using IMF reasoning, explain why — even though both molecules hydrogen-bond.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Both hydrogen-bond, but *how much* differs. Each water molecule has two $\ce{O-H}$ bonds and two lone pairs, letting it hydrogen-bond in up to four directions — a dense network. Ethanol has only one $\ce{-OH}$ group plus a nonpolar $\ce{C2H5}$ tail, so it forms fewer hydrogen bonds per molecule and its overall IMFs are weaker.

Weaker net IMFs → higher vapor pressure (44 > 18 torr) → ethanol escapes and evaporates faster.

**Water hydrogen-bonds more extensively (more $\ce{O-H}$ groups and lone pairs per molecule) than ethanol, giving water stronger IMFs, lower vapor pressure, and slower evaporation.**

</details>

## Quick Reference

**The master rule**

$$\boxed{\text{Stronger IMFs} \;\Rightarrow\; \uparrow \text{BP},\; \uparrow \text{MP},\; \uparrow \text{surface tension},\; \uparrow \text{viscosity},\; \downarrow \text{vapor pressure}}$$

**Property cheat-sheet**

| Property | Definition | Tracks with IMFs? |
|---|---|---|
| Boiling / melting point | Temp. to change phase | Same direction (↑) |
| Surface tension | Tautness of surface skin | Same direction (↑) |
| Viscosity | Resistance to flow | Same direction (↑) |
| Vapor pressure | Escaping tendency into air | **Opposite (↓)** |
| Volatility | How readily it evaporates | Opposite (tracks vapor pressure ↑) |

**Key definitions**

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Volatile | High vapor pressure; evaporates readily (weak IMFs) |
| Boiling | Vapor pressure = surrounding atmospheric pressure |
| Evaporative cooling | Fastest molecules escape → average KE (temperature) of the rest drops |
| Cohesion | Attraction of a liquid to itself |
| Adhesion | Attraction of a liquid to another surface |
| Concave meniscus | Adhesion > cohesion (water in glass); read the bottom |
| Convex meniscus | Cohesion > adhesion (mercury in glass) |

**Temperature effects:** heating **raises** vapor pressure and **lowers** viscosity (both because molecules gain kinetic energy).

**Altitude:** lower atmospheric pressure → lower boiling point. Pressure cooker: higher pressure → higher boiling point.
