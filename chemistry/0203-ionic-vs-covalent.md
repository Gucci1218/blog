# Ionic vs Covalent
<p class="article-date">July 4, 2026</p>

*There are exactly two ways to split boba with a friend. Option one: your friend buys the drink outright, and you hand over three dollars in cash — a complete transfer. The money is *gone* from your pocket and fully in theirs, and what holds the deal together afterward is the balance of it: they owe you loyalty, you expect a sip. Option two: you buy one drink and ask for two straws. Nobody transfers anything — you share, both of you attached to the same cup at the same time. Both arrangements hold a friendship together, but they behave completely differently when stressed. Drop the "transfer" deal into a big group of friends and the cash gets redistributed instantly; the "two straws" deal survives anything because you're physically attached to the same cup. Atoms make the exact same choice with their valence electrons: transfer them completely (ionic bonding) or share them (covalent bonding), and that one decision predicts almost every property of the substance — whether it melts at 800°C or boils away at room temperature, whether it conducts electricity in water, whether it shatters or bends. This article is the fork in the road for all of AP Chemistry: naming, formulas, intermolecular forces, and half the "explain in terms of structure and bonding" FRQs live downstream of it.*

---

## What You'll Learn
- Explain *why* atoms bond at all, using valence electrons and the octet (noble-gas stability) idea
- Describe ionic bonding as electron **transfer** between a metal and a nonmetal, forming cations and anions held together by electrostatic attraction
- Explain why ionic compounds form **lattices** described by formula units, not molecules
- Describe covalent bonding as electron **sharing** between nonmetals, including single, double, and triple bonds
- Distinguish molecular formulas from empirical formulas
- Use electronegativity difference ($\Delta EN$) as a ruler to place a bond on the ionic–polar covalent–nonpolar covalent spectrum
- Predict bond type directly from positions on the periodic table (and spot compounds like $\ce{NH4Cl}$ that contain both bond types)
- Write correct ionic formulas from ion charges using the criss-cross method
- Predict and explain physical properties (melting point, conductivity, brittleness, solubility) from the bonding model

## The Core Concept

### Intuition First

Let's push the boba deal further, because the analogy is doing real structural work here.

**The transfer deal (ionic).** When you hand your friend three dollars for the drink, two things happen: you become "three dollars poorer" and they become "three dollars richer." In atom language: sodium hands its one loose valence electron to chlorine. Sodium becomes $\ce{Na+}$ (it *lost* a negative electron, so it's now positive — a **cation**), and chlorine becomes $\ce{Cl-}$ (it *gained* an electron — an **anion**). Nothing physical ties them together afterward. What holds them is pure attraction: positive pulls on negative, the way an IOU pulls two friends back together. That attraction is the **ionic bond** — an electrostatic force, not a shared object.

And here's the part the analogy nails: an IOU isn't exclusive. A $\ce{Na+}$ ion doesn't attract *one* $\ce{Cl-}$; it attracts **every** negative ion near it, in all directions. So ionic compounds don't form little pairs — they form giant 3D grids called **lattices**, every cation surrounded by anions and vice versa, repeating for billions of ions. There is no "one $\ce{NaCl}$ molecule," just like there's no single friendship in a whole group chat that runs on shared money.

**The two-straws deal (covalent).** When you and your friend each put a straw in the same brown sugar boba, the drink belongs to both of you *simultaneously*. Neither of you gave anything up. In atom language: two hydrogen atoms each contribute one electron to a **shared pair**, and both nuclei are attracted to that same shared pair at the same time. That mutual tug-of-war on the same electrons is the **covalent bond**. And crucially, it *is* exclusive: those two atoms are physically attached to each other, forming a discrete unit — a **molecule** — with a definite membership list ($\ce{H2O}$ is exactly 2 H's and 1 O, no more, no less).

**Unequal sharing (polar covalent).** Real life, though: nobody shares perfectly. If your friend hogs the straw and drinks 70% of the boba, you're still sharing — but unequally. Atoms do this constantly. When hydrogen shares with oxygen, oxygen (more electronegative) hogs the electron pair, picking up a partial negative charge ($\delta-$) while hydrogen goes slightly positive ($\delta+$). That's a **polar covalent** bond — the huge middle ground between perfect sharing and total transfer.

Keep this picture. Every property difference in this article — why salt melts at 801°C while candle wax melts in your hand, why salt water conducts electricity — traces straight back to *transfer vs. share*.

### Why Atoms Bond at All: The Octet Idea

Atoms bond for one reason: **to lower their energy**. The most stable electron arrangements around are the noble gases (Group 18) — full outer shells, chemically smug, bonding with almost nothing. For most main-group atoms, "full outer shell" means **8 valence electrons** (2 for the tiny atoms near helium). This is the **octet rule**:

> Atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons until they have the electron arrangement of the nearest noble gas.

Two different strategies get you there:

| Strategy | Who uses it | Result |
|---|---|---|
| **Lose** electrons (few to dump) | Metals (1–3 valence e⁻) | Cation, e.g., $\ce{Na -> Na+ + e-}$ |
| **Gain** electrons (almost full) | Nonmetals (5–7 valence e⁻) | Anion, e.g., $\ce{Cl + e- -> Cl-}$ |
| **Share** electrons | Two nonmetals (both want to gain) | Covalent bond |

*Honest AP-level caveat:* the octet rule is a very useful pattern, not a law of physics. The deeper reason is energy — Coulombic attraction between nuclei and electrons. The octet rule is the shortcut; keep the energy idea in your back pocket for FRQs.

### Ionic Bonding: The Complete Transfer

**Recipe:** a metal (electron donor) + a nonmetal (electron acceptor).

Watch sodium meet chlorine, Lewis-dot style. Sodium has 1 valence electron; chlorine has 7:

$$\ce{Na}\cdot \quad + \quad :\!\!\underset{\displaystyle \cdot\cdot}{\overset{\displaystyle \cdot\cdot}{\ce{Cl}}}\!\!\cdot \quad \longrightarrow \quad \ce{Na+} \quad + \quad \left[:\!\!\underset{\displaystyle \cdot\cdot}{\overset{\displaystyle \cdot\cdot}{\ce{Cl}}}\!\!:\right]^{-}$$

Sodium's single dot jumps to chlorine. Now:
- $\ce{Na+}$ has the electron arrangement of neon (its nearest noble gas) — stable
- $\ce{Cl-}$ has the electron arrangement of argon — stable
- Opposite charges attract: that attraction **is** the ionic bond

$$\ce{Na + Cl -> NaCl} \quad \text{(really } \ce{Na+} \text{ and } \ce{Cl-} \text{ in a lattice)}$$

**The lattice, and why "formula unit" matters.** Because electrostatic attraction points in every direction, each $\ce{Na+}$ in solid salt is surrounded by six $\ce{Cl-}$ neighbors, each of *those* by six $\ce{Na+}$, on and on. There is no discrete $\ce{NaCl}$ particle. The formula $\ce{NaCl}$ is a **formula unit** — the simplest whole-number *ratio* of ions (1:1), like saying the group chat runs on "one dollar owed per favor." Writing "a molecule of $\ce{NaCl}$" on the AP exam is a genuine error.

**Charges come from the periodic table map:**

| Group | Valence e⁻ | Typical ion |
|---|---|---|
| 1 (alkali metals) | 1 | $\ce{M+}$ (e.g., $\ce{Na+}$, $\ce{K+}$) |
| 2 (alkaline earth) | 2 | $\ce{M^2+}$ (e.g., $\ce{Mg^2+}$, $\ce{Ca^2+}$) |
| 13 | 3 | $\ce{M^3+}$ (e.g., $\ce{Al^3+}$) |
| 15 | 5 | $\ce{X^3-}$ (e.g., $\ce{N^3-}$) |
| 16 | 6 | $\ce{X^2-}$ (e.g., $\ce{O^2-}$, $\ce{S^2-}$) |
| 17 (halogens) | 7 | $\ce{X-}$ (e.g., $\ce{Cl-}$, $\ce{Br-}$) |

### Covalent Bonding: The Shared Straws

**Recipe:** nonmetal + nonmetal. Both atoms want to *gain* electrons; neither will surrender any. So they compromise: share.

One shared pair = one **single bond**. Hydrogen is the simplest case — each H brings one electron, and the shared pair gives *both* atoms a full shell (2 electrons, helium-style):

$$\ce{H}\cdot + \cdot\ce{H} \longrightarrow \ce{H\bond{-}H} \quad (\ce{H2})$$

Atoms can share more than one pair:

| Bond | Shared pairs | Example | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single | 1 pair (2 e⁻) | $\ce{H2}$, $\ce{Cl2}$, $\ce{CH4}$ | Longest, weakest of the three |
| Double | 2 pairs (4 e⁻) | $\ce{O2}$, $\ce{CO2}$ | Shorter, stronger |
| Triple | 3 pairs (6 e⁻) | $\ce{N2}$, $\ce{CO}$ | Shortest, strongest |

That triple bond is why $\ce{N2}$ — 78% of every breath you take — is so unreactive: prying apart three shared pairs costs an enormous amount of energy.

**Molecules and two kinds of formula.** Covalent bonding builds discrete **molecules**, so a covalent compound has an exact headcount:

- **Molecular formula** — the *actual* number of atoms in one molecule: glucose is $\ce{C6H12O6}$.
- **Empirical formula** — the simplest whole-number *ratio*: for glucose, $\ce{CH2O}$.

Ionic compounds only ever get the ratio version (the formula unit *is* an empirical formula), because a lattice has no headcount. Covalent compounds get both, and they can differ — hydrogen peroxide is $\ce{H2O2}$ (molecular) but $\ce{HO}$ (empirical).

### Electronegativity: The Deciding Ruler

**Electronegativity (EN)** is an atom's pull on shared electrons — its straw-hogging ability. From the [Periodic Table Map](/chemistry/0202-periodic-table-map): EN increases up and to the right, peaking at fluorine (4.0). To classify a bond, subtract the two EN values:

$$\Delta EN = |EN_1 - EN_2|$$

| $\Delta EN$ | Bond type | Sharing situation | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| $> 1.7$ | Ionic (mostly) | Transfer — one atom takes the electrons | $\ce{NaCl}$: $|3.0 - 0.9| = 2.1$ |
| $0.4$–$1.7$ | Polar covalent | Unequal sharing — one atom hogs the straw | $\ce{HCl}$: $|3.0 - 2.1| = 0.9$ |
| $< 0.4$ | Nonpolar covalent | (Nearly) equal sharing | $\ce{Cl2}$: $0$; $\ce{C-H}$: $0.4$ |

**Big honesty flag: this is a spectrum, not a wall.** The cutoffs 1.7 and 0.4 are rough conventions — real bonds slide continuously from "perfectly shared" to "essentially transferred," and there's no magic switch at 1.69 vs 1.71. Real chemists (and good AP answers) treat bonds as having *ionic character* and *covalent character* in degrees. The metal/nonmetal shortcut and the $\Delta EN$ ruler agree most of the time; when they disagree, mention both and reason about it.

In a polar covalent bond, the hog gets a partial negative charge and the pushover goes partially positive:

$$\overset{\delta+}{\ce{H}}\bond{-}\overset{\delta-}{\ce{Cl}}$$

Those $\delta$ (delta) symbols mean *partial* charge — less than a full $+1$ or $-1$, because the electrons shifted but never fully left.

### Metallic Bonding: The Third Deal (Briefly)

Metals bonded to *other metals* use a third arrangement: every atom throws its valence electrons into a communal pool — a **"sea of electrons"** — leaving a lattice of cations swimming in delocalized negative charge. Think of a whole friend group throwing cash into one shared snack fund that anyone can dip into. This mobile electron sea explains metals in one shot: they **conduct** (electrons free to flow), they're **malleable** (cations can slide past each other without shattering, since the sea just re-flows around them), and they're **shiny** (mobile electrons interact with light). One section is all this needs for now — it returns in AP Unit 2.

### Properties: Where the Bonding Model Pays Off

This table is the heart of "explain in terms of structure and bonding" questions. Don't memorize the *what* without the *why* — the why is where the points are.

| Property | Ionic (e.g., $\ce{NaCl}$) | Covalent molecular (e.g., $\ce{CH4}$, sugar) | WHY |
|---|---|---|---|
| Melting point | High (801°C for $\ce{NaCl}$) | Low (often liquid/gas at room temp) | Melting ionic solids means overcoming strong cation–anion attractions throughout the whole lattice; melting molecular solids only breaks weak *between-molecule* attractions — the strong covalent bonds inside each molecule stay intact |
| Conductivity (solid) | No | No | Ionic: ions locked in the lattice, charges can't move. Covalent: no charged particles at all |
| Conductivity (molten) | **Yes** | No | Melting frees the ions to move and carry charge |
| Conductivity (aqueous) | **Yes** (if soluble) | No (mostly) | Water pries the lattice apart into mobile ions: $\ce{NaCl(s) -> Na+(aq) + Cl-(aq)}$. Sugar dissolves as whole neutral molecules — nothing charged to carry current |
| Hardness/brittleness | Hard but **brittle** | Often soft | Hit an ionic crystal and one layer shifts: suddenly $+$ sits next to $+$ and $-$ next to $-$ — like-charge repulsion cracks the crystal along a clean plane |
| Solubility tendency | Often soluble in water | "Like dissolves like": polar dissolves in water, nonpolar (oils, wax) doesn't | Polar water molecules can surround and stabilize ions and other polar particles; they have nothing to grab on a nonpolar molecule |

The conductivity fingerprint is the classic lab test: **doesn't conduct as a solid but conducts when molten or dissolved = ionic.** Never conducts = molecular covalent. Conducts as a solid = metallic.

### Writing Ionic Formulas: The Criss-Cross Method

Ionic compounds must be electrically **neutral**: total $+$ charge = total $-$ charge. The shortcut is the **criss-cross**: each ion's charge number becomes the *other* ion's subscript, then reduce to lowest terms.

$$\ce{Al^3+} \; + \; \ce{O^2-} \;\; \xrightarrow{\text{criss-cross}} \;\; \ce{Al2O3}$$

The 3 from aluminum drops down as oxygen's subscript; the 2 from oxide becomes aluminum's subscript. Check: $2(+3) + 3(-2) = 0$. Neutral. ✓

**Always reduce:** $\ce{Mg^2+}$ + $\ce{O^2-}$ criss-crosses to "$\ce{Mg2O2}$" — reduce to $\ce{MgO}$.

**Teaser:** some ions are whole covalent *groups* carrying a charge, like ammonium ($\ce{NH4+}$) and sulfate ($\ce{SO4^2-}$). The criss-cross still works — you just wrap the group in parentheses, as in $\ce{(NH4)2SO4}$. Full story in [Polyatomic Ions](/chemistry/0205-polyatomic-ions).

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Classify $\ce{KBr}$

**Problem:** Is $\ce{KBr}$ ionic, covalent, or metallic? Justify with the periodic table and with electronegativity.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Locate the elements. K is in Group 1 — an alkali **metal**. Br is in Group 17 — a **nonmetal** halogen.

**Step 2:** Metal + nonmetal → electron transfer. K loses its 1 valence electron ($\ce{K+}$, argon arrangement); Br gains it ($\ce{Br-}$, krypton arrangement).

**Step 3:** Confirm with the ruler: $\Delta EN = |2.8 - 0.8| = 2.0 > 1.7$. Ionic-range, agreeing with the shortcut.

**Answer:** **Ionic** — $\ce{K+}$ and $\ce{Br-}$ ions in a lattice, held by electrostatic attraction.

### Example 2: Classify $\ce{CO2}$

**Problem:** Classify the bonding in $\ce{CO2}$ and describe the bonds.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** C and O are both **nonmetals** → sharing, i.e., covalent.

**Step 2:** Check the ruler: $\Delta EN = |3.5 - 2.5| = 1.0$, in the 0.4–1.7 range → each $\ce{C=O}$ bond is **polar covalent** (oxygen hogs the shared pairs).

**Step 3:** Count the sharing. Carbon needs 4 more electrons, each oxygen needs 2 more; carbon forms a **double bond** to each oxygen: $\ce{O=C=O}$. Discrete molecules — $\ce{CO2}$ is a molecular formula, an actual headcount of 1 C and 2 O per molecule.

**Answer:** **Covalent (molecular)** — two polar covalent double bonds.

### Example 3: Classify $\ce{Fe}$

**Problem:** What type of bonding holds a piece of solid iron together?

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Iron is a single element, and it's a metal. Metal atoms bonded to metal atoms → **metallic bonding**.

**Step 2:** Model: $\ce{Fe}$ cations in a lattice, valence electrons delocalized into a shared "sea."

**Step 3:** Sanity check with properties: iron conducts electricity as a solid and can be hammered into shape without shattering — both signatures of the mobile electron sea.

**Answer:** **Metallic** — iron cations in a sea of delocalized electrons.

### Example 4: Classify $\ce{CaO}$ and Show the Transfer

**Problem:** Classify $\ce{CaO}$ and show the electron transfer with ion notation.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Ca = Group 2 metal (2 valence e⁻); O = Group 16 nonmetal (6 valence e⁻). Metal + nonmetal → ionic.

**Step 2:** Transfer both of calcium's valence electrons to oxygen:
$$\ce{Ca -> Ca^2+ + 2e-} \qquad \ce{O + 2e- -> O^2-}$$

**Step 3:** Both ions now have noble-gas arrangements (argon and neon respectively). Charges balance 1:1, so the formula unit is $\ce{CaO}$.

**Answer:** **Ionic** — $\ce{Ca^2+}$ and $\ce{O^2-}$ in a lattice. (Note: the $2+/2-$ charges make this lattice grip *harder* than $\ce{NaCl}$'s $1+/1-$ — more Coulombic attraction, higher melting point. That's AP-gold reasoning.)

### Example 5: Classify $\ce{CH4}$

**Problem:** Classify the bonding in methane, $\ce{CH4}$, and decide whether the bonds are polar.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** C and H are both nonmetals → covalent. Carbon shares one pair with each of four hydrogens: four single bonds, and everyone hits a full shell (octet for C, duet for each H).

**Step 2:** Ruler: $\Delta EN = |2.5 - 2.1| = 0.4$ — right at the bottom edge, so $\ce{C-H}$ bonds are treated as essentially **nonpolar**.

**Step 3:** Consequence: methane molecules barely attract each other, which is why $\ce{CH4}$ is a gas down to $-162°C$.

**Answer:** **Covalent (molecular)**, with effectively nonpolar $\ce{C-H}$ single bonds.

### Example 6: The Gotcha — $\ce{NH4Cl}$

**Problem:** Classify the bonding in ammonium chloride, $\ce{NH4Cl}$.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** First instinct check: N, H, and Cl are all nonmetals — so covalent, right? Not so fast.

**Step 2:** $\ce{NH4Cl}$ is built from **ions**: the ammonium cation $\ce{NH4+}$ and the chloride anion $\ce{Cl-}$. The attraction *between* $\ce{NH4+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$ is ionic — a lattice of cations and anions, exactly like $\ce{NaCl}$.

**Step 3:** But look *inside* the ammonium ion: the four $\ce{N-H}$ bonds are shared pairs — **covalent** bonds within the polyatomic ion.

**Answer:** **Both.** Covalent bonds *inside* $\ce{NH4+}$; ionic bonding *between* $\ce{NH4+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$. Any compound containing a polyatomic ion pulls this double duty — and "all nonmetals, yet ionic" is a favorite exam trap.

### Example 7: Electronegativity Ruler on Three Bonds

**Problem:** Using EN values (F = 4.0, H = 2.1, C = 2.5, Cs = 0.7), classify the bonds $\ce{H-F}$, $\ce{C-C}$, and $\ce{Cs-F}$.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** $\ce{H-F}$: $\Delta EN = |4.0 - 2.1| = 1.9$. Above 1.7 by the raw numbers — but HF is two nonmetals sharing electrons, and it genuinely exists as discrete molecules. Best description: **very polar covalent** with substantial ionic character. (This is exactly why the cutoff is a spectrum, not a wall.)

**Step 2:** $\ce{C-C}$: $\Delta EN = 0$. Perfectly equal sharing → **nonpolar covalent**.

**Step 3:** $\ce{Cs-F}$: $\Delta EN = |4.0 - 0.7| = 3.3$, the most extreme pairing on the table, and metal + nonmetal. **Ionic**, about as ionic as bonds get.

**Answer:** $\ce{H-F}$: polar covalent (high ionic character); $\ce{C-C}$: nonpolar covalent; $\ce{Cs-F}$: ionic.

### Example 8: Criss-Cross a Formula — Aluminum + Sulfur

**Problem:** Write the formula unit for the compound of aluminum and sulfur.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Get the ions from group numbers: Al is Group 13 → $\ce{Al^3+}$; S is Group 16 → $\ce{S^2-}$.

**Step 2:** Criss-cross the charge numbers into subscripts: 3 drops to sulfur, 2 drops to aluminum → $\ce{Al2S3}$.

**Step 3:** Verify neutrality: $2(+3) + 3(-2) = +6 - 6 = 0$. ✓ Already in lowest terms.

**Answer:** $\ce{Al2S3}$

### Example 9: Criss-Cross with a Reduction Trap

**Problem:** Write the formula unit for calcium sulfide.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Ions: $\ce{Ca^2+}$ (Group 2) and $\ce{S^2-}$ (Group 16).

**Step 2:** Naive criss-cross gives "$\ce{Ca2S2}$."

**Step 3:** Formula units are simplest ratios — reduce 2:2 to 1:1. Check: $(+2) + (-2) = 0$. ✓

**Answer:** $\ce{CaS}$ — never leave a formula unit unreduced.

### Example 10: Molecular vs Empirical Formula

**Problem:** Benzene has 6 carbon atoms and 6 hydrogen atoms per molecule. Give its molecular and empirical formulas, and explain why $\ce{NaCl}$ only gets one kind of formula.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Molecular formula = actual headcount: $\ce{C6H6}$.

**Step 2:** Empirical formula = simplest ratio: divide both by 6 → $\ce{CH}$.

**Step 3:** $\ce{NaCl}$ is a lattice — there is no discrete molecule to count atoms in, so the only meaningful formula is the ion *ratio* (1:1). Its formula unit $\ce{NaCl}$ is inherently empirical.

**Answer:** Benzene: molecular $\ce{C6H6}$, empirical $\ce{CH}$. Ionic compounds have only formula units (ratios).

### Example 11: Explain a Property from the Model

**Problem:** Solid $\ce{NaCl}$ does not conduct electricity, but molten $\ce{NaCl}$ does. Explain in terms of structure and bonding (classic FRQ phrasing).

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Identify the charge carriers. In $\ce{NaCl}$ they are the ions $\ce{Na+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$ — there are no mobile electrons.

**Step 2:** Solid: the ions are locked into fixed positions in the crystal lattice by strong electrostatic attractions. Charged particles exist but **cannot move**, so no current flows.

**Step 3:** Molten: melting breaks the lattice; the ions are now **free to move** and migrate toward the electrodes, carrying charge.

**Answer:** Conduction requires *mobile* charged particles. $\ce{NaCl}$ always has ions, but they're mobile only when molten (or dissolved) — hence conductivity appears only then. Note the two-part structure of a full-credit answer: name the particles, then address their mobility.

### Example 12: Why Triple Beats Single

**Problem:** $\ce{N2}$ is famously unreactive while $\ce{F2}$ reacts with almost everything. Use bond multiplicity to explain nitrogen's laziness.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Each N has 5 valence electrons and needs 3 more → the two atoms share **three pairs**: a triple bond, $\ce{N#N}$.

**Step 2:** Each F needs only 1 more electron → $\ce{F2}$ shares **one pair**: a single bond.

**Step 3:** More shared pairs = shorter, stronger bond. Breaking $\ce{N#N}$ costs about 945 kJ/mol versus roughly 160 kJ/mol for $\ce{F-F}$ — any reaction of $\ce{N2}$ has to pay that enormous entry fee first.

**Answer:** The triple bond in $\ce{N2}$ (3 shared pairs) is far stronger than the single bond in $\ce{F2}$, so $\ce{N2}$ is kinetically lazy — which is why the nitrogen in every breath just rides along unreacted.

## Common Mistakes

| The Mistake | Why It Happens | How to Avoid It |
|---|---|---|
| Saying "a molecule of $\ce{NaCl}$" | Molecules feel like the default particle | Ionic = lattice = **formula unit**. Reserve "molecule" for covalent substances |
| Treating 1.7 as a hard wall ("$\Delta EN = 1.8$, therefore 100% ionic") | The cutoff table looks absolute | Bond character is a **spectrum**; use metal/nonmetal + $\Delta EN$ together and hedge appropriately ($\ce{HF}$ is the classic edge case) |
| Classifying $\ce{NH4Cl}$ (or any polyatomic-ion compound) as purely covalent because "no metals" | The metal/nonmetal shortcut applied blindly | Check for **polyatomic ions** first — they mean ionic *between* ions, covalent *within* them |
| Forgetting to reduce after criss-crossing ("$\ce{Mg2O2}$", "$\ce{Ca2S2}$") | The criss-cross is mechanical | Always finish with a reduce-and-verify step: subscripts in lowest terms, total charge = 0 |
| "Ionic bonds break when salt melts/dissolves, covalent bonds break when sugar melts" | Mixing up which attractions are overcome | Melting molecular solids breaks weak attractions **between** molecules; the covalent bonds inside each molecule survive |
| Saying ionic solids conduct electricity | "Ionic = charged = conducts" feels right | Charges must be **mobile**. Solid: no. Molten or aqueous: yes |
| Writing subscripts from the number of atoms you "feel like" instead of balancing charge | Skipping the neutrality check | Total positive charge must equal total negative charge — always verify |
| Confusing polar covalent partial charges ($\delta+$/$\delta-$) with full ionic charges ($+1$/$-1$) | Same symbols territory | $\delta$ means the electrons *shifted*; integers mean they *left*. Unequal sharing ≠ transfer |

## AP Exam Tips

1. **"Explain in terms of structure and bonding" FRQs live off this article.** The scoring pattern is almost always two-part: (a) identify the particles and forces present, (b) connect them to the observed property. "NaCl has a high melting point because it's ionic" scores nothing; "melting requires overcoming the strong electrostatic attractions between $\ce{Na+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$ ions throughout the lattice" scores.

2. **Coulomb's law language preview.** The AP exam loves comparisons like "$\ce{MgO}$ vs $\ce{NaCl}$ melting points." The winning vocabulary: attraction increases with **greater charge** ($2+/2-$ beats $1+/1-$) and **smaller distance** between ions (smaller ions sit closer). Formally, $F \propto \dfrac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$ — charge and distance are the only two levers, and graders look for both words.

3. **The conductivity fingerprint is a free multiple-choice point.** Solid no / molten yes / aqueous yes → ionic. Never → molecular covalent. Solid yes → metallic. Know it cold.

4. **Watch for polyatomic-ion traps.** A compound of all nonmetals that behaves ionic (high MP, conducts when dissolved) contains a polyatomic ion — $\ce{NH4Cl}$, $\ce{NaNO3}$-style. Saying "both ionic and covalent bonding present" is often exactly the expected answer.

5. **Precision with "bond breaking."** If a question involves melting or dissolving a *molecular* substance, do NOT say covalent bonds break. That single sentence has sunk many FRQ points.

6. **You get a periodic table on the whole exam** but not an electronegativity table — so lean on position (metal/nonmetal, trends: EN rises toward F) rather than memorized EN numbers. Rough anchor values worth knowing: F = 4.0, O = 3.5, H = 2.1.

7. **Full sentences, cause-and-effect chains.** FRQ graders reward "because X, therefore Y" reasoning: *transfer → ions → lattice → strong attractions → high melting point.* Practice writing that chain until it's automatic.

## Practice Problems

### Problem 1
Classify the bonding in each: (a) $\ce{MgCl2}$, (b) $\ce{SO2}$, (c) $\ce{Cu}$, (d) $\ce{P4}$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

(a) Mg = Group 2 metal, Cl = nonmetal → **ionic** ($\ce{Mg^2+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$ in a lattice).

(b) S and O are both nonmetals → **covalent (molecular)**, polar covalent bonds ($\Delta EN = |3.5 - 2.5| = 1.0$).

(c) Copper is a metal by itself → **metallic** (cations in a sea of delocalized electrons).

(d) Phosphorus bonded to itself, a nonmetal → **nonpolar covalent** molecules ($\Delta EN = 0$).

**Answers: (a) ionic, (b) polar covalent/molecular, (c) metallic, (d) nonpolar covalent.**

</details>

### Problem 2
Show, with ion notation, the electron transfer that occurs when magnesium reacts with fluorine, and give the resulting formula unit.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Mg (Group 2) loses 2 electrons: $\ce{Mg -> Mg^2+ + 2e-}$ (neon arrangement).

Each F (Group 17) gains 1 electron: $\ce{F + e- -> F-}$ (neon arrangement) — so **two** fluorine atoms are needed to accept magnesium's two electrons.

Balance charge: one $2+$ needs two $1-$. Criss-cross confirms: $\ce{MgF2}$. Check: $(+2) + 2(-1) = 0$. ✓

**Answer: $\ce{Mg^2+} + 2\ce{F-}$ → formula unit $\ce{MgF2}$.**

</details>

### Problem 3
Using the criss-cross method, write formula units for: (a) $\ce{K+}$ and $\ce{O^2-}$, (b) $\ce{Al^3+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$, (c) $\ce{Ba^2+}$ and $\ce{O^2-}$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

(a) Criss-cross: K gets subscript 2, O gets subscript 1 → $\ce{K2O}$. Check: $2(+1) + (-2) = 0$. ✓

(b) Al gets subscript 1, Cl gets subscript 3 → $\ce{AlCl3}$. Check: $(+3) + 3(-1) = 0$. ✓

(c) Criss-cross gives "$\ce{Ba2O2}$" — **reduce** to $\ce{BaO}$. Check: $(+2) + (-2) = 0$. ✓

**Answers: (a) $\ce{K2O}$, (b) $\ce{AlCl3}$, (c) $\ce{BaO}$.**

</details>

### Problem 4
Given electronegativities O = 3.5, H = 2.1, N = 3.0, K = 0.8, Br = 2.8, classify these bonds on the spectrum: (a) $\ce{O-H}$, (b) $\ce{N-H}$, (c) $\ce{K-Br}$, (d) $\ce{Br-Br}$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

(a) $\Delta EN = |3.5 - 2.1| = 1.4$ → **polar covalent** (0.4–1.7 range); oxygen hogs the pair.

(b) $\Delta EN = |3.0 - 2.1| = 0.9$ → **polar covalent**.

(c) $\Delta EN = |2.8 - 0.8| = 2.0 > 1.7$, and metal + nonmetal → **ionic**.

(d) $\Delta EN = 0$ → **nonpolar covalent** (perfectly equal sharing).

**Answers: (a) polar covalent, (b) polar covalent, (c) ionic, (d) nonpolar covalent.**

</details>

### Problem 5
Explain why atoms bond at all. Your answer should mention valence electrons, energy, and the octet idea — and say which one is the deeper reason.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Atoms bond because the bonded arrangement is **lower in energy** than the separated atoms — that's the deep reason. The octet rule is the useful pattern sitting on top of it: main-group atoms tend to gain, lose, or share **valence electrons** until they match the nearest noble gas (8 outer electrons, or 2 for H/He), because those filled-shell arrangements are especially stable.

Metals (few valence electrons) reach an octet by losing; nonmetals (many) by gaining or sharing.

**Answer: bonding lowers energy; the octet rule is the practical shortcut describing how atoms rearrange valence electrons to get there.**

</details>

### Problem 6
A white solid melts at 776°C, doesn't conduct as a solid, but conducts well when dissolved in water. A second solid melts at 80°C, and neither the solid nor its aqueous solution conducts. Classify each and justify.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

**Solid 1:** high melting point + conducts only when dissolved = **ionic**. Ions are held rigidly in a lattice as a solid (no mobility, no conduction); water separates the lattice into free-moving ions that carry current. The high melting point reflects strong electrostatic attractions throughout the lattice.

**Solid 2:** low melting point + never conducts = **covalent molecular**. Melting only overcomes weak attractions *between* molecules (hence 80°C), and dissolving releases neutral molecules — no charged particles, no conduction.

**Answer: Solid 1 is ionic; Solid 2 is molecular covalent — the conductivity fingerprint plus melting point tells the whole story.**

</details>

### Problem 7
Vitamin C has the molecular formula $\ce{C6H8O6}$. (a) What is its empirical formula? (b) Why can't an ionic compound like $\ce{CaCl2}$ have a separate molecular formula?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

(a) Divide subscripts by their greatest common factor, 2: $\ce{C3H4O3}$.

(b) A molecular formula counts atoms in one discrete molecule. $\ce{CaCl2}$ is a **lattice** — a continuous 3D array of $\ce{Ca^2+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$ ions with no discrete unit to count. Its formula unit expresses only the ion *ratio* (1 : 2), which is by definition an empirical formula.

**Answers: (a) $\ce{C3H4O3}$; (b) ionic compounds have no molecules, so only a ratio (formula unit) is meaningful.**

</details>

### Problem 8
Classify the bonding in $\ce{NaNO3}$ (sodium nitrate). Careful — this is a gotcha in the same family as $\ce{NH4Cl}$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

$\ce{NaNO3}$ contains the metal cation $\ce{Na+}$ and the polyatomic anion nitrate, $\ce{NO3-}$.

- **Between** $\ce{Na+}$ and $\ce{NO3-}$: ionic bonding (electrostatic attraction in a lattice).
- **Within** $\ce{NO3-}$: the N and O atoms are nonmetals sharing electron pairs — covalent bonds.

**Answer: both — ionic between $\ce{Na+}$ and $\ce{NO3-}$, covalent inside the nitrate ion.** Any compound containing a polyatomic ion has this dual character.

</details>

### Problem 9
Rank $\ce{N2}$, $\ce{O2}$, and $\ce{F2}$ by bond strength (strongest first) and justify using shared pairs.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Count electrons each atom needs: N needs 3 → triple bond in $\ce{N2}$ (3 shared pairs); O needs 2 → double bond in $\ce{O2}$ (2 pairs); F needs 1 → single bond in $\ce{F2}$ (1 pair).

More shared pairs → shorter, stronger bond.

**Answer: $\ce{N2} > \ce{O2} > \ce{F2}$ — triple > double > single.**

</details>

### Problem 10
$\ce{MgO}$ melts at 2852°C while $\ce{NaCl}$ melts at 801°C, even though both are ionic solids. Explain the difference using Coulomb's-law reasoning (charge and distance).

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Electrostatic attraction grows with the **product of the charges** and shrinks with **distance** between ions ($F \propto |q_1 q_2|/r^2$).

$\ce{MgO}$: $\ce{Mg^2+}$ and $\ce{O^2-}$ → charge product $2 \times 2 = 4$. Also, both ions are relatively small, so they sit close together.

$\ce{NaCl}$: $\ce{Na+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$ → charge product $1 \times 1 = 1$, and $\ce{Cl-}$ is a larger ion.

Larger charges + shorter distance → much stronger attractions throughout the $\ce{MgO}$ lattice → far more energy needed to melt it.

**Answer: $\ce{MgO}$'s doubled ion charges (and smaller ion separation) make its lattice attractions roughly four times stronger, giving the much higher melting point.**

</details>

### Problem 11
Why are ionic crystals hard yet brittle, while metals are malleable? Answer in terms of what happens when a layer of particles shifts.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

**Ionic:** in the lattice, every $+$ is surrounded by $-$. Strike the crystal and a layer slides one position over: suddenly $+$ aligns with $+$ and $-$ with $-$. Like charges **repel**, and the crystal cleaves along that plane — hard (strong attractions resist deformation) but brittle (shifting triggers repulsion).

**Metallic:** the cations sit in a delocalized electron sea. When layers slide, the sea instantly re-flows around the new arrangement — attraction is maintained in the shifted position, so the metal dents and bends instead of shattering.

**Answer: shifting an ionic lattice creates like-charge repulsion (shatter); shifting a metallic lattice keeps the cation–electron-sea attraction intact (bend).**

</details>

### Problem 12
In a water molecule, each $\ce{O-H}$ bond has $\Delta EN = 1.4$. Sketch (describe) the partial charges, and explain the difference between $\delta-$ on the oxygen and the full $-2$ charge on the oxide ion $\ce{O^2-}$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

In $\ce{H2O}$, oxygen hogs each shared pair: oxygen carries $\delta-$ and each hydrogen carries $\delta+$:

$$\overset{\delta+}{\ce{H}}\bond{-}\overset{\delta-}{\ce{O}}\bond{-}\overset{\delta+}{\ce{H}}$$

The $\delta-$ is a **partial** charge: the shared electrons spend more time near oxygen but were never fully transferred — the bond is polar covalent (unequal sharing, one friend hogging the straw).

In $\ce{O^2-}$, oxygen has fully **gained two electrons** from a metal — a complete transfer producing an integer charge of $-2$.

**Answer: $\delta-$ = electron density shifted (sharing, unequal); $-2$ = electrons completely transferred (ionic). Shifted ≠ transferred.**

</details>

### Problem 13
Wax (nonpolar covalent molecules) doesn't dissolve in water, but table salt does. Explain both observations with "like dissolves like."

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Water is highly polar ($\delta-$ oxygen, $\delta+$ hydrogens), so it interacts strongly with charged or polar particles.

**Salt:** water molecules surround each ion — $\delta-$ oxygen ends toward $\ce{Na+}$, $\delta+$ hydrogen ends toward $\ce{Cl-}$ — and those new attractions compensate for breaking up the lattice: $\ce{NaCl(s) -> Na+(aq) + Cl-(aq)}$.

**Wax:** the molecules are nonpolar — no charges, no significant partial charges — so water molecules have nothing to grab. Water would rather stick to itself than mingle with wax, and the wax stays undissolved.

**Answer: polar water dissolves ionic salt by surrounding the ions; nonpolar wax offers no charges for polar water to attract, so it doesn't dissolve — like dissolves like.**

</details>

### Problem 14
Predict the formula and the bonding type of the compound formed from: (a) barium and nitrogen, (b) carbon and hydrogen (one C, enough H to complete carbon's octet).

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

(a) Ba = Group 2 metal → $\ce{Ba^2+}$; N = Group 15 nonmetal → $\ce{N^3-}$. Metal + nonmetal = **ionic**. Criss-cross: $\ce{Ba3N2}$. Check: $3(+2) + 2(-3) = 0$. ✓

(b) Two nonmetals = **covalent**. Carbon needs 4 more electrons, each H shares 1 pair → four single bonds → the molecule $\ce{CH4}$. (No criss-cross here — covalent formulas come from octet counting, not charge balancing.)

**Answers: (a) $\ce{Ba3N2}$, ionic; (b) $\ce{CH4}$, covalent.**

</details>

### Problem 15
FRQ-style: A student claims, "When sugar ($\ce{C12H22O11}$) melts at 186°C, its covalent bonds break, which is why it melts so much lower than $\ce{NaCl}$." Identify the error and write the corrected explanation.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

**The error:** melting sugar does **not** break covalent bonds. The $\ce{C-H}$, $\ce{C-O}$, and $\ce{O-H}$ bonds inside each sugar molecule stay fully intact; whole molecules simply gain enough energy to slide past one another.

**Corrected explanation:** sugar is a molecular covalent solid, so melting it only requires overcoming the relatively weak attractions **between** neutral molecules — a low energy cost, hence 186°C. $\ce{NaCl}$ is an ionic solid; melting it requires overcoming strong electrostatic attractions between $\ce{Na+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$ ions throughout the entire lattice — a much higher energy cost, hence 801°C.

**Answer: melting molecular solids breaks between-molecule attractions, never the covalent bonds within molecules; the strong lattice attractions in ionic $\ce{NaCl}$ explain its far higher melting point.**

</details>
