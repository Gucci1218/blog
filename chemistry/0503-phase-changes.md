# Phase Changes and States of Matter
<p class="article-date">July 5, 2026</p>

*It was 92 degrees on the Fourth, so I made myself an iced boba and set it on the kitchen counter while I answered texts. Twenty minutes later there were four things happening on that counter at once. The ice cubes had shrunk into little rounded pebbles (solid melting into liquid), the tea had gone pale and watery, the outside of the glass was dripping wet — and here's the part that fooled me for years: the glass wasn't leaking. That "sweat" is water vapor from the hot summer air hitting the cold glass and condensing into liquid, gas becoming liquid, the exact reverse of the ice melting inside. Then I went to shower and the bathroom mirror fogged solid white, and by the time I brushed my teeth it had cleared itself — condensation, then evaporation, running backward and forward in five minutes. And the wild thing is that in all of it, nobody added or removed a single water molecule. The same $\ce{H2O}$ that was locked in an ice cube ended up as vapor on the mirror. All that changed was how tightly the molecules were packed and how much energy they carried. That is the entire idea of Topic 3.3: phase changes rearrange molecules and shuffle energy in and out, but they never make or break chemical bonds — and this is where the intermolecular forces you just learned finally start doing visible, dripping-on-the-counter work.*

## The Core Concept

### Intuition First

Back to the counter, because that sweating boba glass is going to teach the whole topic.

**Start with the ice cube.** Inside a solid, the water molecules are packed tight and locked into a fixed arrangement. They still jiggle — they always jiggle above absolute zero — but each one is trapped in place by its neighbors, held by the hydrogen bonds you met in [Intermolecular Forces](/chemistry/0501-intermolecular-forces). A solid holds its shape and barely compresses because there is no room and no freedom to move.

**Add energy and you melt it.** When heat flows into the ice, the molecules jiggle harder until they finally have enough energy to break free of their fixed lattice positions and start sliding past one another. That is melting: solid $\to$ liquid. The molecules are still touching, still gripping each other with IMFs, but now they can flow and take the shape of the glass. Notice the key move — **melting did not break any $\ce{O-H}$ bonds inside the water molecules.** Each molecule is still $\ce{H2O}$. We only loosened the *forces between* molecules, never the *bonds within* them. That distinction is the beating heart of this entire topic.

**Now the sweat on the outside.** The air on a hot day is full of water vapor — free gas molecules zipping around, barely touching, spaced far apart. When those fast gas molecules smack into the cold glass, they lose energy, slow down, and get captured by IMFs into a liquid film. That is condensation: gas $\to$ liquid, the exact reverse of evaporation. The drink didn't leak; the *air* deposited its water onto the cold surface. And crucially, condensation **releases** energy (the glass and the film warm the surrounding air a touch), because forming attractions always releases energy — the same way a magnet snapping onto the fridge gives up energy with a *clack*.

**The mirror finishes the story.** Hot shower steam (gas) hits the cool mirror and condenses into a fog of tiny droplets. A few minutes later, room air pulls that liquid back off as vapor — evaporation — and the mirror clears. Fog, then clear. Condensation, then evaporation. Same molecules, riding energy in and back out.

So here is the map that organizes everything below:

- **The three states = how tightly molecules are packed and how freely they move.** Solid: packed and locked. Liquid: packed but sliding. Gas: spread out and flying free.
- **A phase change = adding or removing energy to overcome or restore IMFs — never making or breaking chemical bonds.** Push energy *in* to pull molecules apart (endothermic, going *up* the ladder). Let energy *out* to let molecules snap together (exothermic, coming *down*).

Keep the boba glass in mind. Every equation, curve, and diagram in this article is just careful bookkeeping for what happened on my counter.

### The Three States at the Molecular Level

| Property | Solid | Liquid | Gas |
|---|---|---|---|
| Arrangement | Ordered, fixed lattice | Close together but disordered | Far apart, random |
| Molecular motion | Vibrate in place | Slide and tumble past each other | Fly freely, fast, mostly straight lines |
| IMFs | Dominant — molecules locked | Significant — molecules touch but move | Negligible — molecules rarely interact |
| Shape | Fixed | Takes shape of container | Fills entire container |
| Volume | Fixed | Fixed | Fills container |
| Compressibility | Nearly none | Very little | Highly compressible (lots of empty space) |
| Relative energy | Lowest | Middle | Highest |

The one-line summary: **as you add energy, molecules move faster and spread out**, climbing from locked (solid) to sliding (liquid) to free (gas). Compressibility is the giveaway — a gas squishes because it is mostly empty space between molecules; a solid and a liquid barely budge because the molecules are already touching.

### The Six Phase Changes

There are three pairs, and each pair is a two-way street. Going *up* the energy ladder (toward gas) always **absorbs** energy — **endothermic**. Coming *down* the ladder (toward solid) always **releases** energy — **exothermic**. That is the whole sign rule, and it never has an exception.

| Phase change | Direction | Energy | Everyday example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Melting** (fusion) | solid $\to$ liquid | Absorbs (endo) | Ice cubes turning to water in the boba |
| **Freezing** | liquid $\to$ solid | Releases (exo) | Water turning to ice cubes in the tray |
| **Vaporization** (boiling/evaporation) | liquid $\to$ gas | Absorbs (endo) | The mirror clearing; a pot boiling dry |
| **Condensation** | gas $\to$ liquid | Releases (exo) | The "sweat" on the cold glass; fog on the mirror |
| **Sublimation** | solid $\to$ gas | Absorbs (endo) | Dry ice smoking; ice cubes shrinking in the freezer |
| **Deposition** | gas $\to$ solid | Releases (exo) | Frost forming on a car window overnight |

$$\underbrace{\text{solid} \;\to\; \text{liquid} \;\to\; \text{gas}}_{\textbf{endothermic (energy in, going up)}} \qquad\qquad \underbrace{\text{gas} \;\to\; \text{liquid} \;\to\; \text{solid}}_{\textbf{exothermic (energy out, coming down)}}$$

**Sublimation deserves a second look**, because it skips the liquid entirely. Dry ice — solid $\ce{CO2}$ — goes straight from solid to gas at ordinary pressure, which is why it "smokes" without ever forming a puddle (the fog is water vapor from the air condensing in the cold, not the $\ce{CO2}$ itself). And here is the household version you have definitely seen: leave ice cubes in the freezer for a month and they shrink into little shriveled nuggets. Nothing melted — the freezer is well below $0\,^\circ\text{C}$. The surface molecules slowly **sublime** directly into water vapor, which then **deposits** as the frost caked on the back wall of the freezer. Deposition is also how **frost** forms on a cold morning: water vapor in the air turns straight into solid ice crystals on the windshield without ever being dew.

### The Heating Curve of Water

This is *the* AP diagram for Topic 3.3. Picture steadily heating a chunk of ice — adding heat at a constant rate — and plotting **temperature (y-axis) versus heat added (x-axis)**. You do not get a straight ramp. You get ramps interrupted by two dead-flat shelves:

```
 T (°C)
  120 |                                              _____ E  (steam heating)
      |                                          ___/
  100 |                 C________________________D          <- boiling plateau (flat!)
      |                 /                        (liquid -> gas)
      |               /
      |             /   (liquid water heating)
    0 |    A_______B                                        <- melting plateau (flat!)
      |    /       (solid -> liquid)
  -10 |  _/  (ice heating)
      |_/____________________________________________________  heat added ->
        (A) ice   (A-B) melt   (B-C) water   (C-D) boil   (D-E) steam
```

Read it left to right, five segments:

1. **Ice warming** (sloped, up from $-10\,^\circ\text{C}$ to $0\,^\circ\text{C}$): heat raises the temperature of solid ice. Molecules vibrate faster. **KE increases.**
2. **Melting plateau** at $0\,^\circ\text{C}$ (flat, A$\to$B): heat pours in but the **temperature does not change**. Every joule goes into breaking the IMFs that lock the ice lattice, letting molecules slip free. **PE increases; KE constant.**
3. **Liquid water warming** (sloped, B$\to$C, $0\,^\circ\text{C} \to 100\,^\circ\text{C}$): temperature climbs again as the liquid heats. **KE increases.**
4. **Boiling plateau** at $100\,^\circ\text{C}$ (flat, C$\to$D, and much *longer* than the melting one): heat pours in, temperature holds steady, and every joule goes into ripping molecules completely away from one another into the gas phase. **PE increases; KE constant.**
5. **Steam warming** (sloped, D$\to$E, above $100\,^\circ\text{C}$): now the gas itself heats up. **KE increases.**

**Why are the plateaus flat? This is the money question.** Temperature measures average **kinetic energy** — how fast the molecules are *moving*. During a phase change, the incoming energy is not speeding molecules up; it is being spent as **potential energy** to pull molecules apart against their IMFs. You are paying the "separation tax," not the "speed-up tax." Until every molecule in the sample has been freed (all the ice melted, or all the water boiled), the temperature cannot rise — there is a queue, and heat waits its turn at constant temperature. The instant the last molecule changes phase, temperature starts climbing again. That is why the melting point and boiling point show up as flat shelves: **during a phase change, added energy raises PE, not KE, so the thermometer sits still.**

The two-slot mental model, which you will reuse all year:

- **Sloped segment $\Rightarrow$ temperature changing $\Rightarrow$ KE changing $\Rightarrow$ use $q = mc\Delta T$.**
- **Flat plateau $\Rightarrow$ temperature constant $\Rightarrow$ PE changing $\Rightarrow$ use $q = m\cdot\Delta H$ (latent heat).**

### Heat Calculations: Sensible Heat vs. Latent Heat

Every calorimetry problem in this topic is built from just two formulas, one for each kind of segment.

**On the slopes — sensible heat (temperature changes):**

$$q = mc\Delta T$$

where $m$ is mass, $c$ is the **specific heat** of that phase, and $\Delta T = T_\text{final} - T_\text{initial}$. Note that ice, liquid water, and steam have *different* specific heats — you must switch $c$ when the phase switches.

**On the plateaus — latent heat (temperature constant):**

$$q = m \cdot \Delta H_\text{fus} \quad\text{(melting/freezing)} \qquad q = m \cdot \Delta H_\text{vap} \quad\text{(boiling/condensing)}$$

or, if you are working in moles, $q = n\cdot\Delta H$ with molar heats of fusion/vaporization. "Latent" means *hidden* — the heat is hidden in the phase change and never shows up on the thermometer.

Here are the numbers for water you will use over and over (AP gives you these on the problem):

| Quantity | Symbol | Value |
|---|---|---|
| Specific heat of ice | $c_\text{ice}$ | $2.09\ \text{J/(g}\cdot^\circ\text{C)}$ |
| Specific heat of liquid water | $c_\text{water}$ | $4.18\ \text{J/(g}\cdot^\circ\text{C)}$ |
| Specific heat of steam | $c_\text{steam}$ | $2.01\ \text{J/(g}\cdot^\circ\text{C)}$ |
| Heat of fusion (melting) | $\Delta H_\text{fus}$ | $334\ \text{J/g}$ = $6.01\ \text{kJ/mol}$ |
| Heat of vaporization (boiling) | $\Delta H_\text{vap}$ | $2260\ \text{J/g}$ = $40.7\ \text{kJ/mol}$ |

**Why is $\Delta H_\text{vap}$ so much bigger than $\Delta H_\text{fus}$?** For water it is nearly seven times larger ($2260$ vs. $334\ \text{J/g}$). Think about what each change actually does to the IMFs. **Melting** only *loosens* the grip — molecules break out of their fixed positions but stay packed together, still touching, still hydrogen-bonding as a liquid. You pay to unlock the lattice, not to leave the building. **Vaporization** *fully separates* every molecule from every other one — you have to break essentially all of the IMFs and drag each molecule out into empty space. Loosening a crowd so people can shuffle around is cheap; sending everyone home to separate houses is expensive. That is why the boiling plateau on the heating curve is so much longer than the melting plateau, and why steam burns are so vicious — condensing steam dumps that entire $2260\ \text{J/g}$ back into your skin.

For a multi-step "ice to steam" problem, the strategy is always the same: **break the journey into segments, use $q=mc\Delta T$ on every slope and $q=m\Delta H$ on every plateau, then add them all up.**

### Phase Diagrams

A heating curve fixes the pressure and varies the heat. A **phase diagram** does something different: it maps out which phase is stable at *every* combination of **pressure (y-axis)** and **temperature (x-axis)**. It is the molecule's whole life story on one chart.

```
  P |         (solid) |            melting
    |                 |           curve
    |    SOLID        |        /
    |                 |      /   LIQUID
    | 1 atm - - - - - + - -/- - - - - - - - - -  * critical point
    |                /|   /                   /
    |     sublim-  /  |  / vaporization    /
    |     ation  /  TRIPLE  curve       /
    |     curve/    POINT           GAS
    |        /       |
    |______/_________|___________________________  T
```

The three **regions** are the three phases — solid (upper left, cold and/or high pressure), liquid (upper middle), and gas (lower right, hot and/or low pressure). The three **curves** are the borders where two phases coexist:

- **Melting (fusion) curve** — the solid–liquid border
- **Vaporization curve** — the liquid–gas border
- **Sublimation curve** — the solid–gas border

Two special points live on this map:

- **Triple point** — the single pressure and temperature where all three curves meet and **solid, liquid, and gas coexist** simultaneously. For water it sits at $0.006\ \text{atm}$ and $0.01\,^\circ\text{C}$.
- **Critical point** — the end of the vaporization curve. Beyond it (higher $T$ and $P$) the liquid and gas become indistinguishable, a **supercritical fluid**; there is no longer a boiling line to cross.

**Reading a phase diagram** is a two-step habit: (1) plot the point for the given $(T, P)$ and read off which region it lands in; (2) to find a phase change, move along a line and note which curve you cross. Move *right* (heat up at constant pressure) and you cross from solid to liquid to gas. Move *down* (drop pressure at constant temperature) and you push toward the gas phase — which is exactly the mountain-boiling story below.

**Water's weird solid–liquid line.** For almost every substance, the melting curve leans slightly to the *right* — a positive slope — because higher pressure favors the denser solid. Water is the famous rebel: its melting curve leans *left*, a **negative slope**. That is because **solid water (ice) is less dense than liquid water** — the hydrogen-bonded ice lattice is open and roomy, which is why ice floats in your boba instead of sinking. The negative slope means squeezing ice can actually *melt* it, and it is the classic (if slightly oversimplified) story for why ice is slippery under a skate blade. Compare $\ce{CO2}$, a totally normal citizen: its solid–liquid line has a positive slope because solid $\ce{CO2}$ is denser than liquid $\ce{CO2}$, like nearly everything else.

**Why dry ice sublimes and water doesn't (at room pressure).** Look at where $1\ \text{atm}$ falls relative to each substance's triple point. Water's triple point is at a very low pressure ($0.006\ \text{atm}$), so at $1\ \text{atm}$ a horizontal heating line passes *above* the triple point and crosses solid $\to$ liquid $\to$ gas — ice melts, then boils, just like on your stove. But $\ce{CO2}$'s triple point sits at about $5.1\ \text{atm}$, *above* $1\ \text{atm}$. So at ordinary room pressure the heating line passes *below* the triple point and crosses only the sublimation curve: solid $\ce{CO2}$ goes straight to gas, no liquid ever. That is why it is called *dry* ice — no puddle, ever.

**Why water boils at a lower temperature on a mountain.** Boiling happens when a liquid's **vapor pressure** (from [Properties from IMFs](/chemistry/0502-properties-from-imfs)) equals the surrounding atmospheric pressure. On a mountain the atmosphere is thinner, so the required vapor pressure is reached at a *lower* temperature — water might boil at $90\,^\circ\text{C}$ instead of $100\,^\circ\text{C}$. On the phase diagram this is just sliding *down* the vaporization curve: lower pressure meets the curve at a lower temperature. (Which is why boxed mac-and-cheese takes longer at altitude — the water is boiling but it is cooler than sea-level boiling water.)

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Name That Change

**Problem:** For each everyday event, name the phase change and state whether it absorbs or releases energy: (a) the boba's ice cubes turn to water, (b) "sweat" forms on the cold glass, (c) the bathroom mirror fogs up, (d) frost forms on a windshield overnight, (e) dry ice "smokes" away to nothing.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Identify the before and after state for each.**

- (a) solid $\to$ liquid = **melting (fusion)** — **absorbs** energy (endo)
- (b) gas $\to$ liquid (water vapor from air becomes liquid) = **condensation** — **releases** energy (exo)
- (c) gas $\to$ liquid (steam becomes droplets) = **condensation** — **releases** energy (exo)
- (d) gas $\to$ solid (water vapor becomes ice crystals) = **deposition** — **releases** energy (exo)
- (e) solid $\to$ gas ($\ce{CO2}$ straight to vapor) = **sublimation** — **absorbs** energy (endo)

**Step 2 — Sanity check with the ladder.** Going up toward gas absorbs; coming down toward solid releases. (a) and (e) go up (absorb); (b), (c), (d) come down (release). Consistent.

**Answer: (a) melting, absorbs; (b) condensation, releases; (c) condensation, releases; (d) deposition, releases; (e) sublimation, absorbs.**

### Example 2: The Shrinking Freezer Ice

**Problem:** Ice cubes left in a $-18\,^\circ\text{C}$ freezer for weeks slowly shrink into small nuggets, and frost builds up on the freezer wall. No melting occurs. Explain both observations with phase-change vocabulary.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — The cubes shrink.** The freezer is far below $0\,^\circ\text{C}$, so nothing melts. Surface water molecules with enough energy escape *directly* from solid ice into water vapor — **sublimation** (solid $\to$ gas). Over weeks, this whittles the cubes down.

**Step 2 — The frost appears.** That water vapor drifts to the coldest surface (the freezer wall) and turns straight back into solid ice crystals — **deposition** (gas $\to$ solid).

**Step 3 — Connect them.** It is the same water: subliming off the cubes, depositing on the wall. No liquid ever appears.

**Answer: The cubes shrink by sublimation (solid $\to$ gas) and the frost forms by deposition (gas $\to$ solid) — the reverse pair, with no melting in between.**

### Example 3: Reading the Heating Curve

**Problem:** On the heating curve of water, a student points to the flat plateau at $100\,^\circ\text{C}$. (a) What is physically happening to the molecules? (b) Is kinetic or potential energy changing? (c) Which formula applies to this segment?

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — What's happening.** At $100\,^\circ\text{C}$ and constant temperature, added heat is converting liquid water to gas — **boiling/vaporization**. Molecules are being pulled completely apart against their IMFs.

**Step 2 — Which energy.** Temperature (average KE) is constant, so kinetic energy is not changing. The energy is going into **potential energy** — separating the molecules.

**Step 3 — Which formula.** A flat plateau means a phase change, so use latent heat: $q = m\cdot\Delta H_\text{vap}$ (not $q = mc\Delta T$).

**Answer: (a) liquid is boiling to gas as IMFs break; (b) potential energy is increasing while KE (temperature) stays constant; (c) $q = m\Delta H_\text{vap}$.**

### Example 4: A Simple Slope Calculation

**Problem:** How much heat is required to warm $150.\ \text{g}$ of liquid water from $20.0\,^\circ\text{C}$ to $85.0\,^\circ\text{C}$? ($c_\text{water} = 4.18\ \text{J/(g}\cdot^\circ\text{C)}$)

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Pick the formula.** Temperature is changing, no phase change, so this is sensible heat: $q = mc\Delta T$.

**Step 2 — Plug in.** $\Delta T = 85.0 - 20.0 = 65.0\,^\circ\text{C}$.

$$q = (150.\ \text{g})(4.18\ \tfrac{\text{J}}{\text{g}\cdot^\circ\text{C}})(65.0\,^\circ\text{C}) = 40{,}755\ \text{J}$$

**Step 3 — Round.** $\approx 4.08 \times 10^4\ \text{J} = 40.8\ \text{kJ}$.

**Answer: about $40.8\ \text{kJ}$ (positive, because heat is absorbed).**

### Example 5: A Simple Plateau Calculation

**Problem:** How much heat is required to melt $150.\ \text{g}$ of ice at $0\,^\circ\text{C}$ into liquid water at $0\,^\circ\text{C}$? ($\Delta H_\text{fus} = 334\ \text{J/g}$)

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Pick the formula.** Temperature is constant at $0\,^\circ\text{C}$ (a phase change), so this is latent heat: $q = m\cdot\Delta H_\text{fus}$.

**Step 2 — Plug in.**

$$q = (150.\ \text{g})(334\ \tfrac{\text{J}}{\text{g}}) = 50{,}100\ \text{J} \approx 50.1\ \text{kJ}$$

**Step 3 — Note the comparison.** Melting $150\ \text{g}$ of ice ($50.1\ \text{kJ}$) takes *more* heat than warming that same water $65$ degrees in Example 4 ($40.8\ \text{kJ}$) — phase changes are expensive.

**Answer: about $50.1\ \text{kJ}$.**

### Example 6: The Flagship Multi-Step Problem — Ice at $-10\,^\circ\text{C}$ to Steam at $120\,^\circ\text{C}$

**Problem:** Calculate the total heat needed to convert $18.0\ \text{g}$ of ice at $-10.0\,^\circ\text{C}$ into steam at $120.0\,^\circ\text{C}$. Use $c_\text{ice}=2.09$, $c_\text{water}=4.18$, $c_\text{steam}=2.01\ \text{J/(g}\cdot^\circ\text{C)}$; $\Delta H_\text{fus}=334\ \text{J/g}$; $\Delta H_\text{vap}=2260\ \text{J/g}$.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Break the journey into five segments** (two slopes for warming solid/liquid, one plateau to melt, one slope to warm liquid... in order: warm ice, melt, warm water, boil, warm steam).

**Step 2 — Segment 1: warm ice from $-10.0$ to $0\,^\circ\text{C}$** (slope, $q=mc\Delta T$):
$$q_1 = (18.0)(2.09)(10.0) = 376\ \text{J} = 0.376\ \text{kJ}$$

**Step 3 — Segment 2: melt ice at $0\,^\circ\text{C}$** (plateau, $q=m\Delta H_\text{fus}$):
$$q_2 = (18.0)(334) = 6012\ \text{J} = 6.01\ \text{kJ}$$

**Step 4 — Segment 3: warm water from $0$ to $100\,^\circ\text{C}$** (slope):
$$q_3 = (18.0)(4.18)(100.0) = 7524\ \text{J} = 7.52\ \text{kJ}$$

**Step 5 — Segment 4: boil water at $100\,^\circ\text{C}$** (plateau, $q=m\Delta H_\text{vap}$):
$$q_4 = (18.0)(2260) = 40{,}680\ \text{J} = 40.68\ \text{kJ}$$

**Step 6 — Segment 5: warm steam from $100$ to $120\,^\circ\text{C}$** (slope):
$$q_5 = (18.0)(2.01)(20.0) = 724\ \text{J} = 0.724\ \text{kJ}$$

**Step 7 — Sum everything:**
$$q_\text{total} = 0.376 + 6.01 + 7.52 + 40.68 + 0.724 = 55.3\ \text{kJ}$$

**Step 8 — Sanity check.** The boiling plateau ($40.68\ \text{kJ}$) dominates — it alone is bigger than all four other segments combined, exactly as we predicted from $\Delta H_\text{vap} \gg \Delta H_\text{fus}$.

**Answer: about $55.3\ \text{kJ}$ ($5.53 \times 10^4\ \text{J}$).**

### Example 7: A Cooling (Exothermic) Multi-Step Problem

**Problem:** How much heat is *released* when $50.0\ \text{g}$ of steam at $100.0\,^\circ\text{C}$ condenses and then cools to liquid water at $40.0\,^\circ\text{C}$?

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Two segments: condense (plateau), then cool the liquid (slope).** Both release heat, so both $q$ values will be negative (energy leaves the water).

**Step 2 — Condense at $100\,^\circ\text{C}$** (plateau; condensation releases the same magnitude as vaporization):
$$q_1 = -(50.0)(2260) = -113{,}000\ \text{J} = -113\ \text{kJ}$$

**Step 3 — Cool liquid from $100$ to $40\,^\circ\text{C}$** (slope), $\Delta T = 40.0-100.0 = -60.0\,^\circ\text{C}$:
$$q_2 = (50.0)(4.18)(-60.0) = -12{,}540\ \text{J} = -12.5\ \text{kJ}$$

**Step 4 — Sum:**
$$q_\text{total} = -113 + (-12.5) = -126\ \text{kJ}$$

**Step 5 — Interpret.** The negative sign means $126\ \text{kJ}$ is *released*. This is exactly why a steam burn is so much worse than a hot-water burn: most of that energy comes from condensation, dumped straight into your skin.

**Answer: $126\ \text{kJ}$ is released (i.e., $q = -126\ \text{kJ}$).**

### Example 8: Why Vaporization Costs More Than Fusion

**Problem:** For water, $\Delta H_\text{fus} = 334\ \text{J/g}$ but $\Delta H_\text{vap} = 2260\ \text{J/g}$. Explain the difference at the molecular level.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — What melting does.** Fusion only *loosens* the lattice: molecules break out of fixed positions but remain packed together and still hydrogen-bond as a liquid. Only *some* IMFs are overcome.

**Step 2 — What vaporization does.** Vaporization *fully separates* every molecule into the gas phase, breaking essentially *all* the remaining IMFs and giving each molecule its own space.

**Step 3 — Compare the cost.** Unlocking a crowd so people can shuffle (melting) is cheap; sending everyone to separate houses (boiling) is far more expensive — hence the ~7× larger value.

**Answer: Melting only loosens IMFs (molecules stay in contact); vaporization breaks essentially all IMFs to fully separate the molecules, so it requires much more energy.**

### Example 9: Reading a Water Phase Diagram

**Problem:** Using water's phase diagram (triple point $0.006\ \text{atm}$, $0.01\,^\circ\text{C}$; normal freezing/boiling at $1\ \text{atm}$): (a) At $1\ \text{atm}$ and $50\,^\circ\text{C}$, which phase? (b) Starting there, what happens as you heat to $150\,^\circ\text{C}$ at constant pressure?

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Locate (a).** At $1\ \text{atm}$, water is liquid between $0\,^\circ\text{C}$ and $100\,^\circ\text{C}$. Since $50\,^\circ\text{C}$ is in that window, the point lands in the **liquid** region.

**Step 2 — Trace the path in (b).** Moving right at constant $1\ \text{atm}$: you stay liquid until $100\,^\circ\text{C}$, where you cross the **vaporization curve** (liquid $\to$ gas). Above $100\,^\circ\text{C}$ you are in the **gas** region, so at $150\,^\circ\text{C}$ you have steam.

**Step 3 — Summarize the crossing.** One phase change on this path: boiling at $100\,^\circ\text{C}$.

**Answer: (a) liquid; (b) it warms as liquid, boils to gas at $100\,^\circ\text{C}$, and ends as steam at $150\,^\circ\text{C}$.**

### Example 10: Why Dry Ice Has No Puddle

**Problem:** $\ce{CO2}$'s triple point is at about $5.1\ \text{atm}$ and $-57\,^\circ\text{C}$. Use a phase diagram argument to explain why solid $\ce{CO2}$ sublimes directly to gas at room pressure ($1\ \text{atm}$) instead of melting.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Compare $1\ \text{atm}$ to the triple point pressure.** Room pressure ($1\ \text{atm}$) is *below* $\ce{CO2}$'s triple-point pressure ($5.1\ \text{atm}$).

**Step 2 — Trace a constant-pressure heating line at $1\ \text{atm}$.** Because that line sits below the triple point, it never enters the liquid region — it passes only across the **sublimation curve**, taking solid straight to gas.

**Step 3 — What it would take to get liquid $\ce{CO2}$.** You would need to raise the pressure above $5.1\ \text{atm}$ first (which is why $\ce{CO2}$ fire extinguishers, pressurized, *do* contain liquid $\ce{CO2}$).

**Answer: At $1\ \text{atm}$ — below the $5.1\ \text{atm}$ triple point — a heating path crosses only the sublimation curve, so solid $\ce{CO2}$ goes directly to gas with no liquid stage.**

### Example 11: Water's Backward Melting Line

**Problem:** Water's solid–liquid line has a *negative* slope, while $\ce{CO2}$'s has the usual *positive* slope. What property of each solid causes this, and what everyday observation reflects water's behavior?

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — What the slope encodes.** The solid–liquid line tilts based on which phase is denser. If the solid is denser than the liquid (normal), the line has a positive slope. If the solid is *less* dense than the liquid, the line tilts negative.

**Step 2 — Water.** Ice's open hydrogen-bonded lattice makes solid water **less dense** than liquid water — so the melting line has a **negative slope**, and increasing pressure on ice can melt it.

**Step 3 — $\ce{CO2}$.** Solid $\ce{CO2}$ is **denser** than liquid $\ce{CO2}$ (the normal case), giving a **positive** slope.

**Step 4 — Everyday sign.** Water's low-density solid is why **ice floats** — in your boba, in lakes, everywhere — and (roughly) why pressure helps a skate blade glide.

**Answer: Water's ice is less dense than its liquid (negative slope); $\ce{CO2}$'s solid is denser than its liquid (positive slope). Ice floating is the everyday consequence of water's anomaly.**

### Example 12: Boiling on a Mountain

**Problem:** At the top of a $4000\ \text{m}$ peak the atmospheric pressure is only about $0.6\ \text{atm}$, and water boils near $86\,^\circ\text{C}$. Explain, using vapor pressure and the phase diagram, why the boiling point drops.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Definition of boiling.** A liquid boils when its **vapor pressure** equals the surrounding atmospheric pressure (from [Properties from IMFs](/chemistry/0502-properties-from-imfs)).

**Step 2 — Lower the surrounding pressure.** At $0.6\ \text{atm}$, the water only needs to reach a vapor pressure of $0.6\ \text{atm}$ to boil — and it reaches that lower vapor pressure at a lower temperature.

**Step 3 — Phase-diagram view.** Dropping the pressure means meeting the **vaporization curve** at a point farther down and to the left — lower pressure, lower boiling temperature.

**Step 4 — Consequence.** The boiling water is genuinely cooler ($\sim86\,^\circ\text{C}$), so food cooks more slowly at altitude.

**Answer: Lower atmospheric pressure means the vapor pressure needed to boil is reached at a lower temperature — you slide down the vaporization curve — so water boils below $100\,^\circ\text{C}$ on a mountain.**

## Common Mistakes

| The Mistake | Why It Happens | The Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "Melting breaks the bonds in water" | Confusing IMFs with covalent bonds | Phase changes break/form **intermolecular forces only** — the $\ce{O-H}$ bonds inside each $\ce{H2O}$ stay intact. It's still water the whole time |
| Using $q=mc\Delta T$ during a phase change | Auto-piloting the "heat" formula | On a flat plateau $\Delta T = 0$, so $mc\Delta T$ gives zero. Use $q = m\Delta H$ there instead |
| Using the same $c$ for ice, water, and steam | Only memorizing $4.18$ | Each phase has its own specific heat ($2.09$, $4.18$, $2.01$). Switch $c$ when the phase switches |
| Thinking temperature rises during boiling | "Adding heat must raise temperature" | During a phase change the added energy raises **PE, not KE**, so temperature holds constant until the change is complete |
| Forgetting a segment in a multi-step problem | Rushing straight to the plateau | Always draw the mini heating curve first and count the segments (up to five for ice$\to$steam). Miss one and the total is wrong |
| Sign errors when cooling/condensing | Not tracking energy direction | Heating/melting/boiling: $q > 0$ (absorbed). Cooling/freezing/condensing: $q < 0$ (released). Set the sign before plugging in |
| "The cold glass leaked" / drink made the water | Misreading condensation | Water vapor **from the air** condenses onto the cold surface — the source is the atmosphere, not the drink |
| Mixing up sublimation and deposition | Both skip the liquid | Sublimation = solid$\to$gas (absorbs, dry ice); deposition = gas$\to$solid (releases, frost). Go up = absorb, down = release |
| Thinking every substance's ice floats | Water feels "normal" | Water is the anomaly: its solid is *less* dense than its liquid (negative-slope melting line). Most substances' solids sink |

## AP Exam Tips

1. **Segment identification is a guaranteed question.** Given a heating curve, be ready to label each region: sloped = single phase warming (KE rising); flat = phase change (PE rising, KE constant). Know that the boiling plateau is *longer* than the melting plateau because $\Delta H_\text{vap} > \Delta H_\text{fus}$.
2. **The "where is KE constant, where is PE changing" concept is the deepest point they test.** The magic sentence: *"During a phase change, the added heat increases potential energy by overcoming intermolecular forces rather than increasing kinetic energy, so the temperature remains constant."* Memorize it.
3. **Formula selection: slope vs. plateau.** Sloped segment $\Rightarrow q=mc\Delta T$. Flat plateau $\Rightarrow q=m\Delta H$ (or $n\Delta H$). Picking the wrong one is the most common calculation error — check "is the temperature changing?" first.
4. **Multi-step problems: draw the mini-curve and sum.** For "ice at $-T_1$ to steam at $T_2$," sketch the five-segment curve, compute each $q$, and add. Partial credit is given segment by segment, so **show every piece**.
5. **Energy sign conventions.** Endothermic (melting, vaporization, sublimation): $q > 0$. Exothermic (freezing, condensation, deposition): $q < 0$. State the direction in words too — graders want "heat is absorbed/released."
6. **Phase diagrams: read the point, then trace the path.** Locate $(T,P)$ to name the phase; move horizontally (change $T$) or vertically (change $P$) and name each curve you cross. Know the triple point (all three coexist) and critical point (liquid/gas become indistinguishable) by definition.
7. **Water's anomaly is a favorite.** Be able to explain the negative-slope solid–liquid line from "ice is less dense than liquid water," and connect it to ice floating. Contrast with a normal substance like $\ce{CO2}$.
8. **Tie boiling to vapor pressure.** Boiling point = temperature where vapor pressure equals external pressure. Lower external pressure (altitude, vacuum) $\Rightarrow$ lower boiling point. This links Topic 3.3 back to Topic 3.1.

## Practice Problems

### Problem 1

Name the phase change and give its energy direction (absorbs/releases) for each: (a) wet clothes drying on a line, (b) a puddle freezing into ice, (c) frost on a windshield, (d) dry ice smoking, (e) dew forming on grass at dawn.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

- (a) liquid $\to$ gas = **vaporization (evaporation)** — absorbs
- (b) liquid $\to$ solid = **freezing** — releases
- (c) gas $\to$ solid = **deposition** — releases
- (d) solid $\to$ gas = **sublimation** — absorbs
- (e) gas $\to$ liquid = **condensation** — releases

Up the ladder (a, d) absorbs; down the ladder (b, c, e) releases.

**(a) evaporation, absorbs; (b) freezing, releases; (c) deposition, releases; (d) sublimation, absorbs; (e) condensation, releases.**

</details>

### Problem 2

Explain, at the molecular level, why a solid holds a fixed shape and is nearly incompressible, while a gas fills its container and compresses easily.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

In a **solid**, molecules are packed into a fixed lattice, held tightly by IMFs and only vibrating in place — there is no room and no freedom to move, so the shape is fixed and there is almost no empty space to squeeze out (nearly incompressible).

In a **gas**, molecules are far apart, moving fast and freely with negligible IMFs — they spread to fill any container, and the large empty space between them can be compressed.

**A solid is packed and locked (fixed shape, incompressible); a gas is spread out and free (fills the container, highly compressible).**

</details>

### Problem 3

On the heating curve of water, the melting plateau (at $0\,^\circ\text{C}$) is much shorter than the boiling plateau (at $100\,^\circ\text{C}$). Explain why.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Plateau length is proportional to the heat needed, i.e. to $\Delta H$. Melting only *loosens* the IMFs ($\Delta H_\text{fus} = 334\ \text{J/g}$), while boiling *fully separates* the molecules by breaking essentially all IMFs ($\Delta H_\text{vap} = 2260\ \text{J/g}$).

Since vaporization needs about seven times more energy at the same heating rate, its plateau is about seven times longer.

**The boiling plateau is longer because $\Delta H_\text{vap} \gg \Delta H_\text{fus}$ — fully separating molecules costs far more than just loosening the lattice.**

</details>

### Problem 4

Calculate the heat required to warm $75.0\ \text{g}$ of ice from $-25.0\,^\circ\text{C}$ to $-5.0\,^\circ\text{C}$. ($c_\text{ice} = 2.09\ \text{J/(g}\cdot^\circ\text{C)}$)

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Temperature changing, single phase (solid) — sensible heat, $q = mc\Delta T$.

$\Delta T = -5.0 - (-25.0) = 20.0\,^\circ\text{C}$

$$q = (75.0)(2.09)(20.0) = 3135\ \text{J} \approx 3.14\ \text{kJ}$$

**About $3.14\ \text{kJ}$ (absorbed).**

</details>

### Problem 5

How much heat is released when $200.\ \text{g}$ of water freezes at $0\,^\circ\text{C}$? ($\Delta H_\text{fus} = 334\ \text{J/g}$)

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Freezing is the reverse of melting at constant temperature — latent heat, and exothermic (releases).

$$q = -(200.)(334) = -66{,}800\ \text{J} = -66.8\ \text{kJ}$$

**$66.8\ \text{kJ}$ is released ($q = -66.8\ \text{kJ}$).**

</details>

### Problem 6

Calculate the total heat needed to convert $36.0\ \text{g}$ of ice at $0\,^\circ\text{C}$ to liquid water at $75.0\,^\circ\text{C}$. ($\Delta H_\text{fus} = 334\ \text{J/g}$; $c_\text{water} = 4.18\ \text{J/(g}\cdot^\circ\text{C)}$)

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Two segments: melt (plateau), then warm liquid (slope).

Melt: $q_1 = (36.0)(334) = 12{,}024\ \text{J} = 12.0\ \text{kJ}$

Warm water $0 \to 75\,^\circ\text{C}$: $q_2 = (36.0)(4.18)(75.0) = 11{,}286\ \text{J} = 11.3\ \text{kJ}$

Total: $q = 12.0 + 11.3 = 23.3\ \text{kJ}$

**About $23.3\ \text{kJ}$.**

</details>

### Problem 7

Calculate the total heat required to convert $10.0\ \text{g}$ of ice at $-15.0\,^\circ\text{C}$ to steam at $110.0\,^\circ\text{C}$. Use $c_\text{ice}=2.09$, $c_\text{water}=4.18$, $c_\text{steam}=2.01\ \text{J/(g}\cdot^\circ\text{C)}$; $\Delta H_\text{fus}=334$; $\Delta H_\text{vap}=2260\ \text{J/g}$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Five segments:

1. Warm ice $-15 \to 0$: $q_1 = (10.0)(2.09)(15.0) = 313.5\ \text{J}$
2. Melt at $0\,^\circ\text{C}$: $q_2 = (10.0)(334) = 3340\ \text{J}$
3. Warm water $0 \to 100$: $q_3 = (10.0)(4.18)(100.0) = 4180\ \text{J}$
4. Boil at $100\,^\circ\text{C}$: $q_4 = (10.0)(2260) = 22{,}600\ \text{J}$
5. Warm steam $100 \to 110$: $q_5 = (10.0)(2.01)(10.0) = 201\ \text{J}$

Sum: $313.5 + 3340 + 4180 + 22{,}600 + 201 = 30{,}634.5\ \text{J}$

**About $30.6\ \text{kJ}$ ($3.06 \times 10^4\ \text{J}$).**

</details>

### Problem 8

A student claims that when they add heat to boiling water on the stove, the water gets hotter and hotter until it's gone. What's wrong with this, and where does the added energy actually go?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Once water reaches its boiling point ($100\,^\circ\text{C}$ at $1\ \text{atm}$), its temperature **stays constant** while it boils. The added heat does not raise the temperature — it goes into **potential energy**, breaking the intermolecular forces to convert liquid into gas.

The thermometer holds at $100\,^\circ\text{C}$ until the last of the liquid has vaporized; only steam heated in a closed system could then exceed $100\,^\circ\text{C}$.

**The water does not get hotter while boiling; the added energy raises PE (breaking IMFs to make gas), not KE (temperature).**

</details>

### Problem 9

Using water's phase diagram (triple point $0.006\ \text{atm}$, $0.01\,^\circ\text{C}$): describe what happens to a sample of ice held at $-5\,^\circ\text{C}$ as the pressure is lowered from $1\ \text{atm}$ down to $0.001\ \text{atm}$ at constant temperature.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Start at $1\ \text{atm}$, $-5\,^\circ\text{C}$: below $0\,^\circ\text{C}$, so **solid** (ice).

Lower the pressure (move straight down the diagram). Since the temperature ($-5\,^\circ\text{C}$) is below the triple-point temperature, dropping the pressure eventually crosses the **sublimation curve** (solid–gas border) when the pressure falls below $\sim0.004\ \text{atm}$.

At $0.001\ \text{atm}$, well below the triple point, the sample is a **gas** — the ice has **sublimed** directly to vapor without ever melting.

**The ice sublimes directly to water vapor: lowering the pressure below the sublimation curve takes solid straight to gas.**

</details>

### Problem 10

Ammonia's triple point is at $0.06\ \text{atm}$ and $-78\,^\circ\text{C}$, well below $1\ \text{atm}$. Does solid ammonia melt or sublime at $1\ \text{atm}$? Explain using the triple point.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Because $1\ \text{atm}$ is *above* ammonia's triple-point pressure ($0.06\ \text{atm}$), a constant-pressure heating line at $1\ \text{atm}$ passes *above* the triple point and crosses the solid–liquid (melting) curve first, then the liquid–gas curve.

So solid ammonia **melts** to liquid, then boils to gas — the normal, water-like sequence — rather than subliming.

**It melts (then boils): $1\ \text{atm}$ is above ammonia's triple-point pressure, so the heating path crosses the melting curve into the liquid region.** (Contrast with $\ce{CO2}$, whose triple point is above $1\ \text{atm}$, so it sublimes.)

</details>

### Problem 11

Two identical $50.\ \text{g}$ blocks of ice at $0\,^\circ\text{C}$ are placed on your skin. One stays solid ice; the other has already melted to $0\,^\circ\text{C}$ water. Which cools your skin more as it warms to body temperature, and why?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The **ice** absorbs more heat from your skin. To reach body temperature it must first *melt* — absorbing $q = (50.)(334) = 16{,}700\ \text{J}$ of latent heat at $0\,^\circ\text{C}$ — and *then* warm as liquid.

The $0\,^\circ\text{C}$ water skips the melting step and only warms up. So over the same temperature rise, the ice pulls far more heat out of your skin, thanks to its heat of fusion.

**The ice cools more, because it must absorb the heat of fusion ($\approx16.7\ \text{kJ}$) to melt before it can warm up.**

</details>

### Problem 12

Explain why steam at $100\,^\circ\text{C}$ causes a far worse burn than liquid water at $100\,^\circ\text{C}$, even though both are at the same temperature.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Same temperature means the same starting KE, but steam carries an enormous extra store of **potential energy**. When steam touches your skin it first **condenses**, releasing $\Delta H_\text{vap} = 2260\ \text{J/g}$ — energy dumped straight into you *before* the resulting hot water even begins to cool.

Liquid water at $100\,^\circ\text{C}$ only releases the sensible heat as it cools; it never delivers that huge condensation payload.

**Steam releases $\sim2260\ \text{J/g}$ of latent heat on condensing (in addition to cooling), so it delivers far more energy to your skin than same-temperature liquid water.**

</details>

### Problem 13

For $18.0\ \text{g}$ (1.00 mol) of water: (a) how much heat is required to melt it at $0\,^\circ\text{C}$, and (b) how much to boil it at $100\,^\circ\text{C}$? Express each in kJ/mol and comment on the ratio. ($\Delta H_\text{fus} = 334\ \text{J/g}$; $\Delta H_\text{vap} = 2260\ \text{J/g}$)

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

(a) Melting: $q = (18.0)(334) = 6012\ \text{J} \approx 6.01\ \text{kJ/mol}$

(b) Boiling: $q = (18.0)(2260) = 40{,}680\ \text{J} \approx 40.7\ \text{kJ/mol}$

Ratio: $40.7 / 6.01 \approx 6.8$. Boiling costs nearly 7 times more per mole because it breaks essentially all IMFs (full separation) rather than just loosening the lattice.

**(a) $6.01\ \text{kJ/mol}$; (b) $40.7\ \text{kJ/mol}$; vaporization is ~7× larger because full separation costs far more than loosening.**

</details>

### Problem 14

A pressure cooker seals steam inside so the internal pressure rises above $1\ \text{atm}$. Using boiling-point reasoning, explain why this cooks food faster than an open pot.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Water boils when its vapor pressure equals the surrounding pressure. In a sealed pressure cooker the internal pressure climbs above $1\ \text{atm}$, so the water must reach a **higher** vapor pressure — and therefore a **higher temperature** — before it boils (often ~$120\,^\circ\text{C}$).

On the phase diagram, raising the pressure moves the boiling point *up* the vaporization curve. Hotter water and steam transfer heat to food faster, so it cooks more quickly. (This is the exact reverse of the mountain case, where lower pressure lowers the boiling point.)

**Higher internal pressure raises the boiling point (up the vaporization curve), so the water is hotter than $100\,^\circ\text{C}$ and cooks food faster.**

</details>

### Problem 15

Sketch (describe) the heating curve for a substance, and identify for each of the five segments whether kinetic energy or potential energy is increasing.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Left to right, temperature vs. heat added:

1. **Solid warming** (slope up): KE increasing.
2. **Melting** (flat plateau): PE increasing (KE constant).
3. **Liquid warming** (slope up): KE increasing.
4. **Boiling** (flat plateau, longer): PE increasing (KE constant).
5. **Gas warming** (slope up): KE increasing.

**Sloped segments → KE increases (temperature rises); flat plateaus → PE increases (IMFs break, temperature constant).**

</details>

## Quick Reference

**The three states**

| | Solid | Liquid | Gas |
|---|---|---|---|
| Packing | Locked lattice | Close, sliding | Far apart, free |
| Shape / volume | Fixed / fixed | Container / fixed | Container / container |
| Compressible? | No | Barely | Yes |
| Energy | Lowest | Middle | Highest |

**The six phase changes**

| Change | Direction | Energy |
|---|---|---|
| Melting (fusion) | s $\to$ l | Absorbs (endo) |
| Freezing | l $\to$ s | Releases (exo) |
| Vaporization | l $\to$ g | Absorbs (endo) |
| Condensation | g $\to$ l | Releases (exo) |
| Sublimation | s $\to$ g | Absorbs (endo) |
| Deposition | g $\to$ s | Releases (exo) |

Up the ladder (toward gas) = **endothermic**; down = **exothermic**.

**Heat calculations**

| Segment type | Formula | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Slope (temp changes) | $q = mc\Delta T$ | Sensible heat — KE changing |
| Plateau (temp constant) | $q = m\Delta H$ (or $n\Delta H$) | Latent heat — PE changing |

Water constants: $c_\text{ice}=2.09$, $c_\text{water}=4.18$, $c_\text{steam}=2.01\ \text{J/(g}\cdot^\circ\text{C)}$; $\Delta H_\text{fus}=334\ \text{J/g}$ ($6.01\ \text{kJ/mol}$); $\Delta H_\text{vap}=2260\ \text{J/g}$ ($40.7\ \text{kJ/mol}$).

**Heating curve:** sloped = one phase warming (KE $\uparrow$); flat plateau = phase change (PE $\uparrow$, KE constant). Boiling plateau longer than melting because $\Delta H_\text{vap} \gg \Delta H_\text{fus}$.

**Phase diagram:** $P$ (y) vs. $T$ (x); three regions (solid/liquid/gas), three curves (melting/vaporization/sublimation), **triple point** (all three coexist), **critical point** (liquid/gas merge). Water's solid–liquid line has a **negative** slope (ice less dense than water $\to$ ice floats); $\ce{CO2}$'s is positive and its triple point is above $1\ \text{atm}$ (so dry ice sublimes). Boiling point falls as external pressure falls (mountain) and rises as pressure rises (pressure cooker).
