# History of Chemistry
<p class="article-date">July 4, 2026</p>

*The first time I watched Knives Out, I got the ending completely wrong — I was sure it was the nephew. On rewatch, though, I realized the movie played fair: every clue was on screen the whole time, and even my wrong suspect wasn't wasted, because ruling him out was exactly what narrowed the case. That's what the best mystery movies do — and the truly great detectives go one step further: before they find the missing piece, they can already tell you what it must look like. Hold that thought, because it's the entire history of chemistry in miniature. Chemistry's backstory is a 2,000-year detective case, where each generation inherited the previous one's case file — the alchemists' distillation flasks, Boyle's pressure data, Lavoisier's obsessively balanced scales — and either kept the evidence, threw out the bad theory, or both. And the greatest detective move of all? Dmitri Mendeleev literally left gaps in his periodic table, like an investigator predicting exactly where and when the next suspect would appear — and then gallium walked through the door matching his description almost perfectly. This article is the case file. It's also the secret backstory of AP Chemistry Unit 1, because the exam doesn't just ask you what the atom is — it asks you how we* know.

## The Core Concept

### Intuition First

Keep the detective-case picture in your head, because every section below follows the same loop:

1. **A suspect theory** explains the evidence so far.
2. **A new measurement** shows up that the theory can't explain.
3. **A better theory** replaces it — but the *evidence and techniques* carry forward.

That last part is the point most textbooks skip. Science doesn't restart from zero when a theory dies. The case file — the burettes, the balances, the data tables — gets handed to the next detective. Phlogiston died; the combustion experiments that killed it became the foundation of modern chemistry. The plum pudding model died; Thomson's cathode ray tubes became the ancestors of every mass spectrometer on the AP exam.

Just like a good mystery movie: wrong suspect, but the evidence board survived — every ruled-out lead stays pinned up, still narrowing the case.

So as you read, track two things for each era: **what they got right** (usually technique and data) and **what they got wrong** (usually the invisible mechanism they guessed at). By the end you'll see the pattern the AP exam loves: *a model is only as good as the evidence it explains, and one clean experiment can overturn a century of consensus.*

## Case File #1: Alchemy — Wrong Theory, Real Lab Skills (~300 BCE – 1600s)

The alchemists wanted two things: turn cheap metals into gold, and find an elixir of immortality. They achieved neither, obviously. Their theory of matter — everything is a blend of four "elements" (earth, air, fire, water) plus mystical "principles" — was wrong in essentially every detail.

**What they got wrong:** the entire theoretical framework. Transmutation of lead into gold by chemical means is impossible (you'd have to change the nucleus, which no flask can do). Their explanations were secretive, symbolic, and untestable on purpose.

**What they got right:** *everything in the lab.* Alchemists invented or refined distillation, crystallization, sublimation, and filtration. They discovered real substances — mineral acids like sulfuric acid ($\ce{H2SO4}$), phosphorus (isolated by Hennig Brand in 1669, from an experiment I will politely just call "boiling a lot of urine"), and countless salts and alloys. Every separation technique in your AP lab manual has an alchemist's fingerprints on it.

### Phlogiston: A Wrong Idea That Was Actually Good Science

By the 1700s, alchemy's heir was **phlogiston theory** — and it deserves respect, because unlike the four elements, it was *testable*.

The claim: every flammable substance contains an invisible fire-stuff called **phlogiston**. Burning releases it. Wood burns to ash because the phlogiston escapes; a candle in a sealed jar goes out because the air becomes "saturated" with phlogiston and can't absorb more.

Notice this explains a lot! It unified combustion, rusting, and respiration under one idea. That's what a good scientific model does.

But it made a prediction: if burning *releases* something, the leftover material should weigh **less**. And when careful chemists burned metals (making a powdery "calx" — what we'd call a metal oxide), the calx weighed **more** than the metal. Defenders tried to patch the theory by proposing phlogiston had *negative mass* — and when your suspect theory needs negative-mass ghost matter to survive, a good detective starts looking for a new suspect.

**The lesson:** phlogiston wasn't stupid. It was a wrong-but-testable idea, and testability is what let it be killed by data. That is the difference between alchemy and chemistry.

## Case File #2: Boyle — Chemistry Becomes an Experimental Science (1661)

Robert Boyle's book *The Sceptical Chymist* (1661) is roughly the moment chemistry gets its detective's badge. Boyle's radical proposals:

- **Stop trusting ancient authority.** Aristotle's four elements aren't evidence; they're just old. Do the experiment.
- **Redefine "element"** as a substance that cannot be broken down into simpler substances — an *operational* definition you can test in a lab, not a philosophical guess.
- **Publish your methods** so others can repeat them. (The alchemists encrypted theirs.)

Boyle backed the talk with data: his experiments with a vacuum pump gave us **Boyle's Law** — at constant temperature, the pressure and volume of a gas are inversely proportional:

$$P_1V_1 = P_2V_2$$

You'll use this in Unit 3, but its historical importance is bigger than the equation: it showed that matter obeys *precise mathematical laws you can discover by measuring*.

**What he got right:** the method — skepticism, experiment, replication, quantitative laws.
**What he got wrong:** he still couldn't say what an element actually *was* made of, and he didn't fully escape alchemical ideas himself (he genuinely believed transmutation might work). The badge was new; the detective was still learning.

## Case File #3: Lavoisier — The Balance Never Lies (1770s–1789)

Antoine Lavoisier's superpower was almost comically simple: **he weighed everything, obsessively, before and after.** His balances were the most precise instruments of his era, and he treated mass measurement the way a detective bags and labels every piece of evidence — if it wasn't weighed and logged, it doesn't exist to the case.

His signature experiment: heat mercury calx (mercury(II) oxide, the red powder) in a sealed system and *track every gram*:

$$\ce{2HgO ->[\Delta] 2Hg + O2}$$

The calx lost exactly as much mass as the gas gained. Run it in reverse — burn mercury in air — and the metal *gains* exactly the mass the air *loses*:

$$\ce{2Hg + O2 -> 2HgO}$$

Two conclusions detonated out of this:

1. **Conservation of mass.** In a chemical reaction, matter is neither created nor destroyed — only rearranged. Total mass in = total mass out. This is the law that makes every balanced equation and every stoichiometry problem you'll ever do possible.
2. **Combustion is combination with a gas** — a real, weighable substance Lavoisier named **oxygen** — not the release of phlogiston. The mysterious mass *gain* of burning metals wasn't negative-mass ghost fluid; it was oxygen from the air being captured. Case closed. Phlogiston dead.

Lavoisier also co-created modern chemical naming (why we say "hydrogen" and "oxygen" instead of "inflammable air" and "dephlogisticated air") and published the first modern chemistry textbook, *Traité élémentaire de chimie* (1789), with a list of 33 elements.

**What he got right:** conservation of mass, the true nature of combustion, the naming system, the measurement-first culture.
**What he got wrong:** some of his 33 "elements" weren't elements (he listed light and "caloric" — heat fluid — among them). Even the best detectives leave a few open cases. Tragically, the French Revolution guillotined him in 1794; the mathematician Lagrange said it took "only a moment to cut off that head, and a hundred years may not produce another like it."

## Case File #4: Dalton — The Atom Gets a Job Description (1803–1808)

John Dalton, a Manchester schoolteacher, took Lavoisier's mass data plus the observation that elements combine in fixed ratios (e.g., water is always the same proportion of hydrogen to oxygen by mass) and asked: *what must matter be like for this to be true?* His answer — **atomic theory** — came in a set of postulates:

1. **All matter is made of tiny indivisible particles called atoms.**
2. **All atoms of a given element are identical**, especially in mass; atoms of different elements have different masses.
3. **Atoms cannot be created, destroyed, or divided** in chemical reactions.
4. **Atoms combine in small whole-number ratios to form compounds** (one atom of A with one, two, or three of B — never 1.5).
5. **Chemical reactions are just rearrangements of atoms** — the atoms themselves are unchanged.

The scorecard, with two centuries of hindsight:

| Postulate | Verdict | Why |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Atoms are indivisible | **Corrected** | Atoms contain electrons, protons, and neutrons — and nuclear reactions *can* split them (chemical reactions can't, so Dalton was right for chemistry's purposes) |
| 2. All atoms of an element are identical | **Corrected** | Isotopes: $\ce{^{12}_{6}C}$ and $\ce{^{13}_{6}C}$ are both carbon but have different masses |
| 3. Atoms not created/destroyed in reactions | **Survived** (for chemical reactions) | This is conservation of mass at the atomic level |
| 4. Whole-number combining ratios | **Survived** | $\ce{H2O}$, $\ce{CO2}$, $\ce{NH3}$ — this is why chemical formulas have subscripts, not decimals |
| 5. Reactions rearrange atoms | **Survived** | The entire logic of balancing equations rests on this |

Dalton's exact numbers were often wrong (he thought water was $\ce{HO}$, which scrambled his atomic masses), but the framework was so right that everything since — including this entire course — is built on it.

## Case File #5: Mendeleev — Leaving Gaps Like a Detective (1869)

By the 1860s, chemists knew about 63 elements — a pile of suspects with no org chart. Dmitri Mendeleev, writing a textbook in St. Petersburg, made cards for each element with its mass and properties and arranged them (the story says like solitaire) until a pattern snapped into place: **ordered by atomic mass, chemical properties repeat periodically.**

Here's the move that makes him a legend. Where the pattern demanded an element that didn't exist yet, Mendeleev **left a gap and refused to force a known element into the wrong spot** — even swapping a couple of elements out of strict mass order when their chemistry demanded it. Then he did the truly audacious thing: he *published predictions* for the missing elements' properties, naming them with the Sanskrit prefix *eka-* ("one beyond").

His **eka-aluminum** (the gap below aluminum) was predicted to have an atomic mass near 68, a density around 5.9 g/cm³, a low melting point, and an oxide of formula $\ce{Ea2O3}$.

Six years later, in 1875, the French chemist Lecoq de Boisbaudran discovered **gallium**: atomic mass 69.7, density 5.91 g/cm³, melting point 29.8 °C (it literally melts in your hand — the low melting point Mendeleev called), oxide $\ce{Ga2O3}$. De Boisbaudran initially measured the density as 4.7; Mendeleev wrote to him suggesting his *sample* was impure. Mendeleev was right — about an element he had never seen. Eka-boron (scandium, 1879) and eka-silicon (germanium, 1886) followed, each matching the predictions.

**What he got right:** periodicity, the courage to leave gaps, prediction as the ultimate test of a model.
**What he got wrong:** the ordering principle. Mendeleev sorted by atomic *mass*; the modern table sorts by atomic *number* (proton count), which Henry Moseley established in 1913 using X-ray spectra — resolving exactly those awkward spots where Mendeleev had to swap elements by hand. The pattern was right; the reason for the pattern needed the next case file.

## Case File #6: Inside the Atom — Four Experiments, Four Models (1897–1926)

Dalton's atom was a featureless billiard ball. In thirty years, four rounds of evidence cracked it open. Each paragraph below is one experiment and the model it forced.

**Thomson and cathode rays (1897).** J.J. Thomson studied the glowing beam inside a cathode ray tube and showed it bent toward a positive plate — the beam was made of *negative particles* — and that these particles were the same no matter which metal emitted them, with a mass nearly 2,000 times smaller than a hydrogen atom. Atoms contain smaller pieces: the **electron**. Postulate 1, corrected. Thomson's model: the **plum pudding** — a diffuse ball of positive charge with electrons dotted through it like boba pearls in milk tea.

**Rutherford and the gold foil (1909–1911).** Rutherford's team (Geiger and Marsden) fired alpha particles ($\ce{^{4}_{2}He^2+}$ nuclei) at gold foil a few hundred atoms thick. Plum pudding predicted they'd all sail through with tiny deflections. Most did — but about 1 in 8,000 bounced back at huge angles. Rutherford: "as if you fired a 15-inch shell at a piece of tissue paper and it came back and hit you." The only explanation: the atom's positive charge and nearly all its mass are crammed into a tiny, dense **nucleus**, with electrons in mostly empty space around it. This is *the* evidence-to-model chain the AP exam loves to ask about.

**Bohr and the hydrogen spectrum (1913).** Rutherford's atom had a fatal flaw: classical physics says an orbiting electron should radiate energy and spiral into the nucleus in nanoseconds. Also, glowing hydrogen doesn't emit a rainbow — it emits only specific, sharp lines of color. Niels Bohr proposed that electrons occupy only certain allowed energy levels, and the spectral lines are photons emitted when an electron drops from one level to another: $\Delta E = h\nu$. Discrete lines exist *because* energy levels are discrete. It nailed hydrogen's spectrum to the decimal point... and failed for every atom with more than one electron.

**Schrödinger and the quantum model (1926).** The fix required abandoning orbits entirely. Electrons behave as waves, and Schrödinger's equation describes them as **orbitals** — three-dimensional probability clouds where an electron is likely to be found, organized into shells and subshells ($s$, $p$, $d$, $f$). This is the model you'll actually use in Unit 1, and its modern evidence includes **photoelectron spectroscopy (PES)** — an AP-favorite technique that measures the energy needed to remove electrons and directly confirms the shell structure.

Notice the loop ran four times in thirty years: model → prediction → experiment breaks it → better model. The case never fully closes; the file just gets thicker and more accurate.

## The Timeline: One Table to Rule the Case File

| Year | Detective | Contribution | What It Fixed |
|---|---|---|---|
| ~300 BCE – 1600s | Alchemists | Distillation, crystallization, acids, real substances | Nothing theoretical — but built the lab toolkit everyone after them used |
| 1661 | Robert Boyle | Experimental method; operational definition of element; $P_1V_1 = P_2V_2$ | Replaced ancient authority with measurement |
| 1770s–1789 | Antoine Lavoisier | Conservation of mass; oxygen theory of combustion ($\ce{2HgO -> 2Hg + O2}$); chemical naming | Killed phlogiston; made mass bookkeeping the law |
| 1803–1808 | John Dalton | Atomic theory postulates | Explained *why* mass is conserved and *why* compounds have fixed ratios |
| 1869 | Dmitri Mendeleev | Periodic table with predictive gaps (eka-aluminum → gallium, 1875) | Organized 63 elements and predicted unknown ones |
| 1897 | J.J. Thomson | Cathode rays → the electron; plum pudding model | Corrected "atoms are indivisible" |
| 1909–1911 | Ernest Rutherford | Gold foil → the nucleus | Replaced plum pudding with the nuclear atom |
| 1913 | Niels Bohr | Hydrogen spectrum → quantized energy levels | Explained why atoms don't collapse and why spectra are lines |
| 1913 | Henry Moseley | X-ray spectra → atomic number | Fixed Mendeleev's ordering (number, not mass) |
| 1926 | Erwin Schrödinger | Quantum model → orbitals | Explained multi-electron atoms; the model AP Chem uses today |

## Why This Matters for the AP Exam

Here's the practical payoff. The AP Chemistry exam does not ask you to memorize dates. It tests **models-and-evidence reasoning** — the exact detective skill this whole article has been practicing:

- **"What evidence supports the nuclear model of the atom?"** You need the gold foil logic: most alpha particles passed through (atom is mostly empty space), a few bounced back (tiny, dense, positive nucleus). Expect this framed as data, not history.
- **"Which of Dalton's postulates is contradicted by the existence of isotopes?"** You need to know the postulates *and* which evidence corrected which one — exactly the scorecard table above.
- **Conservation of mass is the silent assumption behind every stoichiometry and balancing question** in Units 1 and 4. When you balance $\ce{2Hg + O2 -> 2HgO}$, you're doing Lavoisier's bookkeeping.
- **Periodicity questions** (Unit 1: trends in radius, ionization energy, electronegativity) are Mendeleev's pattern, now explained by electron structure. And **PES questions** are the modern version of "what evidence supports this model?" — spectra as fingerprints of shells, the direct descendant of Bohr reading hydrogen's lines.

In other words: this article is the backstory of **Unit 1**. Every model you're about to learn — shells, subshells, the nucleus, the periodic law — earned its place by surviving an experiment designed to kill it. When the exam hands you data and asks "what does this support or refute," you're being asked to be the next detective on the case.

## Reflection Questions

### Question 1
Phlogiston theory predicted that a metal should *lose* mass when burned. What was actually observed, and why does this single measurement matter more than a century of phlogiston's successful explanations?

<details>
<summary><strong>Answer</strong></summary>

Metals *gain* mass when burned (the calx weighs more than the original metal). This matters because a scientific model must survive **every** test, not just most of them — one reproducible contradiction is fatal. Defenders proposed negative-mass phlogiston to patch it, but an unfalsifiable patch is a sign the model is dead. Lavoisier's oxygen theory explained the mass gain directly: the metal combines with oxygen from the air, as in $\ce{2Hg + O2 -> 2HgO}$, so the "extra" mass is the captured oxygen. **A model's worth is decided by the evidence it can't explain, not the evidence it can.**

</details>

### Question 2
Two of Dalton's five postulates were later corrected. Which two, what evidence corrected each, and why is Dalton's theory still considered a triumph?

<details>
<summary><strong>Answer</strong></summary>

**Postulate 1 (atoms are indivisible):** corrected by Thomson's cathode ray experiments (1897), which showed atoms contain electrons — smaller charged pieces. **Postulate 2 (all atoms of an element are identical):** corrected by the discovery of isotopes — e.g., $\ce{^{12}_{6}C}$ and $\ce{^{13}_{6}C}$ are both carbon with different masses. The theory remains a triumph because the surviving postulates (whole-number combining ratios, reactions as rearrangement, no creation/destruction of atoms in chemical change) are the foundation of chemical formulas, balanced equations, and stoichiometry. **A model can be corrected in detail and still be right in structure.**

</details>

### Question 3
Explain why Mendeleev's decision to leave gaps in his table was scientifically braver — and more useful — than filling every slot with a known element.

<details>
<summary><strong>Answer</strong></summary>

Filling every slot would have preserved a tidy table at the cost of the pattern's integrity. Leaving gaps turned the table into a **predictive instrument**: each gap was a falsifiable claim ("an element with these specific properties exists and will be found"). His eka-aluminum prediction — mass ≈ 68, density ≈ 5.9 g/cm³, low melting point, oxide $\ce{Ea2O3}$ — was matched almost exactly by gallium in 1875 (mass 69.7, density 5.91, melts at 29.8 °C, oxide $\ce{Ga2O3}$). Successful *prediction* is far stronger evidence than successful *explanation*, because the answer wasn't known in advance. **The gaps were the theory sticking its neck out — and being right.**

</details>

### Question 4
In the gold foil experiment, most alpha particles passed straight through, but about 1 in 8,000 deflected at large angles. State what each observation implies about atomic structure.

<details>
<summary><strong>Answer</strong></summary>

**Most pass through undeflected** → the atom is mostly empty space; there is no diffuse ball of positive "pudding" filling its volume. **A few bounce back at large angles** → all the positive charge and nearly all the mass must be concentrated in an extremely small, dense region — the nucleus — because only a compact, massive, like-charged target can reverse a fast alpha particle ($\ce{^{4}_{2}He^2+}$). Together they force the **nuclear model**: tiny positive nucleus, electrons in the vast empty space around it. This observation-to-inference pairing is a classic AP question format.

</details>

### Question 5
Why did the hydrogen emission spectrum — sharp, separate lines instead of a continuous rainbow — demand a new model of the electron?

<details>
<summary><strong>Answer</strong></summary>

A continuous rainbow would mean electrons can have *any* energy and lose it in *any* amount. Sharp lines mean atoms emit only specific photon energies, so electrons must occupy only specific allowed energy levels; each line is a jump between two levels ($\Delta E = h\nu$). Rutherford's model had no such levels — worse, classical physics predicted his orbiting electrons would spiral into the nucleus. Bohr's quantized levels explained both the atom's stability and the exact line positions for hydrogen. Its failure for multi-electron atoms then drove the next correction: Schrödinger's quantum model with orbitals. **Spectra are the atom's fingerprint, and fingerprints are evidence.**

</details>

### Question 6
The alchemists' theory was wrong, yet historians call them essential to chemistry. Using one concrete example, defend the claim that "wrong theories can still produce lasting value."

<details>
<summary><strong>Answer</strong></summary>

Example: alchemists pursuing transmutation developed **distillation** and discovered mineral acids such as $\ce{H2SO4}$ — techniques and substances still central to chemistry (and to your AP lab bench) centuries after the theory motivating them died. The theory answered *why* they worked; the techniques answered *how* — and the how survived. The same pattern repeats throughout the case file: phlogiston's combustion experiments became Lavoisier's data; Thomson's cathode ray tubes became modern spectrometers. **Theories are disposable; carefully gathered evidence and technique are inherited by the next generation of detectives.**

</details>
