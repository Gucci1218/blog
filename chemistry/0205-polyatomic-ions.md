# Polyatomic Ions
<p class="article-date">July 4, 2026</p>

*Think about the Avengers for a second. Inside the team there's a whole web of structure — members, relationships, drama, who answers to whom — but the rest of the movie universe never deals with any of that. To everyone else they're just "the Avengers": one name, one reputation, one unit that shows up and acts together. And the same team keeps appearing across totally different movies, behaving exactly the same way every time. Chemistry has the exact same idea: a **polyatomic ion** is a team of atoms covalently bonded together on the inside, so stable that it travels through reactions as one charged unit. The internal relationships are the covalent bonds, the single name-and-reputation is the ion's formula and overall charge, and the team showing up in movie after movie is the same ion appearing in hundreds of different compounds — $\ce{NO3-}$ behaves identically whether it's cast alongside sodium, calcium, or ammonium. Master about twenty of these teams and their patterns, and you unlock naming, formula writing, and the net-ionic-equation FRQs that AP Chemistry loves. Skip them, and Unit 4 is unplayable.*

## What You'll Learn

- Define a polyatomic ion and explain how one compound can contain **both** covalent and ionic bonds

- Extend the pattern with **per-...-ate** and **hypo-...-ite** to decode the entire chlorine oxyanion family

- Write correct formulas for compounds containing polyatomic ions, including when parentheses are required and when they are not

## The Core Concept

### Intuition First

Back to the superhero team-up. Think about what makes a team like the Avengers work as a movie idea:

1. **It has internal structure.** The roster is held together by real relationships — alliances, history, who trusts whom. Those bonds are strong: the team doesn't randomly lose a member walking from one scene to the next.
2. **The outside world sees one unit.** The rest of the movie universe doesn't track the internal drama. It deals with one name and one reputation: "the Avengers just showed up."
3. **It's reusable everywhere.** The same team appears across totally different movies and behaves the same way in every one. Learn the team once, recognize it a hundred times.

Now translate each piece into chemistry:

| Superhero team | Polyatomic ion |
|---|---|
| The roster and the internal relationships holding it together | **Covalent bonds** holding the atoms together (e.g., the $\ce{S-O}$ bonds inside $\ce{SO4^2-}$) |
| The single name and reputation the world sees | The ion's **formula and overall charge** (the $2-$ on $\ce{SO4^2-}$) |
| The same team showing up in different movies | The same ion appearing in hundreds of compounds: $\ce{Na2SO4}$, $\ce{CaSO4}$, $\ce{(NH4)2SO4}$... |
| The team never disbanding mid-movie | The ion staying **intact** through dissolving, precipitation, and most reactions |

Here's the punchline example. Ammonium nitrate, $\ce{NH4NO3}$, is a single compound that contains **both bond types at once**:

- **Inside** $\ce{NH4+}$: four $\ce{N-H}$ covalent bonds (shared electrons)
- **Inside** $\ce{NO3-}$: covalent $\ce{N-O}$ bonds
- **Between** the two ions: an ionic attraction, because $\ce{NH4+}$ carries $1+$ and $\ce{NO3-}$ carries $1-$

Covalent bonds *inside* each team, ionic behavior *outside* between teams. When $\ce{NH4NO3}$ dissolves in water, it doesn't shatter into nitrogen, hydrogen, and oxygen atoms — it splits cleanly at the ionic "seam" into intact $\ce{NH4+}$ and $\ce{NO3-}$ teams:

$$\ce{NH4NO3(s) -> NH4+(aq) + NO3-(aq)}$$

The teams survive. That's the whole concept.

### The Master List

These are the polyatomic ions AP Chemistry expects you to know cold. The exam's periodic table will **not** help you here — this table has to live in your head.

| Name | Formula | Charge |
|---|---|---|
| Ammonium | $\ce{NH4+}$ | $1+$ |
| Hydroxide | $\ce{OH-}$ | $1-$ |
| Cyanide | $\ce{CN-}$ | $1-$ |
| Acetate | $\ce{C2H3O2-}$ | $1-$ |
| Nitrate | $\ce{NO3-}$ | $1-$ |
| Nitrite | $\ce{NO2-}$ | $1-$ |
| Permanganate | $\ce{MnO4-}$ | $1-$ |
| Sulfate | $\ce{SO4^2-}$ | $2-$ |
| Sulfite | $\ce{SO3^2-}$ | $2-$ |
| Carbonate | $\ce{CO3^2-}$ | $2-$ |
| Bicarbonate (hydrogen carbonate) | $\ce{HCO3-}$ | $1-$ |
| Chromate | $\ce{CrO4^2-}$ | $2-$ |
| Dichromate | $\ce{Cr2O7^2-}$ | $2-$ |
| Peroxide | $\ce{O2^2-}$ | $2-$ |
| Phosphate | $\ce{PO4^3-}$ | $3-$ |

Three observations before you panic about memorizing fifteen things:

- **Only one common polyatomic cation exists**: ammonium, $\ce{NH4+}$. Everything else is an anion. If you see a polyatomic ion, it's negative unless it's ammonium.
- Most of these are **oxyanions** — a central atom surrounded by oxygens. Oxyanions come in families, and families follow patterns.
- Once you know the patterns below, the *actual* memorization load drops to about seven "anchor" ions. The rest are derived.

### Pattern 1: -ate vs -ite (One Fewer Oxygen, SAME Charge)

This is the single highest-value pattern. For every **-ate** ion you memorize, the **-ite** version is free:

$$\text{-ite} = \text{-ate} - \text{one oxygen}, \quad \textbf{charge unchanged}$$

| -ate (anchor) | -ite (derived) |
|---|---|
| Nitrate $\ce{NO3-}$ | Nitrite $\ce{NO2-}$ |
| Sulfate $\ce{SO4^2-}$ | Sulfite $\ce{SO3^2-}$ |
| Chlorate $\ce{ClO3-}$ | Chlorite $\ce{ClO2-}$ |
| Phosphate $\ce{PO4^3-}$ | Phosphite $\ce{PO3^3-}$ |

The mistake everyone makes at first: assuming the charge changes when the oxygen count changes. It doesn't. Sulfite is $\ce{SO3^2-}$, not $\ce{SO3-}$. The charge is part of the family name, not the oxygen count. In movie terms: one Avenger fewer on the roster doesn't change the name on the poster — sulfite keeps the family charge.

### Pattern 2: per-...-ate and hypo-...-ite (The Full Oxygen Ladder)

Some elements — chlorine is the showcase — form a **four-rung ladder** of oxyanions. Same charge on every rung; only the oxygen count changes:

| Name | Formula | Oxygens | Pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Per**chlor**ate** | $\ce{ClO4-}$ | 4 | one *more* O than -ate |
| Chlor**ate** | $\ce{ClO3-}$ | 3 | the anchor |
| Chlor**ite** | $\ce{ClO2-}$ | 2 | one fewer O than -ate |
| **Hypo**chlor**ite** | $\ce{ClO-}$ | 1 | one fewer O than -ite |

Memorize **one rung** (chlorate, $\ce{ClO3-}$) and the prefixes generate the other three. The word roots even help: *per-* as in "hyper" (above, more), *hypo-* as in "hypothermia" (below, less).

Bonus: bromine and iodine sit in chlorine's group on the periodic table, so they copy the whole ladder. Bromate is $\ce{BrO3-}$, iodate is $\ce{IO3-}$, hypobromite is $\ce{BrO-}$ — no new memorization required. That's the periodic table doing your homework for you.

### Pattern 3: Adding H Reduces the Charge Magnitude by One

Take any multiply-charged oxyanion and attach a hydrogen ion ($\ce{H+}$, charge $1+$) to it. The math is just addition:

$$(3-) + (1+) = 2-, \qquad (2-) + (1+) = 1-$$

The phosphate family shows the full sequence:

$$\ce{PO4^3-} \xrightarrow{+\ce{H+}} \ce{HPO4^2-} \xrightarrow{+\ce{H+}} \ce{H2PO4-}$$

| Ion | Name | Charge |
|---|---|---|
| $\ce{PO4^3-}$ | Phosphate | $3-$ |
| $\ce{HPO4^2-}$ | Hydrogen phosphate | $2-$ |
| $\ce{H2PO4-}$ | Dihydrogen phosphate | $1-$ |

Same trick explains bicarbonate: carbonate $\ce{CO3^2-}$ plus one $\ce{H+}$ gives $\ce{HCO3-}$, formally called **hydrogen carbonate** but almost always nicknamed **bicarbonate**. Sulfate works too: $\ce{HSO4-}$ is hydrogen sulfate (bisulfate).

### Pattern 4: The -ate Column Trick

Lay the anchor -ate ions on a skeleton periodic table and a shape appears:

| | Oxygens in -ate | Charge | Ions |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Second-row elements** (N, C) | **3** | matches "missing bonds" | $\ce{NO3-}$, $\ce{CO3^2-}$ |
| **Third-row elements** (S, P, Cl) | **4** | steps $2-$, $3-$, $1-$ | $\ce{SO4^2-}$, $\ce{PO4^3-}$, $\ce{ClO4-}$* |

*(Chlorine's 4-oxygen ion is *per*chlorate; chlorate itself has 3 — chlorine is the one rebel, which is exactly why the ladder in Pattern 2 uses it as the showcase.)*

The takeaway: **top-row -ates carry 3 oxygens, third-row -ates carry 4.** Small atoms like N and C can only fit three oxygens around themselves; bigger third-row atoms fit four. Two rules replace six flashcards.

### Writing Formulas: Parentheses and the Criss-Cross

To build a neutral compound, total positive charge must cancel total negative charge — same criss-cross method from [Naming Compounds](/chemistry/0204-naming-compounds), with one upgrade: **the polyatomic ion criss-crosses as a whole unit.** You never reach inside the team and change its internal subscripts.

**The parentheses rule:**

> Use parentheses when you need **more than one** of a polyatomic ion. One copy needs no parentheses.

**When parentheses are required:**

- Calcium nitrate: $\ce{Ca^2+}$ needs two $\ce{NO3-}$ → $\ce{Ca(NO3)2}$
  Writing $\ce{CaNO32}$ would be gibberish — it looks like 32 oxygens. The parentheses say "two complete nitrate teams."
- Ammonium sulfate: two $\ce{NH4+}$ per $\ce{SO4^2-}$ → $\ce{(NH4)2SO4}$

**When parentheses are wrong:**

- Sodium nitrate: one $\ce{Na+}$, one $\ce{NO3-}$ → $\ce{NaNO3}$, **not** $\ce{Na(NO3)}$. One team, no parentheses.
- Potassium permanganate: $\ce{KMnO4}$, not $\ce{K(MnO4)}$.

Think of parentheses as the brackets on the movie poster's billing: you only need them when the same team appears more than once in the credits.

### Naming Compounds That Contain Polyatomic Ions

Nothing new — the rules from [Naming Compounds](/chemistry/0204-naming-compounds) carry over directly:

1. **Cation first, anion second.**
2. Use the polyatomic ion's name **as-is** — no "-ide" ending gets forced onto it. $\ce{NaNO3}$ is sodium **nitrate**, not sodium nitride (that would be $\ce{Na3N}$, a totally different compound).
3. **Transition metals still need Roman numerals.** Work out the metal's charge from the polyatomic ion's known charge. In $\ce{Fe2(SO4)3}$: three sulfates contribute $3 \times (2-) = 6-$, so two irons must contribute $6+$, meaning each iron is $3+$ → iron(III) sulfate.
4. **No Greek prefixes.** "Calcium dinitrate" is wrong; the charges already dictate the ratio. Prefixes belong to molecular (covalent) compounds only.

### Memory Strategies That Actually Work

**Strategy 1 — Group by charge.** Don't memorize a random list; memorize three buckets:

- **$1-$ bucket:** hydroxide, cyanide, acetate, nitrate, permanganate, bicarbonate, (chlorate ladder)
- **$2-$ bucket:** sulfate, carbonate, chromate, dichromate, peroxide
- **$3-$ bucket:** phosphate (alone — easy)
- **$1+$ bucket:** ammonium (also alone)

**Strategy 2 — Anchors + patterns.** Memorize only the anchors (ammonium, hydroxide, cyanide, acetate, nitrate, sulfate, carbonate, phosphate, chlorate, chromate, permanganate, peroxide). Patterns 1–3 generate everything else: nitrite, sulfite, the whole chlorine ladder, bicarbonate, hydrogen phosphate...

**Strategy 3 — "Nick the Camel ate a Clam for Supper in Phoenix."** This sentence encodes five anchor -ate ions. In each capitalized word, **consonants = number of oxygens** and **vowels = magnitude of the charge**:

| Word | Consonants | Vowels | Ion |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Nick** | N, c, k → 3 O | i → $1-$ | Nitrate $\ce{NO3-}$ |
| **Camel** | C, m, l → 3 O | a, e → $2-$ | Carbonate $\ce{CO3^2-}$ |
| **Clam** | C, l, m → 3 O | a → $1-$ | Chlorate $\ce{ClO3-}$ |
| **Supper** | S, p, p, r → 4 O | u, e → $2-$ | Sulfate $\ce{SO4^2-}$ |
| **Phoenix** | P, h, n, x → 4 O | o, e, i → $3-$ | Phosphate $\ce{PO4^3-}$ |

(The first letter tells you the central element; all ions in the sentence are the -ate form.) I wrote this sentence on a sticky note on my monitor next to my movie ticket stubs, and it survived two weeks of quizzing myself at red lights on the way to Torrey Pines. It works.

**Strategy 4 — Flashcards, three ways per ion.** For each ion make three prompts: name → formula, formula → name, and "formula of the sodium/calcium compound" (forces you to practice the criss-cross). Shuffle daily for a week; after that, weekly maintenance is enough.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Two Bond Types in One Compound

**Problem:** Classify every bond in ammonium nitrate, $\ce{NH4NO3}$, as ionic or covalent.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Identify the teams. $\ce{NH4NO3}$ is built from $\ce{NH4+}$ (ammonium) and $\ce{NO3-}$ (nitrate).

**Step 2:** Look inside each team. Within $\ce{NH4+}$, nitrogen shares electrons with four hydrogens: four covalent $\ce{N-H}$ bonds. Within $\ce{NO3-}$, nitrogen shares electrons with three oxygens: covalent $\ce{N-O}$ bonds.

**Step 3:** Look between the teams. $\ce{NH4+}$ ($1+$) and $\ce{NO3-}$ ($1-$) attract electrostatically — that's an ionic bond.

**Answer:** **All bonds *inside* each ion are covalent; the bond *between* $\ce{NH4+}$ and $\ce{NO3-}$ is ionic.**

### Example 2: Naming a Simple Polyatomic Compound

**Problem:** Name $\ce{K2CO3}$.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Cation: $\ce{K+}$ is potassium (Group 1, always $1+$, no Roman numeral needed).

**Step 2:** Anion: $\ce{CO3^2-}$ is carbonate. Charge check: $2(1+) + (2-) = 0$. ✓

**Step 3:** Cation name + anion name, no prefixes.

**Answer:** **Potassium carbonate.**

### Example 3: Formula With Parentheses

**Problem:** Write the formula for calcium nitrate.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Identify the ions: $\ce{Ca^2+}$ and $\ce{NO3-}$.

**Step 2:** Balance the charges: one $2+$ needs two $1-$. Criss-cross the charge magnitudes: Ca gets subscript 1, the nitrate **unit** gets subscript 2.

**Step 3:** More than one polyatomic team → parentheses required.

**Answer:** **$\ce{Ca(NO3)2}$** — never $\ce{CaNO32}$, and never $\ce{CaN2O6}$ (don't dissolve the team into loose atoms).

### Example 4: Formula Without Parentheses

**Problem:** Write the formula for sodium acetate.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Ions: $\ce{Na+}$ and $\ce{C2H3O2-}$.

**Step 2:** Charges already balance one-to-one: $(1+) + (1-) = 0$.

**Step 3:** Only one acetate team — no parentheses.

**Answer:** **$\ce{NaC2H3O2}$** (not $\ce{Na(C2H3O2)}$).

### Example 5: Ammonium Compounds

**Problem:** Write the formula for ammonium sulfate.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Ions: $\ce{NH4+}$ and $\ce{SO4^2-}$.

**Step 2:** One $2-$ sulfate needs two $1+$ ammoniums.

**Step 3:** Two copies of the polyatomic *cation* → parentheses go around the ammonium: two teams, one pair of brackets.

**Answer:** **$\ce{(NH4)2SO4}$.**

### Example 6: The -ate/-ite Pattern

**Problem:** The formula for sodium sulfate is $\ce{Na2SO4}$. Without looking anything up, write the formula for sodium sulfite.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Recall Pattern 1: -ite = -ate minus one oxygen, **same charge**.

**Step 2:** Sulfate is $\ce{SO4^2-}$, so sulfite is $\ce{SO3^2-}$ — still $2-$.

**Step 3:** Same charge means the same criss-cross: two $\ce{Na+}$ per sulfite.

**Answer:** **$\ce{Na2SO3}$.** (The subscript on Na doesn't change, because the charge didn't.)

### Example 7: Climbing the Chlorine Ladder

**Problem:** Name these four potassium compounds: $\ce{KClO4}$, $\ce{KClO3}$, $\ce{KClO2}$, $\ce{KClO}$.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Each contains $\ce{K+}$ and a chlorine oxyanion with charge $1-$; only the oxygen count differs.

**Step 2:** Anchor at chlorate = 3 oxygens, then apply the ladder: 4 O → *per-...-ate*; 3 O → *-ate*; 2 O → *-ite*; 1 O → *hypo-...-ite*.

**Step 3:** Attach "potassium" in front of each anion name.

**Answer:** **$\ce{KClO4}$ = potassium perchlorate; $\ce{KClO3}$ = potassium chlorate; $\ce{KClO2}$ = potassium chlorite; $\ce{KClO}$ = potassium hypochlorite** (the active ingredient in bleach).

### Example 8: Adding Hydrogens to Phosphate

**Problem:** Write the formulas of the three phosphate-family ions and then the formula for sodium dihydrogen phosphate.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Start from phosphate, $\ce{PO4^3-}$. Each added $\ce{H+}$ raises the charge by one (reduces the magnitude by one):

$$\ce{PO4^3-} \to \ce{HPO4^2-} \to \ce{H2PO4-}$$

**Step 2:** Dihydrogen phosphate is $\ce{H2PO4-}$, charge $1-$.

**Step 3:** Pair with $\ce{Na+}$: charges cancel one-to-one, one module, no parentheses.

**Answer:** **$\ce{NaH2PO4}$.**

### Example 9: Transition Metal Detective Work

**Problem:** Name $\ce{Fe2(SO4)3}$.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** The known piece: sulfate is $\ce{SO4^2-}$. Three of them contribute $3 \times (2-) = 6-$.

**Step 2:** The compound is neutral, so the two iron atoms together contribute $6+$, i.e. $3+$ each.

**Step 3:** Iron is a transition metal → Roman numeral required: iron(III).

**Answer:** **Iron(III) sulfate.**

### Example 10: Formula for a Transition-Metal Compound

**Problem:** Write the formula for copper(II) acetate.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** The Roman numeral hands you the cation charge: $\ce{Cu^2+}$. Acetate is $\ce{C2H3O2-}$.

**Step 2:** One $2+$ needs two $1-$: subscript 2 on the acetate unit.

**Step 3:** Two acetate teams → parentheses.

**Answer:** **$\ce{Cu(C2H3O2)2}$.**

### Example 11: Borrowing the Ladder for Bromine

**Problem:** Using only patterns (no memorized bromine ions), write the formula for magnesium bromate.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Bromine sits directly below chlorine in Group 17, so it copies chlorine's oxyanion ladder. Chlorate is $\ce{ClO3-}$, so bromate is $\ce{BrO3-}$ — same 3 oxygens, same $1-$ charge.

**Step 2:** Pair with $\ce{Mg^2+}$: one $2+$ needs two $1-$.

**Step 3:** Two bromate teams → parentheses.

**Answer:** **$\ce{Mg(BrO3)2}$.**

### Example 12: Chromate vs Dichromate vs Permanganate

**Problem:** Name $\ce{K2Cr2O7}$ and $\ce{KMnO4}$, and explain how the potassium subscripts confirm the anion charges.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** $\ce{Cr2O7^2-}$ is dichromate ($2-$); $\ce{MnO4-}$ is permanganate ($1-$). These two are pure memorization — no pattern generates them, which is exactly why they show up on quizzes.

**Step 2:** Check with potassium: $\ce{K2Cr2O7}$ needs two $\ce{K+}$ to cancel a $2-$ anion ✓. $\ce{KMnO4}$ needs just one $\ce{K+}$ for a $1-$ anion ✓.

**Step 3:** Name cation-first: potassium + anion name.

**Answer:** **$\ce{K2Cr2O7}$ = potassium dichromate; $\ce{KMnO4}$ = potassium permanganate.** (Both are intensely colored — orange and deep purple — and both are AP-lab regulars in redox titrations.)

## Common Mistakes

| The Mistake | Why It Happens | How to Avoid It |
|---|---|---|
| Writing $\ce{CaNO32}$ instead of $\ce{Ca(NO3)2}$ | Forgetting the parentheses "brackets" around multiple teams | If you need **more than one** of a polyatomic ion, parentheses are mandatory |
| Changing subscripts *inside* the ion (e.g., $\ce{CaN2O6}$) | Treating the ion as loose atoms instead of an intact team | Criss-cross with the **whole unit**; never touch its internal subscripts |
| Thinking -ite means a different charge (sulfite as $\ce{SO3-}$) | Assuming fewer oxygens = less charge | -ate and -ite differ **only in oxygen count**; the charge is identical |
| Naming $\ce{NaNO3}$ "sodium nitride" | Forcing the -ide ending from binary naming onto a polyatomic | -ide endings are for single-atom anions (and hydroxide, cyanide, peroxide — the three exceptions); polyatomic ions keep their own names |
| Confusing hydroxide $\ce{OH-}$ with peroxide $\ce{O2^2-}$ | Both are small oxygen-containing $2$-atom ions | Hydroxide has H and is $1-$; peroxide is two oxygens and $2-$ ($\ce{Na2O2}$ vs $\ce{NaOH}$) |
| Adding Greek prefixes: "calcium dinitrate" | Carrying molecular-compound rules into ionic naming | Ionic compounds never use prefixes; the charges fix the ratio automatically |
| Treating $\ce{NH4+}$ as an element or forgetting it's the lone polyatomic **cation** | Every other common polyatomic ion is an anion | Ammonium is the one positive team; it takes the *cation* slot in names and formulas |
| Splitting polyatomic ions apart in net ionic equations | Overzealous "breaking into ions" | Dissociation splits at the **ionic** seam only; the covalently bonded team travels intact |

## AP Exam Tips

- **The AP periodic table will not save you.** The reference sheet lists elements, masses, and constants — **no polyatomic ion names, formulas, or charges**. College Board assumes this list is memorized. Budget real study time for it early in the year; it never stops being used.
- **Net ionic equations (Unit 4 FRQs) are the payoff.** A typical FRQ says "write the net ionic equation for the reaction between aqueous solutions of..." — worth multiple points, and impossible without instant recognition. If you can't see in two seconds that $\ce{(NH4)2SO4}$ dissociates into $\ce{2NH4+}$ and $\ce{SO4^2-}$, you can't even start.
- **Polyatomic ions stay intact in solution.** When identifying spectator ions, $\ce{NO3-}$ travels as $\ce{NO3-}$ — never split it into N and O. Splitting a polyatomic ion in a net ionic equation costs the point.
- **Nitrates, acetates, and ammonium salts are always soluble.** These solubility rules (Unit 4) are stated in terms of polyatomic ions, so the memorization pays double.
- **Roman numeral trap:** given something like $\ce{Cu3(PO4)2}$, the exam expects you to *derive* copper's charge from phosphate's known $3-$: total anion charge $6-$, so each copper is $2+$ → copper(II) phosphate. This only works if the polyatomic charge is automatic.
- **Watch for -ate/-ite in answer choices.** Multiple-choice distractors love swapping sodium sulfate for sodium sulfite. Slow down for one second on the vowel.
- **No calculator help here.** This is pure recall — which is good news: it's some of the cheapest, most secure points on the whole exam if you put in the flashcard reps.

### Problem 1

Name $\ce{(NH4)2CO3}$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Cation: $\ce{NH4+}$ = ammonium. Anion: $\ce{CO3^2-}$ = carbonate.

Charge check: $2(1+) + (2-) = 0$ ✓ — that's why two ammoniums are in the parentheses.

**Answer: Ammonium carbonate.**

</details>

### Problem 2

Write the formula for magnesium hydroxide.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Ions: $\ce{Mg^2+}$ and $\ce{OH-}$.

One $2+$ needs two $1-$ → two hydroxide teams → parentheses required.

**Answer: $\ce{Mg(OH)2}$.**

</details>

### Problem 3

Name $\ce{NaClO}$, and identify where you might find this compound at home.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The anion $\ce{ClO-}$ has one oxygen — the bottom rung of the chlorine ladder: **hypo**chlor**ite**.

**Answer: Sodium hypochlorite — the active ingredient in household bleach.**

</details>

### Problem 4

Write the formula for aluminum sulfate.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Ions: $\ce{Al^3+}$ and $\ce{SO4^2-}$.

Least common multiple of 3 and 2 is 6: two $\ce{Al^3+}$ ($6+$) and three $\ce{SO4^2-}$ ($6-$).

Three sulfate teams → parentheses.

**Answer: $\ce{Al2(SO4)3}$.**

</details>

### Problem 5

Name $\ce{Cu(NO3)2}$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Nitrate is $\ce{NO3-}$; two of them contribute $2-$, so copper must be $2+$.

Copper is a transition metal → Roman numeral required.

**Answer: Copper(II) nitrate.**

</details>

### Problem 6

Write the formula for potassium permanganate.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Ions: $\ce{K+}$ and $\ce{MnO4-}$.

Charges cancel one-to-one; only one permanganate team → no parentheses.

**Answer: $\ce{KMnO4}$.**

</details>

### Problem 7

Name $\ce{Ca3(PO4)2}$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Anion: $\ce{PO4^3-}$ = phosphate. Cation: calcium, fixed at $2+$ (Group 2), so no Roman numeral.

Charge check: $3(2+) + 2(3-) = 6 - 6 = 0$ ✓.

**Answer: Calcium phosphate** (the main mineral in bones and teeth).

</details>

### Problem 8

Write the formula for sodium nitrite. Then explain in one sentence why the sodium subscript is the same as in sodium nitrate.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Nitrite = nitrate minus one oxygen, same charge: $\ce{NO2-}$.

One $\ce{Na+}$ balances one $\ce{NO2-}$.

**Answer: $\ce{NaNO2}$.** The sodium subscript matches $\ce{NaNO3}$ because the -ate → -ite change alters only the oxygen count, **not the charge**, and the criss-cross depends only on charges.

</details>

### Problem 9

Using patterns only, write the formula for sodium iodate.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Iodine sits below chlorine in Group 17, so it copies the chlorine ladder: chlorate $\ce{ClO3-}$ → iodate $\ce{IO3-}$ (3 oxygens, $1-$).

Pair with $\ce{Na+}$: one-to-one, no parentheses.

**Answer: $\ce{NaIO3}$.**

</details>

### Problem 10

Name $\ce{NH4C2H3O2}$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Both ions are polyatomic: $\ce{NH4+}$ = ammonium (the lone polyatomic cation) and $\ce{C2H3O2-}$ = acetate.

Charge check: $(1+) + (1-) = 0$ ✓ — one of each, so no parentheses appear.

**Answer: Ammonium acetate.**

</details>

### Problem 11

Name $\ce{Ca(HCO3)2}$, using both the common name and the formal name for the anion.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The anion is carbonate with one added hydrogen: $\ce{CO3^2-} + \ce{H+} \to \ce{HCO3-}$ — charge magnitude drops from 2 to 1.

$\ce{HCO3-}$ = bicarbonate, formally hydrogen carbonate. Two of them balance one $\ce{Ca^2+}$.

**Answer: Calcium bicarbonate (formally: calcium hydrogen carbonate).**

</details>

### Problem 12

Write the formula for iron(III) hydroxide.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

The Roman numeral gives $\ce{Fe^3+}$; hydroxide is $\ce{OH-}$.

One $3+$ needs three $1-$ → three hydroxide teams → parentheses.

**Answer: $\ce{Fe(OH)3}$.**

</details>

### Problem 13

Name $\ce{Na2O2}$ and $\ce{Na2O}$, and explain why they are different compounds.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

$\ce{Na2O}$: the anion is plain oxide, $\ce{O^2-}$ — sodium oxide.

$\ce{Na2O2}$: two sodiums ($2+$ total) balance an anion with total charge $2-$ made of **two** oxygens — that's the peroxide team, $\ce{O2^2-}$: sodium peroxide.

In peroxide, the two oxygens are covalently bonded to each other inside the team (each effectively $1-$); in oxide, a single oxygen carries the full $2-$.

**Answer: $\ce{Na2O2}$ = sodium peroxide; $\ce{Na2O}$ = sodium oxide.**

</details>

### Problem 14

Name $\ce{(NH4)2Cr2O7}$, and verify the formula's subscripts from the ion charges.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Cation: $\ce{NH4+}$ = ammonium. Anion: $\ce{Cr2O7^2-}$ = dichromate.

Verification: the $2-$ dichromate needs two $1+$ ammoniums — hence $\ce{(NH4)2}$ with parentheses (two copies of a polyatomic ion), and dichromate written once with none.

**Answer: Ammonium dichromate** (the classic "volcano" demo compound).

</details>

### Problem 15

Solid $\ce{(NH4)2SO4}$ dissolves in water. Write the dissociation equation and state which bonds broke and which survived.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Dissolving splits the compound at the **ionic** seam only; each covalently bonded team travels intact:

$$\ce{(NH4)2SO4(s) -> 2NH4+(aq) + SO4^2-(aq)}$$

Broke: the ionic attraction between $\ce{NH4+}$ and $\ce{SO4^2-}$. Survived: every covalent $\ce{N-H}$ bond inside ammonium and every covalent $\ce{S-O}$ bond inside sulfate.

**Answer: $\ce{(NH4)2SO4(s) -> 2NH4+(aq) + SO4^2-(aq)}$ — ionic bonds break, covalent bonds within each polyatomic ion stay intact.** This is exactly the skill net ionic equations demand.

</details>

## Quick Reference

**The ions, grouped by charge:**

| $1+$ | $1-$ | $2-$ | $3-$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| $\ce{NH4+}$ ammonium | $\ce{OH-}$ hydroxide | $\ce{SO4^2-}$ sulfate | $\ce{PO4^3-}$ phosphate |
| | $\ce{CN-}$ cyanide | $\ce{SO3^2-}$ sulfite | |
| | $\ce{C2H3O2-}$ acetate | $\ce{CO3^2-}$ carbonate | |
| | $\ce{NO3-}$ nitrate | $\ce{CrO4^2-}$ chromate | |
| | $\ce{NO2-}$ nitrite | $\ce{Cr2O7^2-}$ dichromate | |
| | $\ce{MnO4-}$ permanganate | $\ce{O2^2-}$ peroxide | |
| | $\ce{HCO3-}$ bicarbonate | $\ce{HPO4^2-}$ hydrogen phosphate | |
| | $\ce{ClO4-}$/$\ce{ClO3-}$/$\ce{ClO2-}$/$\ce{ClO-}$ ladder | | |

**The patterns:**

| Pattern | Rule | Example |
|---|---|---|
| -ate → -ite | one fewer O, **same charge** | $\ce{SO4^2-} \to \ce{SO3^2-}$ |
| per-...-ate | one more O than -ate | $\ce{ClO3-} \to \ce{ClO4-}$ |
| hypo-...-ite | one fewer O than -ite | $\ce{ClO2-} \to \ce{ClO-}$ |
| add H | charge magnitude drops by 1 | $\ce{PO4^3-} \to \ce{HPO4^2-} \to \ce{H2PO4-}$ |
| same group, same ladder | Br, I copy Cl's oxyanions | $\ce{BrO3-}$, $\ce{IO3-}$ |

**Formula-writing checklist:** identify both ions and charges → criss-cross with the polyatomic as one sealed unit → parentheses only when the unit's subscript is 2 or more → verify total charge is zero.

**Mnemonic:** *Nick the Camel ate a Clam for Supper in Phoenix* — consonants = oxygens, vowels = charge.
