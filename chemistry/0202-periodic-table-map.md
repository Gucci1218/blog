# Periodic Table Map
<p class="article-date">July 4, 2026</p>

*Walk into my school's cafeteria and I can tell you things about a total stranger just from where they sit. Water polo players by the windows. Gamer kids by the outlets, obviously, because someone's Switch is always at 5%. Theater kids near the stage door. I've never met the new kid sitting at the gamer table, but I already know he'd rather speedrun Zelda than run laps. That's the entire superpower of the periodic table: an element's "seat" — its column and row — predicts its behavior before you've ever met it. Columns are the friend groups (elements in a column share the same number of outer electrons, so they act alike), rows are the grade levels (each new row adds a new electron shell, like each year adds experience), and walking left to right across the room takes you through a personality gradient from flashy, electron-giving metals to electron-hoarding nonmetals. Master this map now, because every unit of AP Chemistry — bonding, reactions, periodic trends — starts with the question "where does this element sit?"*

## The Core Concept

### Intuition First

Back to the cafeteria. The seating map works because seats aren't random — people cluster by shared behavior. The periodic table works for exactly the same reason, and the mapping is one-to-one:

| Cafeteria | Periodic Table | Why it predicts behavior |
|---|---|---|
| Friend group (which table) | **Group** (column) | Same number of **valence electrons** → same chemical personality |
| Grade level (freshman → senior) | **Period** (row) | Each row adds a new **electron shell** → atoms get bigger going down |
| Which side of the room | Metal side (left) vs. nonmetal side (right) | Left side *gives away* electrons; right side *takes* them |
| The huge middle section of tables | **Transition metals** (d-block) | One big extended family with their own house rules |
| The overflow annex nobody walks to | **f-block** (lanthanides/actinides) | Belongs in the main room but is printed at the bottom to save space |

Here's the punchline you should carry through all of AP Chem: **an element's seat tells you its valence electron count, and its valence electron count tells you its chemistry.** You learned in [Atomic Structure Basics](/chemistry/0201-atomic-structure-basics) that electrons — specifically the outermost ones — are the only part of an atom that other atoms ever touch. The periodic table is organized so that elements with the same number of outer electrons stack into the same column. That's not a coincidence Mendeleev stumbled onto; it's the whole design.

### How the Table Is Organized

Three organizing rules:

1. **Elements are arranged by increasing atomic number $Z$** (proton count), left to right, top to bottom. Hydrogen is 1, helium is 2, lithium is 3, and so on with no gaps. Read it like a book.
2. **Columns are called groups**, numbered 1–18 in the modern (IUPAC) system. You'll also see the older American labels — 1A through 8A for the "main groups" — on some tables. Elements in a group share valence electron counts, so they share behavior.
3. **Rows are called periods**, numbered 1–7. The period number tells you how many electron shells the atom's electrons occupy. Period 1 has one shell, period 2 has two, and so on — the "grade level" of the atom.

**Why the weird shape?** The table isn't a neat rectangle because the rows aren't all the same length — and the row lengths follow how many electrons each new shell can hold:

- **Period 1** has only 2 elements ($\ce{H}$ and $\ce{He}$) — the first shell holds just 2 electrons.
- **Periods 2 and 3** have 8 elements each — 8 more electrons fit before behavior repeats.
- **Periods 4 and 5** have 18 — this is where the **d-block** (transition metals) appears, the wide sunken middle section between groups 2 and 13.
- **Periods 6 and 7** would need 32 seats each. Printing 32 columns makes a table too wide for any poster or exam booklet, so 14 of those seats — the **f-block** lanthanides and actinides — are cut out and parked at the bottom like an overflow annex. They *belong* inside periods 6 and 7 (right after $\ce{La}$ and $\ce{Ac}$); the bottom placement is purely a formatting decision.

For now, take the row lengths as descriptive fact. In Unit 1 you'll meet electron configurations and orbitals, and the numbers 2, 8, 18, 32 will suddenly make perfect sense. Today we're just learning to read the map.

### Metals, Nonmetals, and the Staircase

Find the bold **staircase line** that starts at boron ($\ce{B}$) and steps down-right toward astatine ($\ce{At}$). It's the aisle dividing the cafeteria:

- **Metals** — everything to the *left* of the staircase (the vast majority of the table, roughly 75% of elements)
- **Nonmetals** — everything to the *right*, plus hydrogen (more on that misfit below)
- **Metalloids** — the elements hugging the staircase itself: $\ce{B}$, $\ce{Si}$, $\ce{Ge}$, $\ce{As}$, $\ce{Sb}$, $\ce{Te}$ (some lists add $\ce{Po}$ and $\ce{At}$)

| Property | Metals | Nonmetals | Metalloids |
|---|---|---|---|
| Appearance | Shiny (lustrous) | Dull | Often shiny but brittle |
| Conductivity | Excellent (heat + electricity) | Poor (insulators) | **Semiconductors** — in between |
| Malleability | Malleable, ductile (bend, don't break) | Brittle when solid | Brittle |
| State at room temp | Solid (except liquid $\ce{Hg}$) | Solids, liquids ($\ce{Br2}$), many gases | Solid |
| Chemistry | **Lose** electrons → form **cations** | **Gain** electrons → form **anions** | Intermediate, situational |
| Examples | $\ce{Na}$, $\ce{Fe}$, $\ce{Cu}$, $\ce{Au}$ | $\ce{O}$, $\ce{N}$, $\ce{Cl}$, $\ce{S}$ | $\ce{Si}$, $\ce{Ge}$, $\ce{B}$ |

The last chemistry row is the one AP cares about most: **metals lose electrons, nonmetals gain them.** That single sentence powers ion prediction (below) and all of ionic bonding (next article).

The metalloid to actually know is **silicon** — the semiconductor in every chip, including the ones inside the printers Dad works on at HP. "Conducts, but only when you tell it to" is exactly the in-between personality you'd expect from an element sitting on the aisle between the metal and nonmetal sides.

**The hydrogen asterisk:** hydrogen is printed at the top of group 1 because it has 1 valence electron, but it is a **nonmetal** — a colorless gas that usually shares electrons rather than losing one. It sits at the group 1 table but doesn't share the group 1 personality. Every class has that one kid.

### The Named Families

Five columns (plus the middle block) have names and famous personalities. Know one vivid behavior for each:

| Family | Group | Signature personality |
|---|---|---|
| **Alkali metals** | 1 ($\ce{Li}$, $\ce{Na}$, $\ce{K}$, $\ce{Rb}$, $\ce{Cs}$) | So desperate to dump their 1 valence electron that they react **violently with plain water**: $\ce{2Na + 2H2O -> 2NaOH + H2}$ — the $\ce{H2}$ gas often ignites from the reaction heat. Stored under oil because even humid air is too reactive. |
| **Alkaline earth metals** | 2 ($\ce{Be}$, $\ce{Mg}$, $\ce{Ca}$, $\ce{Sr}$, $\ce{Ba}$) | Two electrons to give — reactive, but calmer than group 1. Magnesium burns with a blinding white light (old camera flashes, emergency flares); calcium builds your bones and Torrey Pines' sandstone cliffs (as $\ce{CaCO3}$-cemented rock). |
| **Transition metals** | 3–12 ($\ce{Fe}$, $\ce{Cu}$, $\ce{Au}$, $\ce{Zn}$...) | The dependable middle: hard, dense, high melting points, and famous for **colorful compounds** and **multiple possible charges** ($\ce{Fe^2+}$ *and* $\ce{Fe^3+}$). The group-number shortcut does NOT apply to them. |
| **Halogens** | 17 ($\ce{F}$, $\ce{Cl}$, $\ce{Br}$, $\ce{I}$) | One electron short of a full shell — the most aggressive electron *takers* on the table. Fluorine is the single most reactive nonmetal; chlorine's grab-an-electron habit is why it kills pathogens in pools and drinking water. They pair up as $\ce{F2}$, $\ce{Cl2}$, $\ce{Br2}$, $\ce{I2}$. |
| **Noble gases** | 18 ($\ce{He}$, $\ce{Ne}$, $\ce{Ar}$, $\ce{Kr}$, $\ce{Xe}$) | Full valence shell → **want nothing from anyone**. Essentially unreactive — no compounds under normal conditions. That's why helium balloons don't burn and neon signs glow peacefully for decades. |

Notice the pattern hiding in that table: reactivity on the left comes from being *close to empty* (easy to lose 1–2 electrons), reactivity on the right comes from being *close to full* (desperate to gain 1–2), and the noble gases are unreactive because they're *already full*. **Everything on the periodic table is trying to get a noble-gas electron count.** Hold that thought — it becomes the octet rule in Unit 2.

### Valence Electrons from the Group Number

For **main-group elements** (groups 1, 2, and 13–18), the group number hands you the valence electron count:

$$\text{Groups 1–2: valence } e^- = \text{group number} \qquad \text{Groups 13–18: valence } e^- = \text{group number} - 10$$

| Group (IUPAC) | Old label | Valence $e^-$ | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1A | 1 | $\ce{Na}$ |
| 2 | 2A | 2 | $\ce{Mg}$ |
| 13 | 3A | 3 | $\ce{Al}$ |
| 14 | 4A | 4 | $\ce{C}$ |
| 15 | 5A | 5 | $\ce{N}$ |
| 16 | 6A | 6 | $\ce{O}$ |
| 17 | 7A | 7 | $\ce{Cl}$ |
| 18 | 8A | 8 (He: 2) | $\ce{Ne}$ |

(If your table uses the old A labels, it's even easier: the A-number *is* the valence count.)

**Why does this make group members behave alike?** Because valence electrons are the atom's entire public-facing personality. Lithium, sodium, and potassium all show the world exactly one outer electron, so water "sees" the same thing when any of them hits it — and all three react the same way, just with increasing violence going down the group. Same table in the cafeteria, same lunch order.

**Lewis dot preview:** since only valence electrons matter for bonding, chemists draw an element as its symbol surrounded by one dot per valence electron. Sodium is $\ce{Na}$ with 1 dot; oxygen is $\ce{O}$ with 6 dots (two pairs and two singles); neon is $\ce{Ne}$ with a full 8. You can write the Lewis dot symbol for any main-group element using nothing but its seat on the table. We'll use these constantly starting in [Ionic vs Covalent](/chemistry/0203-ionic-vs-covalent).

### Predicting Ion Charges from Position

Here's the payoff table — the one AP students must own cold, because **the exam's periodic table does not print ion charges**. The logic is one sentence long: *atoms gain or lose whatever gets them to the nearest noble-gas electron count.*

| Group | Valence $e^-$ | Action | Ion formed | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1 | Lose 1 | $1+$ | $\ce{Li+}$, $\ce{Na+}$, $\ce{K+}$ |
| 2 | 2 | Lose 2 | $2+$ | $\ce{Mg^2+}$, $\ce{Ca^2+}$, $\ce{Ba^2+}$ |
| 13 | 3 | Lose 3 | $3+$ | $\ce{Al^3+}$ |
| 15 | 5 | Gain 3 | $3-$ | $\ce{N^3-}$, $\ce{P^3-}$ |
| 16 | 6 | Gain 2 | $2-$ | $\ce{O^2-}$, $\ce{S^2-}$ |
| 17 | 7 | Gain 1 | $1-$ | $\ce{F-}$, $\ce{Cl-}$, $\ce{Br-}$ |
| 18 | 8 | Nothing | none | noble gases don't form ions |

Two boundary notes:

- **Group 14** ($\ce{C}$, $\ce{Si}$) sits dead center — 4 to lose or 4 to gain is too expensive either way, so these elements usually **share** electrons (covalent bonding) instead of forming simple ions.
- **Transition metals** don't follow the rule; many have several stable charges ($\ce{Cu+}$/$\ce{Cu^2+}$, $\ce{Fe^2+}$/$\ce{Fe^3+}$). Their charges get told to you (via names like iron(III) or by charge-balance in a formula). Two worth memorizing because they're boringly consistent: $\ce{Zn^2+}$ and $\ce{Ag+}$.

### Periodic Trends: A Preview

Two trends you should know *directionally* right now. Unit 1 will explain **why** each one happens (it's about nuclear charge and shells); today you just need the arrows.

**Atomic size (radius).** Atoms get **bigger going down** a group — each period adds a whole new electron shell, like adding a new outer layer to the atom. Atoms get **smaller going left to right** across a period, which sounds backwards (more electrons = bigger, right?) but isn't: the added electrons go into the *same* shell while the growing proton count pulls everything in tighter. Direction to memorize: **biggest atoms bottom-left ($\ce{Cs}$), smallest top-right ($\ce{He}$/$\ce{F}$ region)**. Unit 1 will explain WHY.

**Electronegativity** — an atom's greediness for shared electrons — runs **exactly opposite**: it **increases** left to right and **decreases** going down, making the top-right corner the greediest neighborhood on the table. **Fluorine is the most electronegative element, period** (noble gases sit out this contest since they don't want electrons at all). So the table's two corners are the drama: bottom-left elements shove electrons away, top-right elements yank them in — and when the two meet, electrons change hands completely. That handoff is the next article. Again: direction now, reasons in Unit 1.

### Reading an Element Box

Every square on the table is a mini ID card with three pieces of information:

```
        11        ← atomic number Z (protons — the identity)
        Na        ← element symbol
      22.99       ← average atomic mass (amu)
```

Connect each line back to [Atomic Structure Basics](/chemistry/0201-atomic-structure-basics):

- **Atomic number $Z = 11$**: sodium has 11 protons, always — and 11 electrons when neutral.
- **Symbol $\ce{Na}$**: one or two letters, sometimes from Latin (*natrium*), which is why it's not "So."
- **Average atomic mass $22.99$ amu**: the *abundance-weighted average* over all natural isotopes — the same calculation you did in 0201. It is **not** a mass number and is almost never a whole number. Chlorine's $35.45$ doesn't mean a chlorine atom weighs 35.45 amu; it means nature's mix is roughly 76% $\ce{^{35}Cl}$ and 24% $\ce{^{37}Cl}$.

Quick sanity check the exam loves: the **decimal-heavy number is the average mass; the integer is $Z$**. On the AP table, $Z$ is also always the smaller of the two (protons alone weigh less than protons + neutrons).

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Finding an Element from Its Seat

**Problem:** An element sits in period 3, group 17. Identify it, and state how many electron shells its electrons occupy.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Group 17 is the halogen column: $\ce{F}$ (period 2), $\ce{Cl}$ (period 3), $\ce{Br}$ (period 4), $\ce{I}$ (period 5).

**Step 2:** Period 3 selects chlorine.

**Step 3:** Period number = number of occupied shells, so chlorine's electrons occupy 3 shells.

**Answer: Chlorine ($\ce{Cl}$), with electrons in 3 shells.**

### Example 2: Metal, Nonmetal, or Metalloid?

**Problem:** Classify each: $\ce{Ca}$, $\ce{Si}$, $\ce{S}$, $\ce{Fe}$, $\ce{Ge}$.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Locate each relative to the staircase (runs from $\ce{B}$ down toward $\ce{At}$).

**Step 2:** $\ce{Ca}$ (group 2, far left) and $\ce{Fe}$ (d-block, middle-left) are left of the staircase → metals.

**Step 3:** $\ce{S}$ (group 16, upper right) is right of the staircase → nonmetal.

**Step 4:** $\ce{Si}$ and $\ce{Ge}$ sit directly on the staircase (group 14, periods 3 and 4) → metalloids.

**Answer: $\ce{Ca}$ metal, $\ce{Si}$ metalloid, $\ce{S}$ nonmetal, $\ce{Fe}$ metal, $\ce{Ge}$ metalloid.**

### Example 3: Identify the Family from the Behavior

**Problem:** A soft, silvery element is stored under oil. When a pea-sized piece is dropped into water, it skitters across the surface, fizzes furiously, and the escaping gas ignites. Which family, and what is the gas?

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** "Stored under oil" + "violent with water" is the alkali metal signature (group 1).

**Step 2:** The reaction pattern for any alkali metal $\ce{M}$: $\ce{2M + 2H2O -> 2MOH + H2}$.

**Step 3:** The flammable gas produced is hydrogen; the reaction's heat can ignite it.

**Answer: Alkali metals (group 1); the gas is $\ce{H2}$.**

### Example 4: Counting Valence Electrons

**Problem:** How many valence electrons do $\ce{P}$, $\ce{Ca}$, $\ce{Br}$, and $\ce{Ar}$ each have?

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** $\ce{P}$ is group 15: $15 - 10 = 5$ valence electrons.

**Step 2:** $\ce{Ca}$ is group 2: 2 valence electrons.

**Step 3:** $\ce{Br}$ is group 17: $17 - 10 = 7$ valence electrons.

**Step 4:** $\ce{Ar}$ is group 18: $18 - 10 = 8$ valence electrons (a full shell — that's why it's inert).

**Answer: $\ce{P}$: 5, $\ce{Ca}$: 2, $\ce{Br}$: 7, $\ce{Ar}$: 8.**

### Example 5: Lewis Dot Symbols from Position

**Problem:** Draw (describe) the Lewis dot symbols for $\ce{Mg}$, $\ce{N}$, and $\ce{Cl}$.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Get valence counts from group numbers: $\ce{Mg}$ (group 2) → 2; $\ce{N}$ (group 15) → 5; $\ce{Cl}$ (group 17) → 7.

**Step 2:** Place one dot per valence electron around each symbol (up to two per side of the symbol).

**Step 3:** $\ce{Mg}$: symbol with 2 dots. $\ce{N}$: 5 dots — one pair plus three singles. $\ce{Cl}$: 7 dots — three pairs plus one single (that lone single dot is the "one electron short" that makes halogens grabby).

**Answer: $\ce{Mg}$ shows 2 dots, $\ce{N}$ shows 5, $\ce{Cl}$ shows 7 — the dot count is just the group's valence number.**

### Example 6: Predicting Ion Charges

**Problem:** Predict the common ion for each: $\ce{K}$, $\ce{Ba}$, $\ce{Al}$, $\ce{S}$, $\ce{Br}$.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Ask each atom: what's the shortest path to a noble-gas electron count?

**Step 2:** $\ce{K}$ (group 1, 1 valence $e^-$): lose 1 → $\ce{K+}$. $\ce{Ba}$ (group 2): lose 2 → $\ce{Ba^2+}$. $\ce{Al}$ (group 13): lose 3 → $\ce{Al^3+}$.

**Step 3:** $\ce{S}$ (group 16, 6 valence $e^-$): gain 2 → $\ce{S^2-}$. $\ce{Br}$ (group 17): gain 1 → $\ce{Br-}$.

**Answer: $\ce{K+}$, $\ce{Ba^2+}$, $\ce{Al^3+}$, $\ce{S^2-}$, $\ce{Br-}$.**

### Example 7: From Seats to a Formula

**Problem:** Using only periodic table positions, predict the formula of the compound formed between calcium and chlorine.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** $\ce{Ca}$ is group 2 → forms $\ce{Ca^2+}$. $\ce{Cl}$ is group 17 → forms $\ce{Cl-}$.

**Step 2:** Compounds must be electrically neutral, so balance the charges: one $2+$ needs two $1-$.

**Step 3:** Formula: $\ce{CaCl2}$.

**Answer: $\ce{CaCl2}$** — two seats on the map, one formula, zero memorization. (Full treatment in the next article.)

### Example 8: Reading an Element Box

**Problem:** An element box reads: 20 on top, $\ce{Ca}$ in the middle, 40.08 at the bottom. State the proton count, the electron count in the neutral atom, and explain why 40.08 is not a whole number.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** The integer 20 is the atomic number $Z$ → 20 protons.

**Step 2:** Neutral atom: electrons = protons = 20.

**Step 3:** 40.08 amu is the **average atomic mass** — the abundance-weighted average of calcium's natural isotopes ($\ce{^{40}Ca}$, $\ce{^{44}Ca}$, etc.), exactly the weighted average from [Atomic Structure Basics](/chemistry/0201-atomic-structure-basics). No single atom weighs 40.08 amu.

**Answer: 20 protons, 20 electrons; 40.08 amu is an isotope-abundance-weighted average, not any one atom's mass.**

### Example 9: Same Column, Same Chemistry

**Problem:** Sodium reacts with water as $\ce{2Na + 2H2O -> 2NaOH + H2}$. Without any new information, write the equation for potassium reacting with water, and predict whether it's more or less violent.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** $\ce{K}$ sits directly below $\ce{Na}$ in group 1 — same friend group, same 1 valence electron, so the reaction has the same shape.

**Step 2:** Swap the symbol: $\ce{2K + 2H2O -> 2KOH + H2}$.

**Step 3:** Alkali metal reactivity *increases* going down the group (the lone valence electron sits farther out and is easier to lose — Unit 1 explains this fully), so potassium is more violent; its hydrogen gas ignites almost instantly with a lilac flame.

**Answer: $\ce{2K + 2H2O -> 2KOH + H2}$; more violent than sodium.**

### Example 10: Ranking Atomic Size

**Problem:** Rank from smallest to largest atomic radius: $\ce{K}$, $\ce{Li}$, $\ce{Na}$. Then decide: which is larger, $\ce{Na}$ or $\ce{Cl}$?

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** All three are group 1, so this is a pure down-the-column comparison: each step down adds a shell. $\ce{Li}$ (period 2) < $\ce{Na}$ (period 3) < $\ce{K}$ (period 4).

**Step 2:** $\ce{Na}$ vs. $\ce{Cl}$ is an across-the-period comparison (both period 3). Size *shrinks* left to right, and $\ce{Na}$ is far left while $\ce{Cl}$ is far right.

**Step 3:** So $\ce{Na}$ is the larger atom — despite chlorine having more electrons.

**Answer: $\ce{Li} < \ce{Na} < \ce{K}$; and $\ce{Na}$ is larger than $\ce{Cl}$.**

### Example 11: Electronegativity Direction

**Problem:** Using only positions, pick the more electronegative element of each pair: (a) $\ce{O}$ vs. $\ce{S}$, (b) $\ce{F}$ vs. $\ce{Cl}$, (c) $\ce{N}$ vs. $\ce{F}$.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** The rule: electronegativity increases toward the top-right (noble gases excluded).

**Step 2:** (a) $\ce{O}$ is directly above $\ce{S}$ in group 16 → $\ce{O}$ wins.

**Step 3:** (b) $\ce{F}$ is directly above $\ce{Cl}$ in group 17 → $\ce{F}$ wins.

**Step 4:** (c) $\ce{F}$ is to the right of $\ce{N}$ in period 2 → $\ce{F}$ wins. (Fluorine beats everyone.)

**Answer: (a) $\ce{O}$, (b) $\ce{F}$, (c) $\ce{F}$.**

### Example 12: The Mystery Element (AP-Style)

**Problem:** Element X is in period 4. It forms a $2+$ ion, reacts noticeably (but not explosively) with water, and its compounds burn with a red flame used in fireworks... actually, its flame is brick-red and it's essential to bones. Identify X.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** "Forms a $2+$ ion" + "reactive with water but calmer than group 1" → alkaline earth metal, group 2.

**Step 2:** Period 4 + group 2 → calcium.

**Step 3:** Cross-check the extra clues: $\ce{Ca}$ gives a brick-red flame test and builds bones as calcium phosphate. Consistent.

**Answer: Calcium ($\ce{Ca}$).** Notice the method: charge → group, shell count → period, and the seat identifies the stranger.

## Common Mistakes

| The Mistake | Why It Happens | How to Avoid It |
|---|---|---|
| Swapping **group** and **period** | Both are just "lines on a chart" until you attach meaning | Groups are vertical friend **g**roups; **p**eriods go across the **p**age. Group → valence electrons; period → shell count |
| Treating hydrogen as an alkali metal | It's printed at the top of group 1 | $\ce{H}$ is a nonmetal gas that mostly *shares* electrons; it sits at the table but isn't in the friend group |
| Using the group-number valence trick on transition metals | The rule is so convenient you want it everywhere | The shortcut is **main groups only** (1, 2, 13–18). Transition metals have variable charges — wait to be told ($\ce{Fe^2+}$ vs. $\ce{Fe^3+}$) |
| Reading average atomic mass as a mass number | Both are "the big number" | Average mass is a decimal-valued *weighted average* over isotopes; mass number $A$ is a whole-number count for *one* atom. The table prints the average |
| Forgetting the sign or size of an ion charge | Memorizing charges as random facts | Derive, don't memorize: metals lose down to the previous noble gas ($+$), nonmetals gain up to the next one ($-$); the *distance* to that noble gas is the charge size |
| Saying atoms get bigger left → right ("more electrons = bigger") | It's the intuitive guess | Added electrons enter the *same shell* while protons pull harder → atoms **shrink** across a period. Size only grows going *down* |
| Expecting group 14 to form $4+$/$4-$ ions | Extending the charge table too far | Carbon and silicon sit mid-row; 4 electrons is too many to trade, so they **share** (covalent) instead |
| Assuming the AP table lists ion charges or electronegativities | Many textbook tables decorate boxes with extra data | The AP table gives symbol, $Z$, and average mass — **nothing else**. Practice with the real thing |

## AP Exam Tips

- **The exam hands you a periodic table** — every section, no memorizing symbols or atomic masses. But know exactly what it contains: element **symbol**, **atomic number**, and **average atomic mass**. That's it.
- **What it does NOT give you** (so these live in your head): common **ion charges** by group, **polyatomic ion** names and formulas (coming soon — start flashcards early), **electronegativity values**, element **names** (symbols only — know that $\ce{Sn}$ is tin), and family names/locations.
- **Position-based prediction is a genuine AP question type.** Prompts like "element X is in group 2; predict the formula of its oxide" ($\ce{XO}$, from $\ce{X^2+}$ + $\ce{O^2-}$) or "which element behaves most like chlorine?" (anything else in group 17) test exactly this article. The answer is always "read the seat."
- **Trend questions demand the WHY** — on free-response questions, "electronegativity increases across a period" earns nothing without the reason (effective nuclear charge — Unit 1's headline act). For now, lock in the directions so the explanations have somewhere to land.
- **Use the table as a calculation tool:** average atomic masses feed directly into molar masses in Unit 1. Get fast at looking up masses without hunting — know the neighborhood layout (where the halogens live, where the transition metals start).
- **Watch for the average-mass trap:** a multiple-choice favorite is asking about the most abundant isotope of an element. Chlorine's 35.45 sits much closer to 35 than 37 → $\ce{^{35}Cl}$ dominates. Position of the average between isotope masses reveals abundance.

</details>

### Problem 3
Classify each as metal, nonmetal, or metalloid: $\ce{Mg}$, $\ce{As}$, $\ce{P}$, $\ce{Cu}$, $\ce{Sb}$, $\ce{Kr}$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Locate each against the staircase:

- $\ce{Mg}$ — group 2, far left → **metal**
- $\ce{As}$ — on the staircase (group 15, period 4) → **metalloid**
- $\ce{P}$ — group 15, period 3, right of the staircase → **nonmetal**
- $\ce{Cu}$ — transition block → **metal**
- $\ce{Sb}$ — on the staircase (group 15, period 5) → **metalloid**
- $\ce{Kr}$ — group 18 → **nonmetal** (noble gas)

**Answers: metals: $\ce{Mg}$, $\ce{Cu}$; metalloids: $\ce{As}$, $\ce{Sb}$; nonmetals: $\ce{P}$, $\ce{Kr}$.**

</details>

### Problem 4
A shiny solid element conducts electricity, but shatters when struck with a hammer, and its conductivity *increases* with temperature (metals do the opposite). Metal, nonmetal, or metalloid — and name a likely candidate.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Shiny + conducts sounds metallic, but *brittle* and *conductivity rising with temperature* are semiconductor fingerprints. The in-between property set → **metalloid**.

Most likely candidate: **silicon ($\ce{Si}$)** (germanium, $\ce{Ge}$, also acceptable) — the classic semiconductors sitting on the staircase.

**Answer: metalloid; e.g. $\ce{Si}$.**

</details>

### Problem 5
Name the family for each description: (a) forms $1-$ ions and exists as diatomic molecules; (b) never forms compounds under normal conditions; (c) stored under oil; (d) forms colorful compounds and can carry multiple different charges.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

(a) $1-$ ions + diatomic ($\ce{Cl2}$, $\ce{Br2}$...) → **halogens (group 17)**.

(b) Chemically inert, full valence shell → **noble gases (group 18)**.

(c) Too reactive for air or moisture → **alkali metals (group 1)**.

(d) Colorful compounds + variable charges ($\ce{Fe^2+}$/$\ce{Fe^3+}$) → **transition metals (groups 3–12)**.

**Answers: (a) halogens, (b) noble gases, (c) alkali metals, (d) transition metals.**

</details>

### Problem 6
How many valence electrons do each of the following have: $\ce{Si}$, $\ce{Se}$, $\ce{Rb}$, $\ce{Xe}$, $\ce{B}$?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Apply the main-group rule (groups 1–2: group number; groups 13–18: group number $- 10$):

- $\ce{Si}$ — group 14 → $14 - 10 = $ **4**
- $\ce{Se}$ — group 16 → **6**
- $\ce{Rb}$ — group 1 → **1**
- $\ce{Xe}$ — group 18 → **8**
- $\ce{B}$ — group 13 → **3**

**Answers: 4, 6, 1, 8, 3.**

</details>

### Problem 7
How many dots surround each symbol in the Lewis dot symbols of $\ce{Ca}$, $\ce{P}$, $\ce{I}$, and $\ce{Ne}$? Which of the four has *zero* interest in changing its dot count, and why?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Dot count = valence electrons:

- $\ce{Ca}$ (group 2) → **2 dots**
- $\ce{P}$ (group 15) → **5 dots**
- $\ce{I}$ (group 17) → **7 dots**
- $\ce{Ne}$ (group 18) → **8 dots**

$\ce{Ne}$ is content: 8 dots is a **full valence shell**, so it has nothing to gain by losing, gaining, or sharing — which is exactly why noble gases don't react.

**Answers: 2, 5, 7, 8; neon, because its shell is already full.**

</details>

### Problem 8
Predict the common ion of each: $\ce{Rb}$, $\ce{Sr}$, $\ce{N}$, $\ce{Se}$, $\ce{I}$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Shortest path to a noble-gas count:

- $\ce{Rb}$ — group 1, lose 1 → $\ce{Rb+}$
- $\ce{Sr}$ — group 2, lose 2 → $\ce{Sr^2+}$
- $\ce{N}$ — group 15, gain 3 → $\ce{N^3-}$
- $\ce{Se}$ — group 16, gain 2 → $\ce{Se^2-}$
- $\ce{I}$ — group 17, gain 1 → $\ce{I-}$

**Answers: $\ce{Rb+}$, $\ce{Sr^2+}$, $\ce{N^3-}$, $\ce{Se^2-}$, $\ce{I-}$.**

</details>

### Problem 9
An ion has 18 electrons and a charge of $2+$. Identify the element and its period, using positions rather than memorized configurations.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Charge $2+$ means the neutral atom *lost* 2 electrons, so protons $= 18 + 2 = 20$.

$Z = 20$ is **calcium** — and sure enough, $\ce{Ca}$ sits in group 2, exactly where $2+$ ions come from (the position and the algebra agree, which is your built-in error check).

Calcium is in **period 4**.

**Answer: $\ce{Ca^2+}$; calcium, period 4.**

</details>

### Problem 10
Lithium reacts with water. (a) Write the balanced equation by analogy with sodium's reaction. (b) Rank $\ce{Li}$, $\ce{Na}$, $\ce{K}$ from least to most violent with water.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

(a) Copy the group 1 pattern $\ce{2M + 2H2O -> 2MOH + H2}$ with $\ce{M = Li}$:

$$\ce{2Li + 2H2O -> 2LiOH + H2}$$

(b) Alkali reactivity increases going **down** the group (outer electron farther from the nucleus, easier to lose):

$$\ce{Li} < \ce{Na} < \ce{K}$$

**Answers: (a) $\ce{2Li + 2H2O -> 2LiOH + H2}$; (b) $\ce{Li} < \ce{Na} < \ce{K}$.**

</details>

### Problem 11
Using only positions, predict the formulas of the compounds formed between: (a) $\ce{K}$ and $\ce{Br}$; (b) $\ce{Mg}$ and $\ce{O}$; (c) $\ce{Al}$ and $\ce{Cl}$; (d) $\ce{Na}$ and $\ce{S}$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Predict charges from seats, then balance to neutral:

(a) $\ce{K+}$ + $\ce{Br-}$ → charges already balance 1:1 → $\ce{KBr}$

(b) $\ce{Mg^2+}$ + $\ce{O^2-}$ → $2+$ meets $2-$ → 1:1 → $\ce{MgO}$

(c) $\ce{Al^3+}$ + $\ce{Cl-}$ → one $3+$ needs three $1-$ → $\ce{AlCl3}$

(d) $\ce{Na+}$ + $\ce{S^2-}$ → two $1+$ balance one $2-$ → $\ce{Na2S}$

**Answers: (a) $\ce{KBr}$, (b) $\ce{MgO}$, (c) $\ce{AlCl3}$, (d) $\ce{Na2S}$.**

</details>

### Problem 12
Rank from smallest to largest atomic radius: (a) $\ce{F}$, $\ce{Cl}$, $\ce{Br}$; (b) $\ce{Mg}$, $\ce{Na}$, $\ce{Si}$ (all period 3).

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

(a) Same group (17), so size grows going down — each row adds a shell:

$$\ce{F} < \ce{Cl} < \ce{Br}$$

(b) Same period, so size *shrinks* left to right. Order on the map: $\ce{Na}$ (group 1), $\ce{Mg}$ (group 2), $\ce{Si}$ (group 14). Rightmost is smallest:

$$\ce{Si} < \ce{Mg} < \ce{Na}$$

**Answers: (a) $\ce{F} < \ce{Cl} < \ce{Br}$; (b) $\ce{Si} < \ce{Mg} < \ce{Na}$.**

</details>

### Problem 13
Pick the more electronegative element of each pair, positions only: (a) $\ce{C}$ vs. $\ce{O}$; (b) $\ce{Br}$ vs. $\ce{Cl}$; (c) $\ce{S}$ vs. $\ce{Cs}$. Then name the most electronegative element on the entire table.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Electronegativity increases toward the top-right:

(a) Same period (2); $\ce{O}$ is farther right → **$\ce{O}$**

(b) Same group (17); $\ce{Cl}$ is higher → **$\ce{Cl}$**

(c) $\ce{S}$ is top-right territory, $\ce{Cs}$ is the bottom-left corner → **$\ce{S}$** (not close — $\ce{Cs}$ is among the *least* electronegative elements)

Overall champion: **fluorine ($\ce{F}$)** — top-right of the reactive elements.

**Answers: (a) $\ce{O}$, (b) $\ce{Cl}$, (c) $\ce{S}$; most electronegative: $\ce{F}$.**

</details>

### Problem 14
An element box shows 35 on top, $\ce{Br}$ in the middle, and 79.90 at the bottom. Bromine has two major isotopes, $\ce{^{79}Br}$ and $\ce{^{81}Br}$. (a) How many protons and (neutral-atom) electrons? (b) Which isotope is more abundant — or are they close to even? Explain from the average.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

(a) The integer 35 is $Z$: **35 protons**, and a neutral atom has **35 electrons**.

(b) The average, 79.90, sits almost exactly midway between 79 and 81 (midpoint 80, and 79.90 is just barely below it). So the two isotopes are **nearly 50/50**, with $\ce{^{79}Br}$ *slightly* more abundant (the average leans a touch toward 79). In reality it's about 51% / 49% — the position of the average told you that without a calculator.

**Answers: (a) 35 protons, 35 electrons; (b) nearly even, with $\ce{^{79}Br}$ slightly ahead.**

</details>

### Problem 15
AP-style synthesis. Element Q is a period 3 element. It forms an ion with a $2-$ charge, and its most common isotope has 16 neutrons. (a) Identify Q. (b) Write its ion symbol and the number of electrons in that ion. (c) Name one other element expected to show similar chemistry, and justify from the map.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

(a) A $2-$ ion comes from group 16 (gain 2 to reach a noble-gas count). Period 3 + group 16 → **sulfur**. Cross-check: $Z = 16$, and $16 + 16$ neutrons gives $\ce{^{32}S}$, sulfur's most common isotope. Consistent.

(b) Ion: $\ce{S^2-}$. Electrons $= 16 + 2 = $ **18** (same count as argon — that was the whole point of gaining 2).

(c) **Oxygen** (or selenium) — same group 16 seat, same 6 valence electrons, so the same drive to gain 2 electrons and form a $2-$ ion. Same table in the cafeteria, same behavior.

**Answers: (a) sulfur; (b) $\ce{S^2-}$ with 18 electrons; (c) $\ce{O}$ (or $\ce{Se}$), same group → same valence count → same chemistry.**

</details>

## Quick Reference

**The map legend**

| Feature | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Group (column) | Same valence electrons → same chemistry |
| Period (row) | Number of occupied electron shells |
| Staircase ($\ce{B}$ → $\ce{At}$) | Metals left, nonmetals right, metalloids on the line |
| d-block (groups 3–12) | Transition metals — variable charges, no valence shortcut |
| f-block (bottom) | Lanthanides/actinides — belong in periods 6–7, parked below to save width |

**Families at a glance**

| Family | Group | One-liner |
|---|---|---|
| Alkali metals | 1 | Explode their 1 electron into water: $\ce{2Na + 2H2O -> 2NaOH + H2}$ |
| Alkaline earths | 2 | Two to give; reactive but calmer ($\ce{Mg}$ burns white) |
| Transition metals | 3–12 | Hard, colorful compounds, multiple charges |
| Halogens | 17 | One short of full — fiercest electron takers; diatomic |
| Noble gases | 18 | Full shell, want nothing, react with (almost) nothing |

**Ion charges from position (must know cold — NOT on the AP table)**

| Group | 1 | 2 | 13 | 14 | 15 | 16 | 17 | 18 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Valence $e^-$ | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 | 6 | 7 | 8 |
| Common ion | $1+$ | $2+$ | $3+$ | (shares) | $3-$ | $2-$ | $1-$ | none |

**Trend directions (Unit 1 explains why)**

| Trend | Across a period (→) | Down a group (↓) | Extreme |
|---|---|---|---|
| Atomic size | decreases | increases | biggest: bottom-left ($\ce{Cs}$) |
| Electronegativity | increases | decreases | highest: $\ce{F}$ |

**Element box:** integer = $Z$ = protons; decimal = average atomic mass (isotope-weighted average, not a mass number).
