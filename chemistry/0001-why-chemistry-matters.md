# Why Chemistry Matters
<p class="article-date">July 4, 2026</p>

*This morning I woke up at 6:40, unplugged my phone, and checked the battery — 100%, thanks to lithium ions that spent the night shuttling from cathode to anode in a slow, controlled redox reaction. In the shower, my shampoo foamed up because surfactant molecules have a split personality: one end loves water, the other end loves the oil in my hair, so they grab the grease and let the water rinse it away. Downstairs, my toast turned golden-brown and smelled amazing because amino acids and sugars were fusing into hundreds of new flavor molecules — the Maillard reaction, happening at about 150°C in my kitchen. Before heading out to Torrey Pines with my board, I put on sunscreen, a film of molecules engineered to absorb UV photons and convert them to harmless heat before they can wreck the DNA in my skin cells. One ordinary hour, and I'd already run four chemistry experiments without thinking about any of them. That's what hooked me: chemistry isn't a subject that lives in a lab — it's the machinery of everyday life, running silently in your phone, your shower, your breakfast, and your skin. Last year I blogged my way from zero to a 5 on AP Calculus BC, one article at a time. This year, same method, new machine room: this blog documents learning AP Chemistry from scratch — and you're invited to learn it with me.*

## The Core Concept

### Chemistry Is the Study of the Machinery

Here's the one-sentence version:

> **Chemistry is the science of matter — what stuff is made of, why it behaves the way it does, and how it transforms into other stuff.**

Physics tells you the rules of the universe. Biology tells you the story of living things. Chemistry sits right in the middle: it's the *engineering manual* for atoms and molecules. Every material you've ever touched, every smell you've ever noticed, every battery you've ever charged — chemistry explains the mechanism.

When I opened my morning with the phone battery, the shampoo, the toast, and the sunscreen, I wasn't cherry-picking. That's the normal density of chemistry in a normal life. You just need the vocabulary to see it. That's what this course gives you.

### Chemistry Is Everywhere

Here are six more places chemistry was hiding in plain sight, each with the one-line chemical explanation:

1. **A can of soda fizzes when you open it.** Carbon dioxide, $\ce{CO2}$, was dissolved under pressure; opening the can drops the pressure and the equilibrium $\ce{CO2(aq) <=> CO2(g)}$ shifts hard toward gas — that's Le Châtelier's principle escaping as bubbles.

2. **Baking soda makes pancakes fluffy.** Sodium bicarbonate, $\ce{NaHCO3}$, reacts with acids in the batter to release $\ce{CO2}$ gas: tiny bubbles get trapped in the dough, and breakfast rises.

3. **A rusty bike left out by the beach.** Iron surrenders electrons to oxygen in salty, humid air: $\ce{4Fe + 3O2 -> 2Fe2O3}$. Redox chemistry, slowly eating your ride. (Salt spray from the ocean makes it faster — San Diego bikes rust with enthusiasm.)

4. **Hand warmers get hot when you shake them.** Iron powder oxidizing is *exothermic* — the reaction releases energy as heat, which is thermochemistry you can hold.

5. **Boba pearls turn chewy when cooked.** Heat makes starch molecules absorb water and unravel (gelatinization) — the intermolecular forces between long polymer chains reorganize into that perfect bounce.

6. **Ocean water at Torrey Pines tastes salty, but the salt is invisible.** Solid $\ce{NaCl}$ dissolves completely into ions: $\ce{NaCl(s) -> Na+(aq) + Cl-(aq)}$. Water molecules surround each ion and pull the crystal apart, one ion at a time.

Every one of these examples is a preview of an AP Chemistry unit. The soda can is Unit 7 (Equilibrium). The pancakes are Unit 4 (Chemical Reactions). The rust and the hand warmer are Units 4 and 6. The boba is Unit 3 (Intermolecular Forces). The ocean is Units 3 and 4. The course is literally a tour of your own life, in slow motion and at atomic resolution.

## What AP Chemistry Actually Covers

The College Board organizes AP Chemistry into 9 units. Here's the full map, translated into plain English:

| Unit | Official Title | What It's Really About |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Atomic Structure & Properties | What atoms are made of, how electrons arrange themselves, and why the periodic table has the shape it does |
| 2 | Compound Structure & Properties | How atoms bond together — ionic, covalent, metallic — and how to draw and predict molecular shapes |
| 3 | Intermolecular Forces & Properties | The "weak glue" *between* molecules that decides whether something is a gas, liquid, or solid, and why water is so weird |
| 4 | Chemical Reactions | How to write, balance, and classify reactions, and how to count atoms and moles like an accountant |
| 5 | Kinetics | How *fast* reactions happen and what you can do to speed them up or slow them down |
| 6 | Thermochemistry | The energy bookkeeping of reactions — which ones release heat, which ones absorb it, and how much |
| 7 | Equilibrium | What happens when reactions run forward and backward at the same time, and how systems respond when you push on them |
| 8 | Acids & Bases | The chemistry of $\ce{H+}$ and $\ce{OH-}$ — pH, titrations, buffers, and why lemon juice and drain cleaner are opposites |
| 9 | Applications of Thermodynamics | The deepest question in the course: *why* do reactions happen at all? Entropy, free energy, and electrochemistry (your phone battery finally explained properly) |

Notice the arc: Units 1–3 build the *structure* story (atoms → molecules → materials), Unit 4 teaches the *language* of reactions, and Units 5–9 answer the three big questions about any reaction: How fast? How far? And why?

## How the Exam Works

The AP Chemistry exam has two sections:

| Section | Format | Questions | Time | Weight |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | Multiple Choice | 60 questions | 90 minutes | 50% |
| II | Free Response | 7 questions (3 long + 4 short) | 105 minutes | 50% |

Key facts worth knowing from day one:

- **Calculator allowed on both sections.** (This is a relatively recent change — older prep books say no calculator on multiple choice. Ignore them.)
- **You get a periodic table and a formula/constants sheet** for the entire exam. You never memorize atomic masses or fundamental constants — but you *do* need to know which formula to reach for and what every symbol in it means.
- **Pacing math:** 90 seconds per multiple-choice question; roughly 23 minutes for each long free-response and 9 minutes for each short one.
- The free-response section loves **explanations**: "justify your answer in terms of intermolecular forces" is worth as many points as any calculation. Chemistry is a writing-heavy science exam — practice explaining, not just computing.

## Why We Start with Foundations

When I started the calculus blog, I didn't open with limits — I opened with the algebra and function skills that limits secretly depend on. That decision paid for itself a hundred times over. Same plan here. Before AP Unit 1, this blog has two foundation units:

- **Unit 0a — The Math Toolkit.** Scientific notation, significant figures, unit conversions and dimensional analysis, logarithms, and rearranging equations. AP Chemistry is quietly a math course: every unit from stoichiometry to pH ($\text{pH} = -\log[\ce{H+}]$) leans on these skills. If the math is automatic, the chemistry gets all of your attention.

- **Unit 0b — Chemistry Foundations.** What atoms, elements, compounds, and mixtures actually are; how to read a chemical formula like $\ce{C6H12O6}$; states of matter; how to navigate the periodic table; and the mole as a counting unit. These are the words in every sentence the course will ever say to you.

If you've already had a strong honors chemistry class, skim Unit 0b and treat it as a diagnostic. If you're starting cold like me, these units are the difference between building on concrete and building on sand.

## Reflection Questions

No calculations yet — just noticing. Answers are hidden, but think first.

### Question 1
Find three chemical changes in your own first hour of the day (mine were the battery, the toast, and... well, the shampoo one is arguably physical — more on that soon). What makes you suspect each one is a *chemical* change rather than a physical one?

<details>
<summary><strong>Sample Answer</strong></summary>

Look for evidence that *new substances formed*: color change (toast browning), gas production (soda fizzing after dissolved $\ce{CO2}$ escapes is physical, but pancake batter releasing $\ce{CO2}$ from $\ce{NaHCO3}$ is chemical), energy release (hand warmer heating up), or irreversibility (you can't un-toast bread). Physical changes — ice melting, sugar dissolving — change form or state without creating new molecules. The dividing line isn't always obvious, and that's exactly what Unit 0b will sharpen.

</details>

### Question 2
Of the three big questions in Units 5–9 — *How fast? How far? Why?* — which one do you think explains why a diamond doesn't turn into graphite on your finger, even though graphite is more stable? Take a guess.

<details>
<summary><strong>Sample Answer</strong></summary>

**How fast** — kinetics. Thermodynamics (the "why") says diamond *should* convert to graphite; the reaction is favorable. But the rate is so absurdly slow at room temperature that it would take longer than the age of the universe. "Thermodynamically favorable but kinetically frozen" is one of the most important ideas in the whole course.

</details>

### Question 3
The exam gives you a periodic table and a formula sheet. What does that tell you about what the exam is actually testing?

<details>
<summary><strong>Sample Answer</strong></summary>

It's testing *reasoning*, not recall. If they hand you the data and the formulas, the points must come from knowing which tool fits which problem, setting it up correctly, and explaining why. Memorizing less, understanding more — which is good news, because understanding is the only part that's actually fun.

</details>

### Question 4
Why do you think this blog puts a math toolkit (Unit 0a) *before* any chemistry at all?

<details>
<summary><strong>Sample Answer</strong></summary>

Because working memory is limited. If you're wrestling with scientific notation *while* learning stoichiometry, both suffer. Automating the math first means that when mole ratios show up, 100% of your attention goes to the new idea. It's the same reason swimmers drill their stroke technique before racing: automatic fundamentals free your head for the race itself.

</details>
