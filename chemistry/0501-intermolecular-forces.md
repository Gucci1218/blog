# Intermolecular Forces
<p class="article-date">July 5, 2026</p>

*There's a gecko that lives on the outside of our kitchen window in San Diego, and last week I finally sat and watched it do the impossible: it walked straight up the glass, upside-down along the top, and hung there like the wall owed it money. No glue. No suction cups. No claws digging in — it's smooth glass. So how? The answer is the entire point of this unit. A gecko's foot isn't one sticky pad; it's split into millions of microscopic hairs, and each hair gets close enough to the glass that a fleeting, flickering electrical attraction snaps on for an instant. One hair's grip is laughably weak — you'd never feel it, you couldn't hang a paperclip on it. But millions of hairs, all flickering on at once, add up to enough force to hold a whole lizard against gravity. That is the big idea of intermolecular forces in one lizard: the attraction between two individual molecules is pathetically weak compared to the bonds inside a molecule, yet trillions of those weak attractions, added together, decide whether a substance is a gas you breathe, a liquid you pour, or a solid you can hold. Boiling, melting, sticking, dissolving — all of it comes from the gecko's foot.*

---

## What You'll Learn

- Tell the critical difference between **intramolecular** forces (bonds, *inside* a molecule) and **intermolecular** forces (IMFs, *between* molecules) — and know that boiling breaks IMFs while a chemical reaction breaks bonds
- Explain why IMFs are individually weak but collectively decide a substance's phase and physical properties
- Identify **London dispersion forces (LDF)** in every molecule, and predict their strength from electron count, molar mass, and polarizable surface area
- Recognize **dipole-dipole** forces between polar molecules and rank them against nonpolar molecules of similar mass
- Spot **hydrogen bonding** by its strict requirement — H bonded directly to **N, O, or F** — and use it to explain the anomalously high boiling points of $\ce{H2O}$, $\ce{NH3}$, and $\ce{HF}$
- Recognize **ion-dipole** forces and use them to explain why salts like $\ce{NaCl}$ dissolve in water
- List *all* the IMFs present in a given molecule and name the **strongest** one
- Predict which of two substances has the higher boiling point and **justify** it in AP-ready language

## The Core Concept

### Intuition First

Let's keep the gecko on the wall, because every part of it maps onto something real.

**One foot-hair = one instantaneous dipole.** A single gecko hair (a *seta*, if you want the biology word) doesn't grab the glass mechanically. As it drifts close, the electron clouds on the hair and on the glass jostle for a split second into a lopsided arrangement — a tiny "+" here, a tiny "−" there — and opposite charges pull together. It's fleeting: the clouds shift again and the grip releases, then re-forms somewhere else a nanosecond later. That flickering, temporary, individually-weak electrical tug **is literally a London dispersion force.** The gecko story isn't a metaphor for van der Waals forces; it's the actual physics scientists measured when they figured out gecko feet.

**Millions of hairs = trillions of molecules.** No one hair matters. What holds the lizard is *how many* attractions happen at once. This is the whole trick of IMFs: any single molecule-to-molecule attraction is trivial, but a drop of water contains something like $10^{21}$ molecules, all tugging on their neighbors simultaneously. Add up enough weak grips and you get a force strong enough to matter — strong enough to keep water liquid at room temperature while a similar-sized nonpolar molecule is a gas.

**The lizard staying up = a bulk property.** You can't see one hair's grip, but you *can* see the lizard hanging there. In the same way, you never watch a single IMF, but you measure its consequences constantly: the boiling point, the melting point, the surface tension, whether two liquids mix, how sticky or slippery something feels. Those visible, measurable, whole-substance behaviors are what this unit calls **physical properties**, and they are the sum of trillions of invisible weak attractions.

Hold onto that three-part map — one hair = one instantaneous dipole, millions of hairs = trillions of molecules, the lizard hanging = a bulk property — because it carries the entire unit.

### The #1 Distinction: INTRA vs INTER

Before any hierarchy of forces, you have to get one word right, or the rest of the unit collapses. This is the single most common confusion in Unit 3, so we're going to hammer it.

- **Intra**molecular forces are the **bonds inside** a molecule — the covalent bonds holding the two H's onto the O in $\ce{H2O}$. ("Intra" = *within*, like an intramural game is within one school.)
- **Inter**molecular forces (IMFs) are the attractions **between separate** molecules — one whole $\ce{H2O}$ tugging on a different whole $\ce{H2O}$ next to it. ("Inter" = *between*, like an interstate highway runs between states.)

Here's why the distinction is do-or-die on the AP exam:

$$\underbrace{\text{Boiling / melting}}_{\text{breaks IMFs (between molecules)}} \quad\neq\quad \underbrace{\text{Chemical reaction}}_{\text{breaks bonds (inside molecules)}}$$

When you boil water, you are **not** turning it into hydrogen and oxygen gas. The $\ce{O-H}$ bonds stay perfectly intact — steam is still $\ce{H2O}$, just molecules that have escaped each other's grip. Boiling only pries the molecules *apart from one another*; it breaks the weak **inter**molecular forces, not the strong **intra**molecular bonds. To actually rip the $\ce{O-H}$ bonds apart (splitting water into $\ce{H2}$ and $\ce{O2}$) is a *chemical change* and takes far, far more energy.

The numbers make the gap obvious. A typical covalent bond is worth a few hundred $\ce{kJ/mol}$ (the $\ce{O-H}$ bond is about $460\ \ce{kJ/mol}$). A typical IMF is worth only a handful — often less than $40\ \ce{kJ/mol}$, and dispersion forces between small molecules under $2\ \ce{kJ/mol}$. That's the gecko again: the bond *inside* the molecule is the lizard's own strong skeleton; the IMF *between* molecules is one hair's flickering grip. Never mix them up.

### The IMF Hierarchy: Weakest → Strongest

Every intermolecular force is ultimately electrostatic — plus attracting minus. They differ only in *what* is doing the attracting. Here is the whole hierarchy, weakest to strongest, with the one question that tells you when each applies.

| Force | Present in... | Arises from | Relative strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| **London dispersion (LDF)** | **Everything** — all molecules | *Temporary* dipoles from momentary electron sloshing | Weakest (but grows a lot with size) |
| **Dipole-dipole** | Polar molecules only | *Permanent* dipoles (δ+ end meets δ− end) | Moderate |
| **Hydrogen bonding** | Molecules with H bonded to **N, O, or F** | A specially strong dipole-dipole | Strong |
| **Ion-dipole** | An ion + a polar molecule | Full ionic charge pulling on a permanent dipole | Strongest of these |

A few ground rules before we take them one at a time:

- **LDF is never absent.** *Every* substance has dispersion forces, because every atom has electrons that can slosh. When a molecule is nonpolar, LDF is the *only* force it has.
- The forces stack. A polar molecule has LDF **and** dipole-dipole. A hydrogen-bonding molecule has LDF **and** dipole-dipole **and** H-bonding. You add the weak ones underneath the strong one.
- When you're asked for "the strongest IMF," you name the top force on this ladder that the molecule qualifies for.

#### 1. London Dispersion Forces (LDF) — the gecko's actual grip

Take a nonpolar molecule like $\ce{Cl2}$. On average its electrons are spread evenly, so on average it has no dipole. But electrons are always moving, and at any *instant* they might bunch slightly to one side, giving a fleeting **instantaneous dipole**. That momentary δ− pushes the electrons of a neighbor away, inducing a matching dipole in it, and for a split second the two molecules attract. Then it's gone and re-forms elsewhere. Exactly the gecko hair.

The key question is: **how easily do a molecule's electrons slosh?** That's called **polarizability**, and three things increase it:

- **More electrons / higher molar mass.** A big electron cloud is loose and floppy and slosh-able; a small one is held tight. More electrons → stronger LDF. This is the single most reliable trend in the whole topic.
- **Larger surface area.** Molecules that can lie flat against each other, touching over a long stretch, get more points of contact — more hairs on the wall.
- **Shape at equal mass.** Long straight chains touch more than compact branched blobs.

Watch the trend do real work in the **halogens**, all nonpolar diatomics whose *only* force is LDF:

$$\ce{F2}\ (\text{gas}) \quad\to\quad \ce{Cl2}\ (\text{gas}) \quad\to\quad \ce{Br2}\ (\text{liquid}) \quad\to\quad \ce{I2}\ (\text{solid})$$

Going down the group, each molecule has more electrons, so LDF strengthens, so boiling point climbs — and at room temperature you literally walk from gas to liquid to solid just by adding electrons. Same story with the **noble gases**: helium (2 electrons) boils at $4\ \ce{K}$; xenon (54 electrons) boils at $165\ \ce{K}$. Nothing but LDF, and the boiling point tracks electron count perfectly.

And the **surface-area** effect, which AP loves: $n$-pentane and neopentane are both $\ce{C5H12}$ — *identical* molar mass. But $n$-pentane is a long straight chain that lies against its neighbors like a plank, while neopentane is a compact ball that only touches at points. More contact → stronger LDF → higher boiling point. $n$-Pentane boils at $36\,^\circ\ce{C}$; neopentane at $9.5\,^\circ\ce{C}$. Same formula, different shape, 27-degree difference — pure surface area.

#### 2. Dipole-Dipole Forces — permanent, not fleeting

If a molecule is **polar** (a permanent dipole — go back to [Polarity and Hybridization](/chemistry/0407-polarity-hybridization) if that's fuzzy), then it doesn't have to wait for a lucky electron slosh. It's *always* lopsided: a permanent δ+ end and a permanent δ− end. Neighboring polar molecules line up δ+ to δ−, and the attraction is on all the time. This is generally stronger than LDF for molecules of comparable size.

The classic AP move is **similar mass, polar vs nonpolar**. Compare propane $\ce{C3H8}$ (44 g/mol, nonpolar, LDF only) with acetaldehyde $\ce{CH3CHO}$ (44 g/mol, polar, LDF **plus** dipole-dipole). Nearly identical mass, so nearly identical LDF — but acetaldehyde has an extra force stacked on top. Propane boils at $-42\,^\circ\ce{C}$; acetaldehyde at $+20\,^\circ\ce{C}$. That 62-degree gap is dipole-dipole made visible.

#### 3. Hydrogen Bonding — the strong special case (and the #1 trap)

Hydrogen bonding is *not* a new kind of force — it's an unusually strong **dipole-dipole** that shows up under one strict condition:

$$\boxed{\text{An H atom bonded directly to } \ce{N},\ \ce{O},\ \text{or } \ce{F} \ \ \longrightarrow\ \ \text{and attracted to a lone pair on another } \ce{N},\ \ce{O},\ \text{or } \ce{F}}$$

Why only N, O, F? They are the three most electronegative common elements, and they're small. When H bonds to one of them, the H is stripped so bare of electron density that it's almost a naked proton — an intense, concentrated δ+. That δ+ dives into a lone pair on the N/O/F of a neighbor, and the attraction is exceptionally strong for an IMF.

**This is the #1 trap in the unit.** A molecule does **not** have hydrogen bonding just because it contains hydrogen. Methane $\ce{CH4}$ is stuffed with H's and has *zero* hydrogen bonding — the H's are on carbon, not on N/O/F. The rule is about *what the H is bonded to*, not whether an H exists. Say it out loud: **H on N, O, or F, or it doesn't count.**

The payoff is the most famous graph in this unit: the boiling points of the hydrides going down groups 14–17.

- Group 14 ($\ce{CH4}, \ce{SiH4}, \ce{GeH4}...$): no H-bonding anywhere, so boiling point rises smoothly with molar mass (pure LDF), exactly as expected.
- Groups 15, 16, 17: the *first* member — $\ce{NH3}$, $\ce{H2O}$, $\ce{HF}$ — sticks up **way** above the trend line, anomalously high. Water "should" boil around $-80\,^\circ\ce{C}$ if it followed its group's LDF trend; instead it boils at $+100\,^\circ\ce{C}$. That giant anomaly is hydrogen bonding.

Water is the champion because each $\ce{H2O}$ has two O–H bonds *and* two lone pairs — it can form up to **four** hydrogen bonds at once, a dense web of them. That web is behind almost every weird, life-supporting thing water does: high boiling point, high surface tension, ice floating (we'll unpack water's oddities in the next article). For now, just log it: $\ce{H2O}$, $\ce{NH3}$, and $\ce{HF}$ punch above their weight because of hydrogen bonding.

#### 4. Ion-Dipole Forces — the strongest of the bunch

Finally, mix a full-charge **ion** with a **polar** molecule and you get an ion-dipole force. It's the strongest force in this chapter because a whole $+1$ or $-1$ charge is far more forceful than a mere partial δ+/δ−.

This is the answer to "why does salt dissolve in water?" Drop $\ce{NaCl}$ into water and the polar water molecules swarm each ion: the δ− oxygen ends surround each $\ce{Na+}$, and the δ+ hydrogen ends surround each $\ce{Cl-}$. Those ion-dipole attractions are strong enough to pull the ions out of the crystal and wrap ("hydrate") them in a shell of water:

$$\ce{NaCl(s) ->[\ce{H2O}] Na+(aq) + Cl-(aq)}$$

Ion-dipole forces are the setup for the whole solubility story coming later — file it away as *ions + polar solvent = dissolving*.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: The Vocabulary That Decides Everything — INTRA vs INTER

**Problem:** When you boil water into steam, which forces break: the $\ce{O-H}$ bonds or the forces between molecules? Is boiling a physical or chemical change?

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Identify what's what.** The $\ce{O-H}$ bonds are **intra**molecular (inside each molecule). The attractions between separate $\ce{H2O}$ molecules are **inter**molecular (hydrogen bonds, here).

**Step 2 — What does boiling do?** Boiling separates molecules *from each other*; it does not touch the bonds inside them. Steam is still $\ce{H2O}$.

**Step 3 — Classify.** No bonds broken, same substance → **physical change**, and only the (weak) IMFs are overcome.

**Answer: Boiling breaks the intermolecular hydrogen bonds *between* water molecules, not the intramolecular $\ce{O-H}$ bonds. It's a physical change — steam is still $\ce{H2O}$. Splitting $\ce{O-H}$ bonds would be a chemical change and cost far more energy.**

### Example 2: LDF Down the Halogens

**Problem:** At room temperature $\ce{F2}$ and $\ce{Cl2}$ are gases, $\ce{Br2}$ is a liquid, and $\ce{I2}$ is a solid. Explain the trend in terms of IMFs.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — What forces are present?** All four are nonpolar diatomics, so their *only* IMF is London dispersion.

**Step 2 — What changes down the group?** Electron count and molar mass increase ($\ce{F2}$ has 18 electrons, $\ce{I2}$ has 106). Bigger electron clouds are more polarizable.

**Step 3 — Consequence.** More polarizable → stronger LDF → more energy needed to separate molecules → higher boiling/melting point → you climb from gas to liquid to solid.

**Answer: LDF strengthens down the group because electron count (and thus polarizability) rises, so boiling/melting points climb — enough to shift phase from gas ($\ce{F2}, \ce{Cl2}$) to liquid ($\ce{Br2}$) to solid ($\ce{I2}$) at room temperature.**

### Example 3: Same Formula, Different Shape

**Problem:** $n$-pentane boils at $36\,^\circ\ce{C}$ and neopentane (its branched isomer) boils at $9.5\,^\circ\ce{C}$. Both are $\ce{C5H12}$. Explain.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Forces present.** Both are nonpolar; both have LDF only. Same formula → essentially the same number of electrons, so mass alone can't explain the gap.

**Step 2 — The tiebreaker is surface area.** $n$-Pentane is a long straight chain that can lie flat against neighboring molecules along its whole length — lots of contact. Neopentane is a compact, roughly spherical ball that touches neighbors only at points.

**Step 3 — Consequence.** More surface contact → stronger total LDF → higher boiling point.

**Answer: With equal mass, the more spread-out $n$-pentane has greater surface contact, so stronger London dispersion forces and a higher boiling point than the compact, low-contact neopentane.**

### Example 4: Polar Beats Nonpolar at Equal Mass

**Problem:** Propane $\ce{C3H8}$ (44 g/mol) boils at $-42\,^\circ\ce{C}$; acetaldehyde $\ce{CH3CHO}$ (44 g/mol) boils at $+20\,^\circ\ce{C}$. Explain.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — LDF comparison.** Equal molar mass → comparable electron counts → comparable London dispersion forces. LDF can't explain the difference.

**Step 2 — The extra force.** Propane is nonpolar (LDF only). Acetaldehyde has a polar $\ce{C=O}$ group → it's polar, so it has dipole-dipole forces *stacked on top of* its LDF.

**Step 3 — Consequence.** Extra force → more energy to separate → higher boiling point.

**Answer: Both have similar LDF (equal mass), but acetaldehyde is polar and adds dipole-dipole forces that propane lacks, so acetaldehyde boils higher.**

### Example 5: The Hydrogen-Bonding Trap

**Problem:** A student claims methane $\ce{CH4}$ has hydrogen bonding "because it has four hydrogens." Are they right? What's methane's actual strongest IMF?

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Check the rule.** Hydrogen bonding requires H bonded directly to N, O, or F. In $\ce{CH4}$ the H's are bonded to **carbon**.

**Step 2 — Verdict on the claim.** Carbon isn't N/O/F, so no hydrogen bonding — having H atoms is not enough.

**Step 3 — Its real force.** $\ce{CH4}$ is nonpolar, so its only (and therefore strongest) IMF is London dispersion.

**Answer: No — hydrogen bonding needs H on N, O, or F, and methane's H's are on carbon. Methane's only IMF is London dispersion. Containing hydrogen is irrelevant; what the H is bonded to is everything.**

### Example 6: Why Water Boils So High

**Problem:** $\ce{H2S}$ (34 g/mol) boils at $-60\,^\circ\ce{C}$, but $\ce{H2O}$ (18 g/mol) boils at $+100\,^\circ\ce{C}$ — even though water is *lighter*. Explain.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — By mass alone,** the heavier $\ce{H2S}$ should boil higher (more LDF). The reverse happens, so something else dominates.

**Step 2 — Check for H-bonding.** In $\ce{H2O}$, H is bonded to O (one of N/O/F) → strong hydrogen bonding. In $\ce{H2S}$, H is bonded to S (not N/O/F) → no hydrogen bonding, just dipole-dipole + LDF.

**Step 3 — Consequence.** Water's hydrogen-bond network is far stronger than anything in $\ce{H2S}$, so despite being lighter, water needs much more energy to boil.

**Answer: Water hydrogen-bonds (H on O) while $\ce{H2S}$ cannot (H on S). The strong H-bond network makes water boil far higher, overpowering the mass advantage of $\ce{H2S}$.**

### Example 7: List ALL the Forces

**Problem:** List every intermolecular force present in (a) $\ce{CO2}$, (b) $\ce{CH3Cl}$, (c) $\ce{CH3OH}$ (methanol), and name the strongest in each.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — $\ce{CO2}$.** Linear, symmetric → nonpolar → LDF only. Strongest: **LDF**.

**Step 2 — $\ce{CH3Cl}$.** Polar (one unopposed $\ce{C-Cl}$ dipole), no H on N/O/F → LDF + dipole-dipole. Strongest: **dipole-dipole**.

**Step 3 — $\ce{CH3OH}$.** Has an $\ce{O-H}$ group (H on O!) → LDF + dipole-dipole + hydrogen bonding. Strongest: **hydrogen bonding**.

**Answer: (a) $\ce{CO2}$: LDF only. (b) $\ce{CH3Cl}$: LDF + dipole-dipole (strongest: dipole-dipole). (c) $\ce{CH3OH}$: LDF + dipole-dipole + hydrogen bonding (strongest: hydrogen bonding). Remember the weaker forces are always still present underneath the strongest one.**

### Example 8: Rank Four Boiling Points

**Problem:** Rank these from lowest to highest boiling point and justify: $\ce{Ne}$, $\ce{CH4}$, $\ce{HCl}$, $\ce{H2O}$.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Classify forces.** $\ce{Ne}$: LDF only, tiny (10 electrons). $\ce{CH4}$: LDF only, also small but a bit more than Ne. $\ce{HCl}$: polar → LDF + dipole-dipole. $\ce{H2O}$: LDF + dipole-dipole + **hydrogen bonding**.

**Step 2 — Order by strongest force.** LDF-only (weakest) < dipole-dipole < hydrogen bonding. Between the two LDF-only species, $\ce{CH4}$ edges out $\ce{Ne}$ (more electrons).

**Step 3 — Assemble.** $\ce{Ne} < \ce{CH4} < \ce{HCl} < \ce{H2O}$.

**Answer: $\ce{Ne} < \ce{CH4} < \ce{HCl} < \ce{H2O}$ — the two nonpolar species (LDF only) sit lowest, then the polar $\ce{HCl}$ (dipole-dipole), and $\ce{H2O}$ highest because of hydrogen bonding. (Real BPs: $-246$, $-161$, $-85$, $+100\,^\circ\ce{C}$.)**

### Example 9: Why Salt Dissolves

**Problem:** Explain, in terms of IMFs, why $\ce{NaCl}$ dissolves readily in water but not in gasoline (a nonpolar hydrocarbon).

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Water.** Water is polar, so it can form **ion-dipole** forces with the $\ce{Na+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$ ions — δ− oxygen ends surround $\ce{Na+}$, δ+ hydrogen ends surround $\ce{Cl-}$. These are strong enough to pull ions out of the crystal.

**Step 2 — Gasoline.** Nonpolar molecules have no permanent dipole, so they can't form ion-dipole attractions. They can't compete with the strong ionic bonds holding the crystal together.

**Step 3 — Consequence.** Water hydrates and separates the ions; gasoline can't, so the salt stays undissolved.

**Answer: $\ce{NaCl}$ dissolves in water because polar water forms strong ion-dipole forces that pull the ions apart and surround them. Nonpolar gasoline offers no ion-dipole attraction, so it cannot dissolve the salt — "like dissolves like."**

### Example 10: The Justify-It FRQ

**Problem:** Which has the higher boiling point, $\ce{NH3}$ or $\ce{PH3}$? Give a complete AP-style justification.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Mass check.** $\ce{PH3}$ (34 g/mol) is heavier than $\ce{NH3}$ (17 g/mol), so by LDF alone $\ce{PH3}$ would win. If the answer is $\ce{NH3}$, another force must dominate.

**Step 2 — Identify the deciding force.** In $\ce{NH3}$, H is bonded to N (one of N/O/F) → hydrogen bonding. In $\ce{PH3}$, H is bonded to P (not N/O/F) → no hydrogen bonding, only dipole-dipole + LDF.

**Step 3 — Conclude.** $\ce{NH3}$'s hydrogen bonding is stronger than the forces in $\ce{PH3}$, so it needs more energy to boil.

**Answer: $\ce{NH3}$ boils higher. Even though $\ce{PH3}$ is heavier (stronger LDF), $\ce{NH3}$ has hydrogen bonding (H bonded to N) while $\ce{PH3}$ does not, and that stronger force determines the higher boiling point.**

### Example 11: Noble Gas Boiling Points

**Problem:** He boils at $4\ \ce{K}$, Ar at $87\ \ce{K}$, and Xe at $165\ \ce{K}$. All are single, nonpolar atoms. Explain the trend.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Only force available.** Individual noble-gas atoms are nonpolar with no bonds to neighbors — their *only* IMF is London dispersion.

**Step 2 — What varies.** Electron count rises He (2) → Ar (18) → Xe (54). More electrons → larger, more polarizable cloud → stronger LDF.

**Step 3 — Consequence.** Stronger LDF → higher boiling point, smoothly up the group.

**Answer: With LDF as the only force, boiling point tracks electron count: more electrons (He → Ar → Xe) means a more polarizable cloud, stronger dispersion forces, and a higher boiling point.**

### Example 12: Two Traps in One

**Problem:** A student writes: "$\ce{CH3OCH3}$ (dimethyl ether) has hydrogen bonding because it has both H and O, so it boils higher than its isomer $\ce{CH3CH2OH}$ (ethanol)." Find and fix the error.

**Solution:**

**Step 1 — Check the H-bond rule for dimethyl ether.** Hydrogen bonding needs H bonded *directly to* N/O/F. In $\ce{CH3OCH3}$ every H is on **carbon**; the oxygen sits between two carbons with no H attached to it. So **no hydrogen bonding** — just dipole-dipole + LDF.

**Step 2 — Check ethanol.** In $\ce{CH3CH2OH}$ there's an $\ce{O-H}$ group — an H bonded directly to O. **Yes, hydrogen bonding.**

**Step 3 — Fix the conclusion.** Same formula ($\ce{C2H6O}$), but ethanol H-bonds and the ether doesn't, so **ethanol** boils higher — the opposite of the student's ranking. (Ethanol $78\,^\circ\ce{C}$ vs. ether $-24\,^\circ\ce{C}$.)

**Answer: The error is assuming "has H and O" means hydrogen bonding — the H must be bonded *to* the O/N/F. Dimethyl ether's H's are all on carbon (no H-bonding), while ethanol's $\ce{O-H}$ gives it hydrogen bonding, so **ethanol** boils much higher.**

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why it happens | How to avoid it |
|---|---|---|
| Confusing **intra** and **inter**molecular forces | The words look almost identical | *Intra* = **in**side one molecule (bonds); *inter* = **be**tween molecules (IMFs). Boiling breaks IMFs, not bonds. |
| Saying "boiling breaks bonds" | Feels like heat destroys the molecule | Steam is still $\ce{H2O}$ — only the forces *between* molecules break. Bond-breaking is a *chemical* change. |
| Claiming H-bonding whenever a molecule contains H | "Hydrogen bonding" sounds like "has hydrogen" | The H must be bonded **directly to N, O, or F**. $\ce{CH4}$ has 4 H's and zero H-bonding. |
| Forgetting LDF exists in polar/H-bonding molecules | Focusing only on the "strongest" force | LDF is in *everything*. Polar molecules have LDF **and** dipole-dipole; list them all. |
| Ranking boiling points by molar mass alone | LDF really does track mass, so it becomes a reflex | Mass only decides among molecules with the *same* strongest force. A stronger force (H-bond) beats mass — see $\ce{H2O}$ vs $\ce{H2S}$. |
| Thinking IMFs are just weak versions of bonds | Both are "attractions" | IMFs are 10–100× weaker than covalent bonds and act *between* molecules. Different scale, different location. |
| Ignoring shape/surface area at equal mass | Only comparing formulas | Straight chains have more surface contact → stronger LDF than branched isomers ($n$-pentane vs neopentane). |

## AP Exam Tips

- **The signature FRQ prompt is "identify the strongest intermolecular force and justify."** Two things earn the points: (1) name the correct top force, and (2) tie it to a structural reason (polarity, an $\ce{O-H}$ group, electron count/surface area). "It's polar" alone is not justification; "it's polar, so it has dipole-dipole forces that the nonpolar one lacks" is.
- **Use the words intramolecular and intermolecular correctly** and never interchangeably. Graders watch for this. If a question asks what breaks when a substance boils, the answer is *intermolecular* forces — writing "bonds break" loses the point.
- **LDF is never absent.** Do not write that a nonpolar molecule "has no intermolecular forces." It always has London dispersion; that's often the whole answer.
- **Bigger / more electrons = stronger LDF.** When two molecules have the *same* strongest force, decide the tie by electron count/molar mass (and shape/surface area at equal mass).
- **The hydrogen-bonding gate is N, O, F only** — and the H must be bonded to it. Memorize "H on N, O, or F." This single rule shows up on nearly every IMF question.
- **When two molecules have similar mass**, the exam is almost always testing whether you'll notice the *extra* force (dipole-dipole or H-bonding). Similar mass = "look past LDF."
- **No calculator is needed for this topic** — it's all qualitative reasoning. Points come from correct vocabulary and cause-and-effect logic, so write in complete "because" sentences.
- **Higher boiling point = stronger IMFs.** These two phrasings are interchangeable on the exam; a question about boiling point, melting point, or volatility is really an IMF question in disguise.

### Problem 2

When dry ice ($\ce{CO2}$ solid) sublimes into gas, which forces are overcome? Is any $\ce{CO2}$ molecule destroyed?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Subliming separates $\ce{CO2}$ molecules from one another, overcoming the **intermolecular** London dispersion forces between them. The $\ce{C=O}$ bonds inside each molecule are untouched — each molecule is still $\ce{CO2}$.

**Answer: Only the intermolecular (London dispersion) forces are overcome. No molecule is destroyed — it's a physical change; the $\ce{C=O}$ bonds stay intact.**

</details>

### Problem 3

Which has the stronger London dispersion forces, $\ce{Cl2}$ or $\ce{Br2}$? Explain.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Both are nonpolar (LDF only). $\ce{Br2}$ has more electrons and a higher molar mass than $\ce{Cl2}$, so its electron cloud is larger and more polarizable → stronger LDF. (Consistent with $\ce{Br2}$ being a liquid while $\ce{Cl2}$ is a gas at room temperature.)

**Answer: $\ce{Br2}$ — more electrons/higher molar mass means a more polarizable cloud and stronger dispersion forces.**

</details>

### Problem 4

List all intermolecular forces present in liquid $\ce{HF}$ and name the strongest.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

$\ce{HF}$ has H bonded directly to F → hydrogen bonding. It's also polar → dipole-dipole. And like everything, it has LDF. All three are present.

**Answer: LDF, dipole-dipole, and hydrogen bonding; the strongest is hydrogen bonding.**

</details>

### Problem 5

$n$-butane and isobutane are both $\ce{C4H10}$. Which boils higher, and why?

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Same formula → same electron count, so LDF strength is decided by surface contact. The straight-chain $n$-butane lies flat against neighbors along its length; branched isobutane is more compact with less contact. More contact → stronger LDF → higher boiling point.

**Answer: $n$-butane boils higher — its greater surface area gives more contact and stronger London dispersion forces than compact isobutane.**

</details>

### Problem 6

Explain why $\ce{CH4}$ does not exhibit hydrogen bonding despite containing four hydrogen atoms.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Hydrogen bonding requires H bonded directly to N, O, or F. In $\ce{CH4}$ the H's are bonded to carbon, which is not electronegative enough to create the intense δ+ that hydrogen bonding needs. Merely containing H is irrelevant.

**Answer: Its H atoms are bonded to carbon, not to N/O/F, so no hydrogen bonding occurs — only London dispersion forces are present.**

</details>

### Problem 7

Rank from lowest to highest boiling point and justify: $\ce{Ar}$, $\ce{F2}$, $\ce{HCl}$, $\ce{HF}$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

- $\ce{Ar}$: LDF only, 18 electrons.
- $\ce{F2}$: LDF only, 18 electrons — similar to Ar; roughly comparable, slightly higher due to shape/contact.
- $\ce{HCl}$: polar → LDF + dipole-dipole.
- $\ce{HF}$: H on F → LDF + dipole-dipole + hydrogen bonding.

Order by strongest force: the two LDF-only species lowest, then the dipole-dipole species, then the hydrogen-bonder.

**Answer: $\ce{Ar} \approx \ce{F2} < \ce{HCl} < \ce{HF}$ — LDF-only species lowest, then polar $\ce{HCl}$, and $\ce{HF}$ highest because of hydrogen bonding.**

</details>

### Problem 8

Acetone $\ce{(CH3)2CO}$ (58 g/mol) boils at $56\,^\circ\ce{C}$; butane $\ce{C4H10}$ (58 g/mol) boils at $-1\,^\circ\ce{C}$. Explain the difference.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Equal molar mass → comparable LDF. Acetone has a polar $\ce{C=O}$ group (polar molecule → dipole-dipole on top of LDF); butane is nonpolar (LDF only). The extra dipole-dipole force in acetone requires more energy to overcome.

**Answer: With equal mass and similar LDF, acetone is polar and adds dipole-dipole forces that nonpolar butane lacks, so acetone boils higher.**

</details>

### Problem 9

Why does $\ce{NaCl}$ dissolve in water? Name the specific intermolecular force involved.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Water is polar. Its δ− oxygen ends attract the $\ce{Na+}$ ions and its δ+ hydrogen ends attract the $\ce{Cl-}$ ions — **ion-dipole** forces. These attractions are strong enough to pull ions from the crystal lattice and surround (hydrate) them: $\ce{NaCl(s) -> Na+(aq) + Cl-(aq)}$.

**Answer: Ion-dipole forces between the ions and polar water molecules pull the ions out of the lattice and hydrate them, dissolving the salt.**

</details>

### Problem 10

$\ce{H2O}$ boils at $100\,^\circ\ce{C}$ but $\ce{H2Te}$ (much heavier) boils at only about $-2\,^\circ\ce{C}$. Explain this apparent contradiction.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

By mass alone, heavier $\ce{H2Te}$ has stronger LDF and "should" boil higher. But $\ce{H2O}$ has H bonded to O → strong hydrogen bonding; $\ce{H2Te}$ has H on Te (not N/O/F) → no hydrogen bonding. Water's extensive H-bond network dominates over LDF, giving it the much higher boiling point.

**Answer: Water hydrogen-bonds and $\ce{H2Te}$ does not (Te isn't N/O/F). The strong H-bond network in water overrides $\ce{H2Te}$'s greater LDF, so water boils far higher.**

</details>

### Problem 11

Identify all IMFs in each and name the strongest: (a) $\ce{N2}$, (b) $\ce{H2S}$, (c) $\ce{NH3}$.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

- (a) $\ce{N2}$: nonpolar → **LDF only** (strongest: LDF).
- (b) $\ce{H2S}$: polar, H on S (not N/O/F) → LDF + dipole-dipole (strongest: **dipole-dipole**).
- (c) $\ce{NH3}$: H on N → LDF + dipole-dipole + hydrogen bonding (strongest: **hydrogen bonding**).

**Answer: (a) LDF only; (b) LDF + dipole-dipole; (c) LDF + dipole-dipole + hydrogen bonding — strongest forces LDF, dipole-dipole, and hydrogen bonding respectively.**

</details>

### Problem 12

Two molecules have the same strongest type of IMF (both are nonpolar with LDF only). Molecule A has 10 electrons; molecule B has 54 electrons. Predict which boils higher and why.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

When the strongest force is the same type, electron count breaks the tie. Molecule B has far more electrons → larger, more polarizable cloud → stronger LDF → higher boiling point.

**Answer: Molecule B boils higher — more electrons means a more polarizable cloud and stronger London dispersion forces.**

</details>

### Problem 13

Methanol $\ce{CH3OH}$ and ethane $\ce{C2H6}$ have similar molar masses (32 vs 30 g/mol) but methanol boils at $65\,^\circ\ce{C}$ while ethane boils at $-89\,^\circ\ce{C}$. Explain.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Similar mass → similar LDF. Methanol has an $\ce{O-H}$ group (H bonded to O) → hydrogen bonding. Ethane is nonpolar (LDF only). Methanol's hydrogen bonding is far stronger, requiring much more energy to boil.

**Answer: Methanol hydrogen-bonds (it has an $\ce{O-H}$ group) while ethane has only LDF, so despite similar mass methanol boils far higher.**

</details>

### Problem 14

A student says: "Freezing water is a chemical change because the water turns hard and solid — it must be forming new bonds." Correct the reasoning.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

Freezing arranges $\ce{H2O}$ molecules into a fixed lattice held by the *same* intermolecular hydrogen bonds — it does **not** form new covalent bonds. Ice is still $\ce{H2O}$; nothing chemically new is made, so it's a **physical** change (a phase change), driven entirely by intermolecular forces.

**Answer: Freezing is a physical change — no new bonds form; molecules just lock into place via intermolecular hydrogen bonds. Ice is still $\ce{H2O}$, so no chemical change occurred.**

</details>

### Problem 15

Explain, using the gecko analogy, how forces that are individually far weaker than covalent bonds can still hold a substance together as a liquid or solid.

<details>
<summary><strong>Solution</strong></summary>

A single gecko foot-hair makes one fleeting, weak dispersion attraction to the wall — negligible on its own. But millions of hairs acting together hold up the whole lizard. Likewise, one molecule-to-molecule IMF is far weaker than a covalent bond, but a macroscopic sample has on the order of $10^{21}$–$10^{23}$ molecules all attracting their neighbors at once. Summed over that many pairs, the collectively enormous attraction is enough to keep molecules condensed into a liquid or solid — even though no single IMF comes close to a covalent bond in strength.

**Answer: Individually each IMF is tiny, but a real sample contains a staggering number of molecules; trillions of weak attractions added together (like millions of gecko hairs) produce a force large enough to hold a liquid or solid together.**

</details>

## Quick Reference

**INTRA vs INTER — get this right first**

| | Location | Example | Broken by |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Intra**molecular | *Inside* a molecule (bonds) | $\ce{O-H}$ bond in $\ce{H2O}$ | Chemical reaction (100s of kJ/mol) |
| **Inter**molecular (IMF) | *Between* molecules | H-bond linking two $\ce{H2O}$ | Boiling/melting (a few kJ/mol) |

**The IMF hierarchy (weakest → strongest)**

| Force | When it applies | Key driver |
|---|---|---|
| London dispersion (LDF) | **Every** molecule | ↑ electrons / molar mass / surface area → ↑ strength |
| Dipole-dipole | Polar molecules | Permanent δ+/δ− ends |
| Hydrogen bonding | H bonded to **N, O, or F** | Intense δ+ H meets a lone pair |
| Ion-dipole | Ion + polar solvent | Full charge on a dipole (dissolving) |

**Decision flow for "strongest IMF"**

$$\text{Ion present?} \to \text{ion-dipole} \quad|\quad \text{H on N/O/F?} \to \text{H-bond} \quad|\quad \text{polar?} \to \text{dipole-dipole} \quad|\quad \text{else} \to \text{LDF}$$

**Golden rules**

- LDF is in *everything* — never say "no IMFs."
- Higher boiling point = stronger IMFs.
- Same strongest force? Break the tie with electron count / molar mass (then surface area).
- "Has hydrogen" ≠ "has hydrogen bonding" — the H must be bonded to N, O, or F.
- Boiling breaks IMFs; a chemical reaction breaks bonds.
